Non-equilibrium Solidification and Microsegregation Mechanism Based on Interface Evolution and Discrete Crystal Growth

Abstract

The interface layer and discrete crystal growth in solidification are traced by analyzing the microsegregation of aluminum alloy. Based on the interface evolution and discrete crystal growth, the non-equilibrium microsegregation mechanism is qualitatively proposed. The solute content of solid is decided by the solidification temperature of interface. And the solidification temperature of interface is further decided by the evolution of interface compositions and interface temperature. The fluctuation of interface compositions and actual interface temperature persists in the whole solidification process.

Manuscript submitted September 24, 2014.

The microstructures and mechanical properties of metal alloys are significantly influenced by the solidification and segregation. For the typical non-rapid solidification process, the equilibrium solute partition is assumed at the solid–liquid interface. Further, the Scheil model[1] is established based on two additional assumptions. One assumption is the absolutely uniform melt without considering interface layer, and the other assumption is neglecting the solute diffusion in the solid. According to the Scheil model, the solute content of solid at different solid fraction can be calculated without considering the crystal morphology. Thus, the Scheil model provides a highly simplified but valuable mathematical description for segregation simulation. Based on Scheil model, refinement was further performed by considering the phenomena of back diffusion and dendrite coarsening.[2–8]

In some non-rapid solidification conditions, because the assumption of absolutely uniform melt fails, the interface layer is crucial for the microsegregation. If diffusion is the only way for mass transfer in the melt, the solute distribution within the interface layer tends to become stable. The solute distribution within the steady interface layer can be described by the equation established by Tiller.[9] And the steady interface layer will produce the solidified solid with constant solute content. If both diffusion and convection are considered for mass transfer in the melt, both the solute distribution within the interface and the solute content outside the interface will change continuously in the growth process of crystal. In this situation, the Scheil model can be revised through replacing the solute distribution coefficient k0 by the effective partition coefficient ke, which was derived by Burton et al.[10]

It should be recognized that above models significantly contribute to our understanding about solidification and segregation. However, more investigation on the underlying mechanism should be encouraged. For example, all above models did not consider the morphology of growing crystal. In view of current grid dimension used in actual simulation, the solid in the same element may actually form under different local solid fractions at the same time because of the influence of crystal morphology. Thus, the crystal morphology will actually influence the microsegregation. In order to investigate the microsegregation incorporating crystal morphology, the phase field modeling is a promising method.[11,12] Besides, all above models assume the equilibrium solute partition at the solid–liquid interface. However, for the rapid solidification process, the equilibrium solute partition at the solid–liquid interface cannot be satisfied. Jackson and Gilmer have performed the Monte Carlo simulations of the Ising model to reproduce the solute trapping during rapid solidification.[13,14] And Aziz has developed the continuous growth models to describe the solute partition at the surface of a rapidly growing crystal.[15–17] These detailed investigations on the mechanism further extend the understanding about microsegregation.

In our previous research about steel,[18] we proposed that the enriched interface layer exists in the growth process of crystal. And we reported a new mechanism of discrete crystal growth. The enriched interface layer can be periodically trapped in the discrete crystal growth of dendrite. The solidification and microsegregation mechanism becomes more complicated based on the phenomenon of ‘interface trapping’. Thus, although the highly simplified Scheil model is still valuable in practice, more underlying phenomena about discrete crystal growth and interface trapping should be further investigated. And new microsegregation mechanism based on interface trapping and discrete crystal growth should be proposed. In this paper, the microsegregation of Al-5 wt pctCu alloy by non-rapid solidification is measured. Through analyzing the experimental data, the interface layer and discrete crystal growth are traced and confirmed. And the non-equilibrium microsegregation mechanism based on interface evolution and discrete crystal growth is proposed.

The Al-5 wt pctCu alloy was melted and held for 20 minutes at 1223 K (950 °C) in a resistance furnace. Subsequently, the melt was poured into a graphite crucible (Φ60 × 60 mm) which was preheated at 933 K (660 °C) In the room air, the ingot was solidified in the crucible and was air cooled to room temperature. The ingot was sectioned, grinded, polished, and etched with an HNO3 (5 mL)-HCl (3 mL)-HF (2 mL)-H2O (190 mL) solution. The solidified microstructures at the position of 30 mm away from the bottom and 20 mm away from the ingot surface were observed. And the solute distribution in the dendrite was qualitatively characterized by scanning electron microscope (SEM) and energy-dispersive spectrometer (EDS) in the etched sample. And the solute distribution in the dendrite was quantitatively characterized by electron probe micro-analyzer (EPMA) in the un-etched sample.

The solidified microstructures of Al-5 wt pctCu alloy consist of the α-Al dendrite phase and the interdendritic Al2Cu phase, as shown in Figures 1(a) and (b). The EDS result for the interdendritic Al2Cu phase is shown in Figure 1(c). The Cu distribution along the dendritic stem was tested by EDS along two lines D and E, as shown in Figure 1(a). The results are shown in Figures 1(d) and (e). The solute distribution along the dendritic stems is rather uniform without confirmable segregation.
Further, the Cu distribution in the coarsening direction of the dendritic arm was scanned by EDS along the lines F and G, as shown in Figure 1(b). The results are shown in Figures 1(f) and (g). Segregation of Cu can be obviously confirmed in the interdendritic positions, as shown in Figure 1(f). The Cu content increases slightly from the center of the dendritic arm to the outer of the dendritic arm, as shown in Figure 1(g). The Cu distribution in the coarsening direction of the dendritic stem was scanned by EDS along the line H, as shown in Figure 1(a). The Cu content also increases slightly from the center of the dendritic stem to the outer of the dendritic stem, as shown in Figure 1(h). As the existence of microsegregation has been identified in the coarsening direction of dendritic arm, quantitative analysis was further performed by the EPMA in several dendritic arms, as shown in Figures 2(a) and (b). The results are shown in Figures 2(c) and (d). The Cu content increases from about 1.4 wt pct in the center of dendritic arm to about 4.0 wt pct in the outer of the dendritic arm. The Cu content decreases slightly from about 4.0 to about 3.5 pct near the interdendritic Al2Cu phase, and then increases abruptly to about 45 wt pct in the position of interdendritic Al2Cu phase. The range of 1.4 to 4.0 wt pct is further amplified in Figure 2(e).

In order to establish the relation between the solid fraction and the solute content for the dendrite, the EPMA data normalization[19] is widely accepted as a reliable experimental method. In this method, it is assumed that the solid with the same solute content is produced with the same solid fraction in the whole test area. It is reasonable for the solidification process dominated exactly by the Scheil mechanism. In the solidification process dominated by other mechanisms, because the solid with the same solute content does not necessarily form with the same solid fraction, this method will produce a meaningless plot. With the method of EPMA data normalization, the selected two-dimensional test area is taken as a research target. However, as the solidification actually happens in a three-dimensional space, the increase of solid fraction in the two-dimensional test area is different from the real increase of solid fraction in the three-dimensional space. Besides, it is also a puzzle to ascertain the appropriate test area in the two-dimensional plane. The increase of solid fraction is variable depending on the different selected test areas, especially for the situation that the test area cannot cover several dendrites. Thus, some misinterpretations exist in traditional processing method about experimental data. In order to avoid such logical flaws, a different characterization method is adopted in this paper by measuring and analyzing the solute distribution along the dendritic stem and in the coarsening direction of dendritic arm, respectively.

First, the dendrite stem together with the dendrite arm are taken as the whole research target. By assuming the cylindrical coarsening for dendritic arms, the solid fraction in the dendritic arm coarsening process can be calculated by the Eq. [1].
$$ f_{\text{s}} = f_{\text{s}}^{\text{initial}} + \, \left( {1 - f_{\text{s}}^{\text{initial}} } \right)\frac{{\pi r^{{{\prime }2}} }}{{\pi r^{2} }}, $$
(1)
in which, r′ is the distance away from the centerline of dendritic arm; r is the distance from the border to the centerline of dendritic arm; and fsinitial is the initial solid fraction at the beginning of the dendritic arm coarsening process. Thus, the plot of distance vs Cu content in Figure 2(d) can be converted to the plot of solid fraction vs Cu content, as shown in Figure 3(a). Because the tested dendritic arm is selected randomly, the initial solid fraction fsinitial at the beginning of the dendritic arm coarsening cannot be confirmed. Thus, we tried the fsinitial from 0, 0.4, 0.73 to 0.9, as shown in Figure 3(a). It seems that part of the data coincides well with the Scheil model if the fsinitial is set as 0.73. Based on the hypothesis that fsinitial is 0.73, the average solute content in the zone of dendritic stem must be lower than 1.4 wt pct. Accordingly, the average solute content in the zone of dendritic arm must be higher than 14.7 wt pct. However, the solute content in zone of dendritic arm is experimentally accumulated by assuming the lowest, highest, and average solute content in every step of solid fraction. The range of the average Cu content for the local dendritic arm should be between 3.19 and 5.02 wt pct. This result means that although the dendritic arm forms later with higher solid fraction, the solute content of the melt solidified as dendritic arm is similar with the nominal solute content. Thus, it may be fundamentally wrong to correlate the growth of dendrite stem with the growth of dendrite arm. Actually, the dendrite arm solidifies independently.
Further, we should consider whether Scheil model can dominate the coarsening process in the independent area of dendritic arm. Thus, we evaluated the Scheil model in the local domain of dendritic arm. The local solid fraction fslocal can be calculated by the Eq. [2]
$$ f_{\text{s}}^{\text{local}} = \frac{{\pi r^{{{\prime }2}} }}{{\pi r^{2} }}, $$
(2)
in which, r′ is the distance away from the centerline of dendritic arm; r is the distance from the border to the centerline of dendritic arm; and fslocal is the solid fraction at the local zone of dendritic arm. As shown in Figure 3(b), if the average Cu content of the local domain is 16.3 wt pct, the Scheil model seems to fit the experimental data well. However, two contradictions still exist. First, according to the result of Scheil model for Al-16.3 wt pctCu alloy, the Cu content of solid should increase to 5.78 wt pct as the solid fraction increases to 0.75, which coincides with the formation of eutectic. But, experimental data show that the Cu content of 5.78 wt pct is not reached yet with the solid fraction of 0.9. Second, the solute content is experimentally accumulated by assuming the lowest, highest, and average solute content in every step of solid fraction. The range of the average Cu content for the local domain should be between 3.19 and 5.02 wt pct. This accumulating result fundamentally disapproves the Scheil model fitted by Al-16.3 wt pctCu. Based on the actual Cu content by experimental accumulation, in the local coarsening process of dendritic arm, the Scheil model considering back diffusion and dendrite coarsening also significantly deviates from the experimental microsegregation data.

The mismatch between the microsegregation profile and Scheil model is usually attributed to back diffusion, coarsening of dendritic arms, and dissolution of non-equilibrium eutectic phase. However, the dissolution of eutectic phase after solidification seems impossible in our sample. According to the phase diagram, as the temperature decreases after solidification, the α-Al near the Al2Cu phase will discharge Cu to the Al2Cu phase. At the same time, the homogenization will lower the Cu content in α-Al near the Al2Cu phase. Therefore, if homogenization can maintain a positive concentration gradient near the Al2Cu phase, the dissolution of Al2Cu phase is possible. However, the negative concentration gradient in our sample (range 4.0 to 3.5 wt pct) means that the dissolution of Al2Cu phase is impossible. The back diffusion and dendrite coarsening indeed may contribute to reducing the mismatch between the microsegregation profile and Scheil model. Especially, because of coarsening process, the diffusion length in the solid during solidification is variable, which is smaller than the half dendrite arm spacing at the end of solidification. However, it should be addressed that our results about Scheil model of Al-5.02 wt pctCu alloy have considered the back diffusion and dendrite coarsening. In the calculation considering back diffusion, we adopted the model described by Brody.[1] In this model, the half dendrite arm spacing at the end of solidification is adopted as the diffusion length. In calculation, half of dendrite arm spacing is 36 μm, the characteristic solidification time is 330 seconds, and the temperature is set as 873 K (600 °C). In the calculation considering dendrite coarsening, we adopt the model described by Basaran.[6] In calculation, the diffusion length in the solid during solidification is variable, the length of the volume element at the end of solidification is 36 μm, the characteristic solidification time is 330 seconds, the n parameter for coarsening is 3, and the temperature is set as 873 K (600 °C). For simplification, in order to evaluate the solid fraction at any moment, we assume that the velocity of solidification interface is constant. It should be noted that our calculation results about Scheil model considering back diffusion and dendrite coarsening are very close to the reported simulation results in the literatures.[2,3] In the literatures, the experimental data coincide well with the Scheil model considering the back diffusion in solid.[2,3] However, as shown in Figure 3(b), the experimental data in current paper cannot be fitted by the Scheil model considering back diffusion. Actually, the experimental data in current paper are significantly different from the experiment data in the literature.[2,3] This difference may be attributed to the different solidification conditions. In the literature, in order to measure the compositions perpendicular to the direction of dendrite growth, the directional solidified samples were applied, while in the current paper, the equiaxed solidified sample is applied. The temperature gradient and solidification rate are fundamentally different in directional solidification and in equiaxed solidification. As a supporting evidence existed in previous literature,[2,3,19] the experimental data for a sample without directional solidification are significantly different from the data for the sample with directional solidification, although the same EPMA data normalization was adopted.

The deviation of Scheil model (considering back diffusion and coarsening) from the experimental data implicates that underlying mechanism may exist. In some publication about solute trapping, such as the models proposed by Aziz,[15–17] the interface layer exists in the growth of crystal, and the departure from equilibrium at the interface also exists. Although our current work focuses on non-rapid solidification, the periodic non-equilibrium interface is also suggested. Once the departure from equilibrium happens at the interface, liquidus temperature can be lower than the solidus temperature at the interface.[18] Part of the interface layer will acquire the undercooling and will solidify earlier than the interface, namely the discrete crystal growth and enriched interface trapping will occur. The mechanisms for discrete crystal growth and periodic enriched boundary trapping are sketched in Figures 4(a) and (b). The reasoning for the discrete crystal growth and periodic enriched boundary trapping is as follows. It is assumed that the periodic boundary trapping does not occur in the coarsening process of dendritic arm. The initial Cu content in the solid is about 1.4 wt pct, and the solidification temperature should be about 898 K (625 °C) according to the phase diagram, while the actual Cu content in the melt is about 5 wt pct, and the liquidus temperature for Al-5 wt pctCu melt is about 920 K (647 °C). The undercooling reaches a maximum of about 22K (22 °C). New discrete solid may be formed in the undercooled melt in front of the interface. In fact, before the undercooling reaches 22 K (22 °C), discrete crystal growth and enriched boundary trapping might have happened. Besides, based on this mechanism, we can reasonably explain the difference of microsegregation profiles between the directional solidification and equiaxed solidification, as shown in Figure 4(b). In equiaxed solidification, because the latent heat must be transported by the surrounding melt, the temperature gradient in the interface is negative, which is favorable for the interface trapping. In the directional solidification, because the latent heat is transported by the solid, the temperature gradient in the interface is positive. Once the temperature gradient is larger than the critical temperature gradient, the interface trapping cannot occur.
By analyzing the experimental data, the enriched interface layer and discrete crystal growth are traced and proposed. However, more robust and direct evidence is still expected. Because the trapped enriched interface will finally be solidified as the same phase with the former solidified crystal, such enriched interface and discrete crystal growth are usually difficult to be observed. However, as long as diffusion cannot completely eliminate the solute enrichment in the trapped interface, some trace information can be identified. In Figure 2(e), as the Cu content increases from 1.4 to 4.0 wt pct, several small solute fluctuations appear. These solute fluctuations may be the residual trace of trapped enriched interface after solid diffusion process. In order to further support the phenomenon of discrete crystal growth, we tried to search some direct evidences in the Al-20 wt pctCu alloy, in which some enriched boundary may finally appear as the Al2Cu phase. The Al-20 wt pctCu alloy was melted at 1223 K (950 °C) and held at 933 K (660 °C) in a resistance furnace. The melt was poured into a graphite crucible (Φ60 × 60 mm). In the room air, the ingot was solidified in the crucible. Then, the ingot was water cooled after solidification. In this alloy, the trapped interface and discrete crystal growth are confirmed at some positions. As shown in Figure 5, a trapped interface characterized as Al2Cu phase appears in the interior of a dendritic arm.

Based on the interface layer and discrete crystal growth, the following non-equilibrium solidification and microsegregation mechanism is qualitatively suggested.

First, the microsegregation should be considered based on the evolution of local interface. The interface in the growth process of dendritic stem is independent of the interface in the coarsening process of dendritic arm. The microsegregation in the dendritic stem should not be correlated with the coarsening process of dendritic arm. Thus, if we want to establish the relation between the solid fraction and the solute content in the solid, the element in simulation should not exceed the scale of dendritic arm. As microsegregation should be considered based on the local interface, the solute content of the solid at the interface is only decided by the actual solidification temperature of interface. For example, the solidification process can be divided into the negative segregation period and positive segregation period according to the solidus temperature of 830 K (557 °C) of Al-5 wt pctCu alloy. Positive segregation can occur below this temperature.

Second, the actual solidification temperature of interface is decided by the evolution of interface compositions and interface temperature. The interface composition is decided by the growth velocity of interface and the diffusion velocity of solute from the interface. The interface temperature is decided by the heat transport and the release of latent heat. As the interface moves forward, the interface composition will increase. The corresponding liquidus temperature of interface will decrease, and the actual interface temperature will also decrease. In the well-accepted solidification theory, the local equilibrium interface is usually assumed that the actual interface temperature is always equal to the liquidus temperature of interface composition. Unfortunately, this assumption about local equilibrium interface may be not accurate. Actually, if the actual interface temperature is higher than the liquidus temperature of interface, solid will cease growth and wait for further decrease of actual temperature. If the actual interface temperature is lower than the liquidus temperature of interface, the interface is undercooled. Thus, the solidification velocity and latent heat release will increase. Further, the composition of interface will increase, the liquidus temperature of interface will decrease, but the actual interface temperature will decrease slower or even increase. Finally, such fluctuation of interface compositions and interface temperature will persist in the whole solidification process, as sketched in Figure 4(c). According to the above fluctuation analysis, the interface always actually solidifies with fluctuant undercooling. However, it is not clear whether equilibrium solute partition or solute trapping can occur at the interface with actual fluctuant undercooling. If the solute trapping occurs, Aziz’s model[16] may be used to ascertain the solute content of solid at the moving interface.

Third, the discrete crystal growth will abruptly and strongly change the interface composition, which will further induce the abrupt and large undercooling. Subsequently, the solidification velocity and the release of latent heat will increase abruptly and strongly, namely the discrete crystal growth intensifies the fluctuation of interface compositions and actual interface temperature. Interestingly, in previous publications, some models based on the fluctuation concept have discussed the interface stability and pattern formation of crystal.[20,21]

The above given non-equilibrium solidification and microsegregation mechanism is just descriptive and qualitative, expounding which factors and how the factors determine the microsegregation in the solidification. In order to further establish the mathematical or computational model about non-equilibrium solidification and microsegregation mechanism, phase field modeling may be a powerful method. As long as the related physical models and physical parameters about the diffusion, convection, heat transfer, latent heat, temperature gradient, solute partition coefficient, and solidification rate are all reliable, all the microscopic solidification phenomenon and data may be reproduced in the phase field simulation. However, huge efforts are still needed to assure the accurate physical models and physical parameters. Besides, compared to the convenient analytical model, the phase field simulation needs massive computations to reproduce the experimental measurements, especially in macrosegregation simulation. Thus, the analytical model about the non-equilibrium microsegregation mechanism is still expected in the future. Some research has made such attempt.[8]

In summary, the microsegregation in the Al-5 wt pctCu-equiaxed dendrite was investigated. The non-equilibrium solidification and microsegregation mechanism based on interface evolution and discrete crystal growth is proposed. The evolution and fluctuation of interface compositions and actual interface temperature fundamentally decide the microsegregation distribution. The findings of current study reveal the underlying phenomena in the formation of dendrite and have important implications for refining the segregation model in the future.
