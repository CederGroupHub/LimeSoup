Poly(allylamine hydrochloride) modified screen-printed carbon electrode for sensitive and selective detection of NADH

Highlights

•

A new NADH sensor based on poly(allylamine hydrochloride) modified SPE is described.

•

A wide response range and low detection limit for NADH detection was achieved.

•

DPV allowed selective determination of NADH in the presence of ascorbic acid.

Abstract

A screen-printed electrode (SPE) modified with poly(allylamine hydrochloride) (PAH) for electrochemical detection of NADH is described. FT-IR spectrum confirms the presence of a positively charged PAH SPE that was exploited to accumulate the NADH on the electrode surface for a faster electrochemical detection of this analyte. A wide response range was achieved in amperometry, at an applied potential of +0.6 V, from 0.01 to 5 mM, with a detection limit of 0.22 μM. Differential pulse voltammetry allowed selective detection of NADH in presence of ascorbic acid. This approach provides a simple and effective way to detect low amounts of NADH with potential application in dehydrogenase-based biosensors.

1. Introduction

In the last two decades the polymers have gained increasing importance in the development of electrochemical sensors and biosensors due to the fact that they are flexible materials that can be tailored for different needs [1]. The literature reveals that polymers are mainly used as a coating material or as an immobilization matrix for some bio-molecules. Among the different type of polymers, the non-conducting one allow the immobilization of different biological components by encapsulation, cross-linking, or inclusion in their supramolecular structure [2]. On the other hand the conducting polymers and the polyelectrolytes were extensively used, alone or in combination with other materials such as carbon nanotubes, nanofibers, graphene or metal nanoparticles in order to improve the performances of the electrochemical transducers [3]. Electropolymerization is a very simple and attractive approach for the controlled immobilization of enzymes on electrode surfaces [1] and [4]. Polyaniline, polypyrrole and polythiophene are the most known conducting polymers used for immobilization of horseradish peroxidase or different oxidases for detection of hydrogen peroxide, glucose, cholesterol, lactate, glutamate [5], [6], [7], [8], [9] and [10]. The binding forces involved are electrostatic interactions, hydrogen bonds, Van der Waals forces, etc. Such an approach leaded to an increase of five times the current response for immobilization of GOD in polypyrrole [11]. The main drawback of this method is the high amount of enzyme required for the immobilization process and the impossibility to calculate the exact amount of immobilized enzymes on the electrode surface [12]. The polyelectrolytes are polymers obtained from monomers that have attached on the molecule polar groups. The polymers chain contains charged groups of the same sign. The polyelectrolytes can be strong (constant charge) or weak (with pH-dependent charge) depending on the pKa of charged groups. They are suitable for developing films that in aqueous media could have positive or negative charges, layer-by-layer (LBL) materials, with potential application in sensing. Positively charges polyelectrolytes such as, poly(diallyldimethylammonium chloride) (PDDA), poly(allylamine hydrochloride) (PAH) or negatively charged polystyrene sulfonate (PSS), poly acrylic acid (PAA) are the common used for sensors and biosensor development. One of the advantages of polyelectrolyte films is the elimination of the interferences that could reach the electrode surface if the interfering species have opposite charge to that of the analyte. Nafion polyelectrolyte membrane used for immobilization of biomolecules by simple casting on the working electrodes, leads toward improved sensors in term of selectivity because it is negatively charged and has ion-exchange properties. However, these sensors present a slow response and a low sensitivity, due the low diffusion of the analytes through the film [13].

The polyelectrolyte layer ensures a high active area and the electrostatic interaction between analyte and polymer could facilitate its accumulation at the electrode surface, resulting in the enhancement of analytical signal [14].

Different composite materials based on polymers and other conductive materials (carbon nanotubes, metal nanoparticles) were also used to increase the electroactive surface of the electrode for a fast electron transfer to the electrode surface [3], [15], [16], [17] and [18]. Mediators could also be entrapped into the polymer structure facilitating the electron transfer between the analyte and electrode surface [19].

There are not so many reports about systems based on dehydrogenases that use polyelectrolytes [20], [21] and [22]. Nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide, reduced form (NADH) and its oxidized form (NAD+) play an important role in the living organism for functioning of more than 300 NAD-dependent enzymes. Usually the compound that is electrochemically detected by dehydrogenase based electrochemical biosensors is the NADH. However, the direct oxidation of NADH at bare classical electrodes occurs at a high overpotential and is affected by several electrochemical interferences. Moreover, a low sensitivity of these sensors was achieved and fouling of the electrode surface should be overcome [23]. Various redox mediators [24] and [25], conducting polymers [12], carbon nanotubes, graphene [26] nanomaterials or nano-composite materials [18], ionic-liquid based materials [27], [28] and [29] were used to modify the electrode surface in order to increase the rate of the electron transfer. Despite the fact the chemical modification of the electrode leads to a decrease of the applied potential and an increase of the sensitivity, the sensors exhibited a limited stability because of the mediator leakage [25], a narrow linear range and do not eliminate completely the interferences. Also, the analytical applications of such NADH sensors are limited.

In the present work, to the best of our knowledge, for the first time is reported a simple and effective way to detect NADH using poly(allylamine hydrochloride) (PAH) modify carbon screen-printed electrodes (SPE). The electrostatic interaction between positively charged polyelectrolyte and NADH facilitates the accumulation of the analyte at the surface of the modified electrode and lead to an enhancement of the oxidation current for the NADH.

2. Experimental

2.1. Reagents

Nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide reduced disodium salt (NADH), poly(allylamine hydrochloride) (PAH, MW = 15,000), potassium ferricyanide (K3[Fe(CN)6], potassium ferrocyanide (K4[Fe(CN)6], hexaammine-ruthenium (II) chloride ([Ru(NH3)6]Cl2), hexaammine-ruthenium (III) chloride ([Ru(NH3)6]Cl3) were purchased from Sigma–Aldrich and used as received without further purification. All other chemicals used were of analytical grade. Phosphate buffer solution (PBS, 0.1 M, containing 0.1 KCl, pH 6.0) was prepared by mixing solution of Na2HPO4, NaH2PO4 and KCl. All solutions were prepared in ultrapure water (Millipore, 18 MΩ cm).

2.2. Equipments and materials

Cyclic voltammetry (CV), differential pulse voltammetry (DPV) and amperometric measurements were performed using a computer-controlled Autolab PGSTAT 101 and electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) experiments with a Autolab PGSTAT 12 with FRA2 module (Eco Chemie, Utrecht, Netherlands). IR spectra were recorded on Nicolet 4700 FT-IR spectrophotometer (Thermo Scientific). Screen-printed electrodes from Dropsens (DRP-C110) with carbon working electrode, Ag pseudo-reference electrode and carbon counter electrode were used for the experiments. All potentials are reported vs. pseudo-reference Ag electrode. All electrochemical experiments were performed at room temperature.

2.3. Preparation of the PAH modified electrodes

Prior to polymer modification, the screen-printed electrodes (SPE) were electrochemically pre-treated by cycling the potential between −0.5 and 0.5 V in PBS (0.1 M, pH = 6) until a reproducible shape of the voltammogram was obtained. A negatively charged electrode surface was achieved by ending the cycling at a negative potential, thus favoring the interaction with PAH, a positively charged polyelectrolyte. After pre-treatment, 5 μL of the PAH solution (0.5 mg/mL) was deposited on the surface of working electrode and then was left to dry, in a desiccator, at the room temperature, for 24 h.

3. Results and discussion

3.1. Characterization of PAH layer

The SPE modified with PAH 0.5 mg/mL was characterized by IR spectroscopy. The FT-IR spectrum presented in Fig. 1 shows characteristic peaks at 990.7, 1454.3, 1644.5 cm−1 and 3060.1 cm−1. The peaks were associated with the following vibrational modes: R-CHCH2, symmetric deformation vibration of CH2N+H3, NH of a primary amine bending vibration and asymmetric NH stretching vibration of N+H3 group.

The FT-IR spectrum indicated the presence of quaternary nitrogen on the polymer structure, which charges positively the modified electrode surface. This is essential for the electrochemical detection of NADH. The electrostatic interaction between negatively charged NADH molecules and the positively charged surface of the electrode can lead to an accumulation of the NADH near the electrode. Consequently, an enhancement of the electrochemical response of the sensor can be achieved.

3.2. Cyclic voltammetry studies

The bare SPE and PAH modified electrodes (PAH/SPE) were tested in the presence or in the absence of NADH 5 mM solution by cyclic voltammetry. No oxidation peak was observed for both types of electrode in the absence of NADH. The CV response of bare SPE and PAH/SPE electrodes shows the oxidation peak of NADH +550 mV (Fig. 2A). The increase of the oxidation current from 4 μA for bare electrode to 56 μA in the presence of the polyelectrolyte layer suggests a faster oxidation process at the electrode surface for the last one. This phenomenon could be attributed to the electrostatic attraction between the polymer layer, which is positively charged and NADH molecules, negatively charged. This leads to an accumulation of the analyte on the electrode surface and to an enhancement of the oxidation current of the NADH.

(B) Influence of the scan rate on the peak current for NADH 5 mM. Inset: CVs recorded for SR = 1/200 mV/s.

The cyclic voltammograms recorded in NADH 5 mM at different scan rates in the range of 1–200 mV/s revealed the fact that the current intensity of the oxidation peak of NADH increase with the increase of the scan rate (Fig. 2B). A linear relationship could be established between the peak current (ip) and the square root of the scan rate (v1/2). Nicholson–Shain criteria state that this is an indication that the electrochemical process is controlled by the diffusion of the NADH [30]. Despite the fact that the Randles–Sevcik plot is linear the non-zero intercept of the equation suggests that the process is not fully diffusion-controlled [31]. The intercept is more or less of the same order of magnitude with the smallest Faradaic current recorded and the diffusion controlled oxidation process could not be considered dominating. This may be due to the presence of the electrostatic interaction between PAH layer and the analyte that could play an important role and affect the electrochemical process. A shift of the peak potential to more positive values was also observed.

3.2.1. Effect of thickness of the PAH film on the sensor response

The sensor response to NADH is affected by the thickness of the polyelectrolyte film. In order to study this effect the same volume of PAH solution of different concentration was deposited on the surface of working electrode and left to dry according to the procedure described in Section 2.2. The SPE sensors were tested by cyclic voltammetry and the peak currents for NADH 5 mM were compared. The highest current was recorded for a PAH concentration of 0.5 mg/mL. At lower concentrations of PAH a very low response and unstable polyelectrolyte films were obtained. For higher concentrations of PAH the signal was lower due to the increase of the polymer film thickness that make the diffusion inside the film to be limitative for the sensor response. The optimal concentration of PAH was considered to be 0.5 mg/mL and it was used for all further experiments. The thickness of the polyelectrolyte film is important for further development of dehydrogenase-based biosensors where the diffusion of the coenzyme through the film has an impact on the response time as well as on the sensitivity of the detection.

3.2.2. Effect of pH on the sensor response

The pH plays an important role in the detection of NADH with PAH modified SPE sensor. The variation of the pH value will modify the electrical charges of both polyelectrolyte film and analyte and thus will affect the electrostatic interaction between the polyelectrolyte and NADH. Therefore, the PAH deposition was realized from solution prepared in distilled water. No buffer solution was used for preparation of PAH solution because the presence of salts could have a negative impact to the adherence of the film to the electrode surface. In order to study the pH effect on the NADH sensor response, solutions of 5 mM NADH with different pH were tested by CV. The results reveal a maximum oxidation current recorded for pH = 6 and a minimum current for pH = 8 (data not shown). The minimum value of the current could be explained by taking into account the pKa of PAH, which is reported in the literature to be between 8.0 and 8.7 [32]. At this pH the PAH layer is not electrically charged anymore. The accumulation of the NADH at the electrode surface cannot occur because there are no electrostatic interactions between PAH and NADH.

3.3. Electrochemical impedance spectroscopy studies

To investigate more the interaction of PAH layer with charged chemical species electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) studies were performed. Two redox couples, one negatively charged Fe(CN)63−/Fe(CN)64− and another one positively charged [Ru(NH3)6]2+/[Ru(NH3)6]3+ were considered. Fig. 3 shows the Nyquist plots for bare and PAH modified SPE electrodes. The Fe(CN)63−/Fe(CN)64− plot for PAH/SPE (Fig. 3A) presents a semicircular portion followed at lower frequencies by a linear part, characteristic to a diffusion of redox species. The Randles model circuit based on the solution resistance (Rs), the charge transfer resistance (Rct), the constant phase element (CPE) and the Warburg impedance (ZW) was chosen to fit the impedance data and it is presented as inset in Fig. 3A. A fast electron transfer was observed for PAH/SPE with a value of the resistance of the electron transfer of 19.2 ± 3.2 Ω while the bare SPE showed a much slower electron transfer (Rct = 705 ± 97 Ω). Lower resistance for PAH modified electrode could be attributed to the electrostatic interaction between PAH and Fe(CN)63−/Fe(CN)64−. The presence of electrostatic interaction between the electrode surface and redox couple leads further to an electrochemical reaction and affect the double layer capacitance. It is more appropriate to consider a CPE in the equivalent circuit than a capacitor.

(B) 5 mM Ru(NH3)62−/Ru(NH3)63− for bare SPE (a) and PAH/SPE (b). (100 kHz–0.01 Hz, perturbation signal 5 mV, OCP, 6 points per frequency decade).

On the other hand, the [Ru(NH3)6]2+/[Ru(NH3)6]3+ couple has a different behavior (Fig. 3B). Nyquist diagram does not exhibit a semicircular part at higher frequencies. Only a linear part can be distinguished that suggest a diffusion process of the redox species. At lower frequencies the Warburg impedance increases in the case of PAH/SPE compared to unmodified SPE electrode because the redox species have to diffuse farther and the PAH layer acts as barrier for the positively charged species. That could be explained by an electrostatic repel between the electrode surface and the mediator molecules both positively charged. EIS results suggest that faster electron transfer could be achieved for negatively charged species. The polyelectrolyte film can be successfully used for electrochemical application for ionizable analytes with negative charge.

3.4. Chronoamperometric measurements

Amperometric determination requires working at an applied potential that allow a sensitive detection of the analyte. The optimization of the working potential for PAH/SPE sensor was performed by recording the response curves for NADH 1 mM at different working potential in the range of +300 to +800 mV. A very low response was observed for a potential of +300 mV. The current increases at +400 mV and reach a maximum at +600 mV. This value was used for all amperometric measurements.

The catalytic rate constant (kcat) and diffusion coefficient (D) of NADH for PAH/SPE were estimated by chronoamperometry. Chronoamperometric measurements were performed in phosphate buffer and solutions containing different NADH concentrations (1, 2 and 5 mM) at an applied potential of +600 mV vs. Ag/AgCl ( Fig. 4A).

The catalytic rate constant kcat, was calculated using the formula

equation (1 )
i NADH i PBS = π 1 / 2 ( K cat ⋅ C ⋅ t ) 1 / 2
where i NADH and i PBS are the currents for NADH solution and for PBS buffer respectively, C is the NADH concentration and t is the time in second.

The catalytic rate constant was calculated from the slope of the plot icat/ibuffervs. t1/2 for a NADH concentration of 5 mM (inset Fig. 4A). A value of 9.38 × 104 M−1 s−1 was calculated for the PAH modified SPE, which reveals the fact that PAH film is suitable for developing NADH sensors. This value is almost 10 times higher than catalytic constant reported in the literature by other groups. For instance, using SPE electrode modified with SWCNT-COOH and Variamine blue and chronoamperometric data a value of 1.5 × 104 M−1 s−1 was calculated [33] and [34]. By using hydrodynamic voltammetry on poly(phenosafranin)-modified carbon electrodes a maximum value of the catalytic constant of 1.6 × 104 M−1 s−1 was reported [32].

A slightly higher catalytic constant of 3.36 × 105 M−1 s−1 was reported for a modified electrode with composite material based on poly(phenosafranin) functionalized single-walled carbon nanotubes [35].

The NADH diffusion coefficient (D) was estimated based on Cottrel equation by chronoamperometric measurements [36]:

equation (2 )
i = n F A D 1 / 2 C π 1 / 2 t 1 / 2
The linear parts of the plots of i vs. t −1/2 were selected ( Fig. 4B) and used to calculate the slope for each NADH concentration. The plot ( i· t 1/2) vs. C NADH allowed us to determine the diffusion coefficient of 1.15 × 10 −7 cm 2 s −1.

3.5. Calibration of the NADH sensor

The calibration graph was performed, for NADH concentration range of 0.01–5 mM (Fig. 5A) at an applied potential of +600 mV. A fast response of the sensor was observed. The current reaches 95% of steady state value within 10 s for the entire range of concentrations we have tested. A first linear range was recorded from 0.01 to 0.2 mM with a sensitivity of 15.81 ± 0.37 μA mM−1 (125.9 ± 2.9 μA mM−1 cm2). For the range of 0.2–5 mM was calculated a sensitivity of 10.71 ± 0.85 μA mM−1, which corresponds to a specific sensitivity of 85.3 ± 6.7 μA mM−1 cm2. A detection limit (based on signal to noise ratio of 3) of 0.22 μM was calculated.

The inset of Fig. 5A reveals a very stable amperometric response for NADH during calibration procedure. Amperometric response was stable for more than 10 min. This is a proof that no passivation of the electrode surface occurred. This phenomenon could be attributed to the presence of PAH, which does not favor the accumulation of the oxidation product, NAD+, on the electrode surface. The good stability of the sensor response represents an attractive feature comparing with other NADH sensors. By comparison, the NADH sensor presented in this paper exhibits one of the largest response ranges with a low detection limit. A significant lower detection limit and a higher sensitivity were reported for sensors based on SWCNT functionalized with poly styrensulfonic acid [35].

A wider linear range could be achieved by using differential pulse voltammetry (DPV). This technique also allows a fast and selective detection of NADH. Optimal results were obtained in conditions mentioned in Fig. 5B. The calibration graph was plotted using current intensity of the NADH peak vs. NADH concentration and the sensitivity was 13.33 ± 0.58 μA/mM. The specific sensitivity was calculated to be 106.1 μA/mM cm2. A linear range of two decades of concentration (0.05–5 mM) is very useful for developing of further application based on dehydrogenases. Stability of the PAH/SPE sensor was tested on NADH 5 mM. After each measurement sensor was washed twice with buffer. A standard deviation of 5.2% was obtained after 20 consecutive measurements.

Ascorbic acid (AA) is the common interfering compound in the electrochemical detection of NADH from biological samples. The AA interference in amperometric detection could not be eliminated because both target and interfering compounds are negatively charged. The selective determination of this coenzyme in the presence of AA could be achieved by differential pulse voltammetry. In Fig. 6 is presented the response of the PAH/SPE recorded in 5 mM NADH solution containing 5 mM AA. Two well defined oxidation peaks are observed at 343 mV for AA and 625 mV for NADH, respectively. A peak separation of 282 mV indicates that PAH modified sensor can be successfully used for selective detection of NADH in the presence of AA.

4. Conclusions

The efficiency of the poly(allylamine hydrochloride) for sensing application using screen-printed electrodes was explored in this work. The presence of the positively charge polyelectrolyte on the electrode surface was demonstrated by FT-IR and PAH modified SPE was used for detection of a negatively charged analyte NADH. The PAH/SPE sensor was optimized in terms of PAH concentration, pH of the working solution and applied potential for amperometric detection of NADH. The EIS experiments confirm the ability of polyelectrolyte modified electrode toward electrochemical detection of negatively charged species. The sensor calibration by amperometry and by differential pulse voltammetry revealed that NADH could be detected in the range of μM to mM. Moreover, DPV technique has allowed the selective determination of NADH in the presence of ascorbic acid.

The PAH modified SPE presents the advantage of a low background current compared to CNT based electrodes. A very attractive feature is the very simple preparation procedure compared to sensors based on electropolymerization or some nano-composite based on CNT, which require laborious procedures of purification and/or functionalization.

The modification of SPE with PAH leads to a major improvement for NADH detection and could exploited further for immobilization of dehydrogenases using layer-by-layer technique for developing sensitive and selective enzyme biosensors. PAH itself or in combination with other polyelectrolytes could play a double role, ones it improves the detection of NADH and second represents a good immobilization matrix for dehydrogenases.
