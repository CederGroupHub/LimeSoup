    Abstract

    The pitting potential of pure aluminum thin films in <mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:mn>50</mml:mn><mml:mspace width="0.3em"></mml:mspace><mml:mi>mM</mml:mi></mml:mrow></mml:math>50mM <mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:msub><mml:mi mathvariant="normal">K</mml:mi><mml:mrow><mml:mn>2</mml:mn></mml:mrow></mml:msub><mml:mi mathvariant="normal">S</mml:mi><mml:msub><mml:mi mathvariant="normal">O</mml:mi><mml:mrow><mml:mn>4</mml:mn></mml:mrow></mml:msub></mml:mrow></mml:math>K2SO4 was measured as a function of implanted Cl fluence. Samples were implanted with <mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:mn>35</mml:mn><mml:mspace width="0.3em"></mml:mspace><mml:mi>keV</mml:mi></mml:mrow></mml:math>35keV <mml:math display="inline"><mml:msup><mml:mi mathvariant="normal">Cl</mml:mi><mml:mo>+</mml:mo></mml:msup></mml:math>Cl+ at room temperature using fluences from <mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:mn>2.25</mml:mn><mml:mo>×</mml:mo><mml:msup><mml:mrow><mml:mn>10</mml:mn></mml:mrow><mml:mrow><mml:mn>16</mml:mn></mml:mrow></mml:msup><mml:mspace width="0.3em"></mml:mspace><mml:mtext>to</mml:mtext><mml:mspace width="0.3em"></mml:mspace><mml:mn>3.25</mml:mn><mml:mo>×</mml:mo><mml:msup><mml:mrow><mml:mn>10</mml:mn></mml:mrow><mml:mrow><mml:mn>16</mml:mn></mml:mrow></mml:msup><mml:mspace width="0.3em"></mml:mspace><mml:mtext>ions</mml:mtext><mml:mspace width="0.3em"></mml:mspace><mml:msup><mml:mi>cm</mml:mi><mml:mrow><mml:mo>−</mml:mo><mml:mn>2</mml:mn></mml:mrow></mml:msup></mml:mrow></mml:math>2.25×1016to3.25×1016ionscm−2 in increments of <mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:mn>0.25</mml:mn><mml:mo>×</mml:mo><mml:msup><mml:mrow><mml:mn>10</mml:mn></mml:mrow><mml:mrow><mml:mn>16</mml:mn></mml:mrow></mml:msup></mml:mrow></mml:math>0.25×1016 . An empirical relationship between pitting potential and fluence was found which suggests a critical Cl concentration in the oxide is necessary for pit initiation. No correlation between pitting potential and the measured Cl concentration or distribution in the metal was found.

    A key aspect to understanding oxide destabilization and subsequent pitting of passive metals is to elucidate the role of aggressive anions, particularly <mml:math display="inline"><mml:msup><mml:mi mathvariant="normal">Cl</mml:mi><mml:mo>−</mml:mo></mml:msup></mml:math>Cl− . Although numerous theories have been proposed in which fundamental physical and chemical processes for the interaction of <mml:math display="inline"><mml:msup><mml:mi mathvariant="normal">Cl</mml:mi><mml:mo>−</mml:mo></mml:msup></mml:math>Cl− and passivating oxides are described,1–9 the exact mechanism which causes oxide breakdown has yet to be determined. Some of the information that is lacking to date is the concentration of Cl and its specific location (i.e., surface, bulk, or interfacial) in the oxide that results in failure.

    The interaction of aqueous chloride and passive oxides has been studied by a variety of methods. Kolics et al. used radioactive tracers to study passive film breakdown as a function of chloride adsorption incorporation. In a comparative study of sulfate and chloride adsorption on 99.999% aluminum they showed that a significant portion of adsorbed chloride is incorporated into the passive film and the irreversibility of adsorption is enhanced by film growth.10 Their findings support that chloride anion incorporation rather than adsorption alone contributes to initiation of the pitting process. Berzins et al.11 measured localized chloride adsorption on 99.5% aluminum and found a quantitative relationship between Cl adsorption and NaCl solution concentration between 1 and <mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:msup><mml:mrow><mml:mn>10</mml:mn></mml:mrow><mml:mrow><mml:mo>−</mml:mo><mml:mn>5</mml:mn></mml:mrow></mml:msup><mml:mspace width="0.3em"></mml:mspace><mml:mi mathvariant="normal">M</mml:mi></mml:mrow></mml:math>10−5M . The concentration of chloride within the oxide was not determined, however, and pitting did not occur at all adsorption sites. Therefore, the relationship between localized chloride adsorption and pitting was not clear. Bockris and Minevski found a linear dependence of the breakdown potential on the log of concentration for aqueous chloride levels between <mml:math display="inline"><mml:msup><mml:mrow><mml:mn>10</mml:mn></mml:mrow><mml:mrow><mml:mo>−</mml:mo><mml:mn>4</mml:mn></mml:mrow></mml:msup></mml:math>10−4 and <mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:msup><mml:mrow><mml:mn>10</mml:mn></mml:mrow><mml:mrow><mml:mo>−</mml:mo><mml:mn>2</mml:mn></mml:mrow></mml:msup><mml:mspace width="0.3em"></mml:mspace><mml:mi mathvariant="normal">M</mml:mi></mml:mrow></mml:math>10−2M .12

    In another set of studies, ion implantation was used to study the migration of halide species in anodic alumina. Brown and Mackintosh and Skeldon et al. found that during film growth (i.e., under an electric field), implanted chloride ions migrate inward, toward the aluminum substrate.13, 14 These findings were supported by Shimuzu et al. in another migration study which used a different source of chloride ions incorporated into oxide films.15 Shimuzu’s reported chloride migration rates were in excellent agreement with values obtained by using implanted chloride, indicating that ion-implantation damage had little effect on the ionic transport processes of chloride. While the mobility of chloride in aluminum oxide has therefore been established, its relationship to pitting was not addressed.

    Most recently, Natishan et al. studied the uptake and incorporation of <mml:math display="inline"><mml:msup><mml:mi mathvariant="normal">Cl</mml:mi><mml:mo>−</mml:mo></mml:msup></mml:math>Cl− into native oxides on aluminum using X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) and X-ray absorption near edge structure (XANES).16–18 In their work, <mml:math display="inline"><mml:msup><mml:mi mathvariant="normal">Cl</mml:mi><mml:mo>−</mml:mo></mml:msup></mml:math>Cl− was detected both as an adsorbed species in the near-surface region and as an incorporated species in the bulk of the oxide after potentiostatic polarization at potentials below the pitting potential. Their results also provided evidence of chloride migration as well as oxide thinning. The chloride concentrations measured by these techniques, however, are average values which may not reflect the local concentration at a pit site.

    A continuing and specific goal of this research effort is to link both the location and concentration of chloride in the oxide film directly to pitting behavior. Unique in this undertaking are the use of a microcapillary test cell and ion implantation as the source of chloride. Use of a microcapillary allows small currents <mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:mo>(</mml:mo><mml:msup><mml:mrow><mml:mn>10</mml:mn></mml:mrow><mml:mrow><mml:mo>−</mml:mo><mml:mn>12</mml:mn></mml:mrow></mml:msup><mml:mspace width="0.3em"></mml:mspace><mml:mi mathvariant="normal">A</mml:mi><mml:mo>)</mml:mo></mml:mrow></mml:math>(10−12A) consistent with local oxide breakdown to be measured. Ion implantation provides a method by which the Cl concentration in the sample can be defined, unlike the case for solution-derived chloride, and thus provides a means of correlating <mml:math display="inline"><mml:msup><mml:mi mathvariant="normal">Cl</mml:mi><mml:mo>+</mml:mo></mml:msup></mml:math>Cl+ fluence to pitting behavior. Previous work in our laboratory demonstrated that implanted Cl could induce pitting of pure Al ingots and wires exposed to a <mml:math display="inline"><mml:msup><mml:mi mathvariant="normal">Cl</mml:mi><mml:mo>−</mml:mo></mml:msup></mml:math>Cl− -free electrolyte.19, 20 By testing over a range of fluences (at constant implant energy), a transition in pitting susceptibility was identified such that samples with <mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:mn>3</mml:mn><mml:mo>×</mml:mo><mml:msup><mml:mrow><mml:mn>10</mml:mn></mml:mrow><mml:mrow><mml:mn>16</mml:mn></mml:mrow></mml:msup><mml:mspace width="0.3em"></mml:mspace><mml:msup><mml:mi mathvariant="normal">Cl</mml:mi><mml:mo>+</mml:mo></mml:msup><mml:mspace width="0.3em"></mml:mspace><mml:msup><mml:mi>cm</mml:mi><mml:mrow><mml:mo>−</mml:mo><mml:mn>2</mml:mn></mml:mrow></mml:msup></mml:mrow></mml:math>3×1016Cl+cm−2 exhibited pitting in <mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:mn>50</mml:mn><mml:mspace width="0.3em"></mml:mspace><mml:mi>mM</mml:mi></mml:mrow></mml:math>50mM <mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:msub><mml:mi mathvariant="normal">K</mml:mi><mml:mrow><mml:mn>2</mml:mn></mml:mrow></mml:msub><mml:mi mathvariant="normal">S</mml:mi><mml:msub><mml:mi mathvariant="normal">O</mml:mi><mml:mrow><mml:mn>4</mml:mn></mml:mrow></mml:msub></mml:mrow></mml:math>K2SO4 , while samples with a fluence of <mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:mn>1</mml:mn><mml:mo>×</mml:mo><mml:msup><mml:mrow><mml:mn>10</mml:mn></mml:mrow><mml:mrow><mml:mn>16</mml:mn></mml:mrow></mml:msup><mml:mspace width="0.3em"></mml:mspace><mml:msup><mml:mi mathvariant="normal">Cl</mml:mi><mml:mo>+</mml:mo></mml:msup><mml:mspace width="0.3em"></mml:mspace><mml:msup><mml:mi>cm</mml:mi><mml:mrow><mml:mo>−</mml:mo><mml:mn>2</mml:mn></mml:mrow></mml:msup></mml:mrow></mml:math>1×1016Cl+cm−2 did not (Fig. 1). These results suggested that a minimum fluence, greater than <mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:mn>1</mml:mn><mml:mo>×</mml:mo><mml:msup><mml:mrow><mml:mn>10</mml:mn></mml:mrow><mml:mrow><mml:mn>16</mml:mn></mml:mrow></mml:msup><mml:mspace width="0.3em"></mml:mspace><mml:msup><mml:mi mathvariant="normal">Cl</mml:mi><mml:mo>+</mml:mo></mml:msup><mml:mspace width="0.3em"></mml:mspace><mml:msup><mml:mi>cm</mml:mi><mml:mrow><mml:mo>−</mml:mo><mml:mn>2</mml:mn></mml:mrow></mml:msup></mml:mrow></mml:math>1×1016Cl+cm−2 , is required for pit initiation.

    The goals of this work are the following: (i) determine the speciation, concentration, and distribution of implanted <mml:math display="inline"><mml:msup><mml:mi mathvariant="normal">Cl</mml:mi><mml:mo>+</mml:mo></mml:msup></mml:math>Cl+ in the oxide, (ii) validate TRIM calculations in the near surface region with XPS and time-of-flight secondary ion mass spectroscopy (TOF-SIMS) surface analysis results, (iii) correlate pitting potential and <mml:math display="inline"><mml:msup><mml:mi mathvariant="normal">Cl</mml:mi><mml:mo>+</mml:mo></mml:msup></mml:math>Cl+ implant fluence, and (iv) determine the effect of implanted Cl in the metal.

    XPS and TOF-SIMS measurements

    Implant regions of these Al films were analyzed using XPS with a Kratos Axis Ultra spectrometer. Chemical speciation of the Cl in the oxide was determined using Mg <mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:mi mathvariant="normal">K</mml:mi><mml:mi>α</mml:mi></mml:mrow></mml:math>Kα X-ray irradiation with the sample surface normal to the analyzer axis (90° analysis or take-off angle, maximum sampling depth). Cl concentration measurements were made using monochromatic Al <mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:mi mathvariant="normal">K</mml:mi><mml:mi>α</mml:mi></mml:mrow></mml:math>Kα X-ray irradiation with the sample at analysis angles of 90 and 30° to confirm expected Cl concentration increase with increasing implant fluence within the sampling depth limit. The implant profiles were measured using TOF-SIMS with a Physical Electronics TRIFT-I spectrometer and a <mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:mn>15</mml:mn><mml:mspace width="0.3em"></mml:mspace><mml:mi>keV</mml:mi></mml:mrow></mml:math>15keV <mml:math display="inline"><mml:mmultiscripts><mml:mi mathvariant="normal">Ga</mml:mi><mml:none></mml:none><mml:mo>+</mml:mo><mml:mprescripts></mml:mprescripts><mml:none></mml:none><mml:mrow><mml:mn>69</mml:mn></mml:mrow></mml:mmultiscripts></mml:math>Ga+69 source. Profiles were generated in a static mode with short periods of sputtering, as opposed to conventional dynamic mode profiling. Sputter rates were calibrated using a known thickness, amorphous <mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:msub><mml:mi mathvariant="normal">Al</mml:mi><mml:mrow><mml:mn>2</mml:mn></mml:mrow></mml:msub><mml:msub><mml:mi mathvariant="normal">O</mml:mi><mml:mrow><mml:mn>3</mml:mn></mml:mrow></mml:msub></mml:mrow></mml:math>Al2O3 standard. Oxide thickness values were determined by measuring the midpoint in the abrupt decrease in the secondary <mml:math display="inline"><mml:msup><mml:mi mathvariant="normal">O</mml:mi><mml:mo>−</mml:mo></mml:msup></mml:math>O− ion intensity. Samples were exposed to <mml:math display="inline"><mml:msub><mml:mi mathvariant="normal">O</mml:mi><mml:mrow><mml:mn>3</mml:mn></mml:mrow></mml:msub></mml:math>O3 in a commercial UV oven for <mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:mn>20</mml:mn><mml:mspace width="0.3em"></mml:mspace><mml:mi>min</mml:mi></mml:mrow></mml:math>20min immediately prior XPS analysis to remove adventitiously adsorbed hydrocarbon contaminants. The lack of measurable oxide growth with <mml:math display="inline"><mml:msub><mml:mi mathvariant="normal">O</mml:mi><mml:mrow><mml:mn>3</mml:mn></mml:mrow></mml:msub></mml:math>O3 exposure was confirmed by conducting TOF-SIMS analysis prior to and after cleaning.

    Electrochemical polarization

    Electrochemical measurements were made using a Gamry FAS2 Femtostat and a glass microcapillary test cell to expose sample areas on the order of <mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:mn>0.00005</mml:mn><mml:mspace width="0.3em"></mml:mspace><mml:msup><mml:mi>cm</mml:mi><mml:mrow><mml:mn>2</mml:mn></mml:mrow></mml:msup></mml:mrow></mml:math>0.00005cm2 . The capillary tip was coated with a noncorrosive, room temperature curing silicone rubber to provide an elastomeric seal with the sample. No evidence of crevicing under the seal was observed. The three-electrode test cell included a Pt wire counter electrode and a <mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:mi mathvariant="normal">Ag</mml:mi><mml:mo>∕</mml:mo><mml:mi mathvariant="normal">Ag</mml:mi><mml:mi mathvariant="normal">S</mml:mi><mml:msub><mml:mi mathvariant="normal">O</mml:mi><mml:mrow><mml:mn>4</mml:mn></mml:mrow></mml:msub></mml:mrow></mml:math>Ag∕AgSO4 pseudoreference electrode.     Results and Discussion

    TRIM–92 depth profiles

    Transport of Ions in Matter (TRIM) simulations21 were used to calculate the spatial (Gaussian) distribution of implanted Cl in aluminum as a function of ion fluence and implant energy. Although the effect of Cl in the near-surface region (in the native oxide layer and the vicinity of the oxide–metal interface) on pitting potential is the intended system of study, ion implantation inherently results in a large concentration of Cl in the metal and the possible effects on pitting behavior cannot be disregarded. The TRIM simulations clearly show that the bulk of the implanted Cl resides in the aluminum with much lower concentrations in the oxide layer. As one would expect, the simulations also show that for a given implant energy increasing the fluence results in a higher Cl content, most visibly indicated by an increase in peak height. The distribution (e.g., peak width and location) of Cl in the sample can be manipulated by varying the implant energy and provides a means to investigate the effect of Cl in the metal.

    Because the TRIM simulations do not take into account surface interactions arising from the implantation process, e.g., material loss due to sputtering and different sputtering rates of the oxide vs the metal, the predicted values for the near-surface region, i.e., the oxide layer, are subject to uncertainty. Despite this, TRIM simulations can be used to estimate the location of the oxide–metal interface and the Cl concentration in the oxide. The location of the oxide–metal interface on the TRIM simulation can be estimated by assuming (i) <mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:mn>3</mml:mn><mml:mo>–</mml:mo><mml:mn>4</mml:mn><mml:mspace width="0.3em"></mml:mspace><mml:mi>nm</mml:mi></mml:mrow></mml:math>3–4nm of material loss due to sputtering during the implantation process and (ii) a uniform oxide thickness on the order of <mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:mn>3</mml:mn><mml:mspace width="0.3em"></mml:mspace><mml:mi>nm</mml:mi></mml:mrow></mml:math>3nm . Using these guidelines, the oxide–metal interface is located at <mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:mn>6</mml:mn><mml:mspace width="0.3em"></mml:mspace><mml:mi>nm</mml:mi></mml:mrow></mml:math>6nm on the predicted Cl depth profile, as shown in Fig. 2 and 3 and (this does not apply to the TOF-SIMS generated profiles shown in Fig. 5). Furthermore, the total Cl content in the oxide layer can be estimated by integrating the area under the curve between 3 and <mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:mn>6</mml:mn><mml:mspace width="0.3em"></mml:mspace><mml:mi>nm</mml:mi></mml:mrow></mml:math>6nm , the results of which are shown in Table I for the fluences under consideration. The results indicate that unaccounted for effects of implant energy play a determinant role in pitting behavior.

    Conclusion

    There are several straightforward findings in this study. First, the speciation of implanted Cl in the aluminum thin film was found to be <mml:math display="inline"><mml:msup><mml:mi mathvariant="normal">Cl</mml:mi><mml:mo>−</mml:mo></mml:msup></mml:math>Cl− . Second, a dependence of pitting potential on Cl fluence (for a fixed energy) was demonstrated and described by an empirical mathematical expression. Finally, TRIM calculations were found to be useful estimates of the Cl concentration in the near-surface region, overestimating actual concentrations by approximately a factor of 2.

    Less clear are the relationships between the measured trends in Cl concentration and distribution within the oxide to the pitting behavior. A distinction is made between fluence and concentration, where fluence takes into account all of the accumulated damage (i.e., displacement-induced defects, electronic defects, and other impurities that are kinematically mixed into the oxide). These effects may be acting in tandem with the Cl in the oxide to produce the level of sensitivity to implant fluence observed in the XPS measurements. A second issue is that the small fluence and hence concentration increments used in this study are apparently below the detection limits of either XPS or SIMS. This finding is surprising for the more sensitive SIMS technique and raises questions as to what represents the largest source of variance in this material and process system. One possibility is that implantation results in a laterally heterogeneous Cl concentration. The 24% relative standard deviation measured for the <mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:mn>3</mml:mn><mml:mo>×</mml:mo><mml:msup><mml:mrow><mml:mn>10</mml:mn></mml:mrow><mml:mrow><mml:mn>16</mml:mn></mml:mrow></mml:msup><mml:mspace width="0.3em"></mml:mspace><mml:msup><mml:mi>cm</mml:mi><mml:mrow><mml:mo>−</mml:mo><mml:mn>2</mml:mn></mml:mrow></mml:msup></mml:mrow></mml:math>3×1016cm−2 sample may be an indication of a long-range lateral variation (<mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:mo></mml:mo><mml:mn>30</mml:mn><mml:mspace width="0.3em"></mml:mspace><mml:mi>μ</mml:mi><mml:mi mathvariant="normal">m</mml:mi></mml:mrow></mml:math>30μm , the SIMS analysis area). Lateral variation might take the form of a nonuniform implantation or a redistribution of Cl, once implanted, resulting in the formation of Cl-enriched nanodomains at the leading edge of the implant profile, as have been observed experimentally in Cl-implanted Al thin films.29 Thus, we have reached the following conclusions:

    1. Using XPS analysis, implanted Cl in the oxide was found to be consistent with the anion form of Cl.

    2. Although useful as a guide, TRIM calculations overestimate the actual Cl concentrations.

    3. The pitting potential for the samples under study exhibit a statistically significant variation as a function of implant fluence. A logarithmic dependence of <mml:math display="inline"><mml:msub><mml:mi>E</mml:mi><mml:mi>pit</mml:mi></mml:msub></mml:math>Epit on Cl fluence (for a given implant energy) was demonstrated. This type of relationship suggests a critical concentration effect.

    4. A qualitative correlation between TOF–SIMS-measured relative <mml:math display="inline"><mml:msup><mml:mi mathvariant="normal">Cl</mml:mi><mml:mo>−</mml:mo></mml:msup></mml:math>Cl− content and <mml:math display="inline"><mml:msub><mml:mi>E</mml:mi><mml:mi>pit</mml:mi></mml:msub></mml:math>Epit exists for the <mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:mn>35</mml:mn><mml:mspace width="0.3em"></mml:mspace><mml:mi>keV</mml:mi></mml:mrow></mml:math>35keV implant energy samples.

    5. The effect of Cl in the metal could not be determined from this data set.
