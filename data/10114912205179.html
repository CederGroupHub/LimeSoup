    Abstract

    The deposition of thin iron oxide films on <mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:mi>Si</mml:mi><mml:mo>(</mml:mo><mml:mn>100</mml:mn><mml:mo>)</mml:mo></mml:mrow></mml:math>Si(100) by metallorganic chemical vapor deposition at <mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:mn>55</mml:mn><mml:mspace width="0.3em"></mml:mspace><mml:mi>mbar</mml:mi></mml:mrow></mml:math>55mbar was systematically studied as a function of temperature between 673 and <mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:mn>1023</mml:mn><mml:mspace width="0.3em"></mml:mspace><mml:mi mathvariant="normal">K</mml:mi></mml:mrow></mml:math>1023K . Ferrocene and oxygen were used as precursors. The growth rate was measured as a function of temperature and the films were characterized by X-ray diffraction (XRD), Auger electron spectroscopy, energy dispersive X-ray analysis, and scanning electron microscopy. The change from the kinetically controlled regime to the transport controlled regime occurs near <mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:mn>750</mml:mn><mml:mspace width="0.3em"></mml:mspace><mml:mi mathvariant="normal">K</mml:mi></mml:mrow></mml:math>750K . At similar temperatures, a phase change of the deposited material was observed. Films prepared at temperatures higher than <mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:mn>823</mml:mn><mml:mspace width="0.3em"></mml:mspace><mml:mi mathvariant="normal">K</mml:mi></mml:mrow></mml:math>823K show the structure of <mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:mi>α</mml:mi><mml:mtext>-</mml:mtext><mml:msub><mml:mi>Fe</mml:mi><mml:mrow><mml:mn>2</mml:mn></mml:mrow></mml:msub><mml:msub><mml:mi mathvariant="normal">O</mml:mi><mml:mrow><mml:mn>3</mml:mn></mml:mrow></mml:msub></mml:mrow></mml:math>α-Fe2O3 , whereas deposition at lower temperature leads to the growth of <mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:mi>α</mml:mi><mml:mtext>-</mml:mtext><mml:msub><mml:mi>Fe</mml:mi><mml:mrow><mml:mn>2</mml:mn></mml:mrow></mml:msub><mml:msub><mml:mi mathvariant="normal">O</mml:mi><mml:mrow><mml:mn>3</mml:mn></mml:mrow></mml:msub></mml:mrow></mml:math>α-Fe2O3 and other oxide phases. The XRD pattern of these films can be explained by the coexistence of different iron oxide phases, namely <mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:mi>α</mml:mi><mml:mtext>-</mml:mtext><mml:msub><mml:mi>Fe</mml:mi><mml:mrow><mml:mn>2</mml:mn></mml:mrow></mml:msub><mml:msub><mml:mi mathvariant="normal">O</mml:mi><mml:mrow><mml:mn>3</mml:mn></mml:mrow></mml:msub></mml:mrow></mml:math>α-Fe2O3 , <mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:mi>γ</mml:mi><mml:mtext>-</mml:mtext><mml:msub><mml:mi>Fe</mml:mi><mml:mrow><mml:mn>2</mml:mn></mml:mrow></mml:msub><mml:msub><mml:mi mathvariant="normal">O</mml:mi><mml:mrow><mml:mn>3</mml:mn></mml:mrow></mml:msub></mml:mrow></mml:math>γ-Fe2O3 , and/or <mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:mi>β</mml:mi><mml:mtext>-</mml:mtext><mml:msub><mml:mi>Fe</mml:mi><mml:mrow><mml:mn>2</mml:mn></mml:mrow></mml:msub><mml:msub><mml:mi mathvariant="normal">O</mml:mi><mml:mrow><mml:mn>3</mml:mn></mml:mrow></mml:msub></mml:mrow></mml:math>β-Fe2O3 .

    Thin iron oxide films have been studied extensively in recent decades because of their interesting physical and chemical properties and possible applications. A comprehensive review of the basic types of iron oxides, their physical and chemical properties, and several possible applications are given in Ref. 1.

    Depending on the oxygen content, iron can form several oxides, such as wustite <mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:mo>(</mml:mo><mml:mi>FeO</mml:mi><mml:mo>)</mml:mo></mml:mrow></mml:math>(FeO) , magnetite <mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:mo>(</mml:mo><mml:msub><mml:mi>Fe</mml:mi><mml:mrow><mml:mn>3</mml:mn></mml:mrow></mml:msub><mml:msub><mml:mi mathvariant="normal">O</mml:mi><mml:mrow><mml:mn>4</mml:mn></mml:mrow></mml:msub><mml:mo>)</mml:mo></mml:mrow></mml:math>(Fe3O4) , and <mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:msub><mml:mi>Fe</mml:mi><mml:mrow><mml:mn>2</mml:mn></mml:mrow></mml:msub><mml:msub><mml:mi mathvariant="normal">O</mml:mi><mml:mrow><mml:mn>3</mml:mn></mml:mrow></mml:msub></mml:mrow></mml:math>Fe2O3 . From the iron-oxygen phase diagram2, 3 one can expect that at low oxygen content <mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:mo>(</mml:mo><mml:mo><</mml:mo><mml:mn>57</mml:mn><mml:mo>%</mml:mo><mml:mo>)</mml:mo></mml:mrow></mml:math>(<57%) and temperatures below <mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:mn>843</mml:mn><mml:mspace width="0.3em"></mml:mspace><mml:mi mathvariant="normal">K</mml:mi></mml:mrow></mml:math>843K , magnetite is formed, whereas at higher oxygen content <mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:msub><mml:mi>Fe</mml:mi><mml:mrow><mml:mn>2</mml:mn></mml:mrow></mml:msub><mml:msub><mml:mi mathvariant="normal">O</mml:mi><mml:mrow><mml:mn>3</mml:mn></mml:mrow></mml:msub></mml:mrow></mml:math>Fe2O3 is favored. Thereby the latter modification can occur in stable phases (hematite: <mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:mi>α</mml:mi><mml:mtext>-</mml:mtext><mml:msub><mml:mi>Fe</mml:mi><mml:mrow><mml:mn>2</mml:mn></mml:mrow></mml:msub><mml:msub><mml:mi mathvariant="normal">O</mml:mi><mml:mrow><mml:mn>3</mml:mn></mml:mrow></mml:msub></mml:mrow></mml:math>α-Fe2O3 ) and metastable phases (maghemite: <mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:mi>γ</mml:mi><mml:mtext>-</mml:mtext><mml:msub><mml:mi>Fe</mml:mi><mml:mrow><mml:mn>2</mml:mn></mml:mrow></mml:msub><mml:msub><mml:mi mathvariant="normal">O</mml:mi><mml:mrow><mml:mn>3</mml:mn></mml:mrow></mml:msub></mml:mrow></mml:math>γ-Fe2O3 ): For thin films, a coexistence between the stable phase (α-phase) and the metastable phases (γ- and β-phase) of <mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:msub><mml:mi>Fe</mml:mi><mml:mrow><mml:mn>2</mml:mn></mml:mrow></mml:msub><mml:msub><mml:mi mathvariant="normal">O</mml:mi><mml:mrow><mml:mn>3</mml:mn></mml:mrow></mml:msub></mml:mrow></mml:math>Fe2O3 was observed at low temperatures,4 whereas with increasing temperature the α-phase is preferentially present.5, 6

    The applications of iron oxides are manifold, as some phases have interesting magnetic properties, some can be used as a catalyst, and some have semiconductor properties.1 Magnetite <mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:mo>(</mml:mo><mml:msub><mml:mi>Fe</mml:mi><mml:mrow><mml:mn>3</mml:mn></mml:mrow></mml:msub><mml:msub><mml:mi mathvariant="normal">O</mml:mi><mml:mrow><mml:mn>4</mml:mn></mml:mrow></mml:msub><mml:mo>)</mml:mo></mml:mrow></mml:math>(Fe3O4) , for example, is a ferrimagnet with a high Curie temperature of <mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:mn>858</mml:mn><mml:mspace width="0.3em"></mml:mspace><mml:mi mathvariant="normal">K</mml:mi></mml:mrow></mml:math>858K and electronically conducting with highly spin-polarized conduction electrons. Consequently, it is an interesting candidate for magnetic recording media or spin-valve applications.7 Hematite <mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:mo>(</mml:mo><mml:mi>α</mml:mi><mml:mtext>-</mml:mtext><mml:msub><mml:mi>Fe</mml:mi><mml:mrow><mml:mn>2</mml:mn></mml:mrow></mml:msub><mml:msub><mml:mi mathvariant="normal">O</mml:mi><mml:mrow><mml:mn>3</mml:mn></mml:mrow></mml:msub><mml:mo>)</mml:mo></mml:mrow></mml:math>(α-Fe2O3) has semiconductor properties with a bandgap of <mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:mn>2.2</mml:mn><mml:mspace width="0.3em"></mml:mspace><mml:mi>eV</mml:mi></mml:mrow></mml:math>2.2eV .1 It was studied extensively in recent years due to its possible usage in several applications, so it is interesting for the photocatalytic production of hydrogen from water,8–11 but also for the usage in gas sensors12 or humidity sensors.13 Recently, it was discussed as a coating of architectural glazing.14 It is remarkable that hematite can easily be structured on the nanoscale, e.g., as nanoclusters/particles15 or as nanowires.16 This may give rise to future applications.

    Besides the particular physical and chemical properties of hematite, this phase is the preferable form for several applications at higher temperatures or under oxidizing conditions, e.g., catalytic applications, because it is thermodynamically stable. Therefore, the production of hematite films is often of preferential interest because this phase is not expected to be gradually transformed with time under such conditions.

    Several methods have been used to prepare iron oxide films including physical vapor deposition (PVD),17 chemical vapor deposition (CVD), and some wet chemical procedures.5 Among these techniques, metallorganic chemical vapor deposition (MOCVD) is a powerful and inexpensive method to deposit rather thick films of a few micrometers thickness with high growth rates.     Different precursors were used in the MOCVD experiments. Several groups report on the successful preparation of iron oxide films from <mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:mi>Fe</mml:mi><mml:mo>(</mml:mo><mml:mi>CO</mml:mi><mml:msub><mml:mo>)</mml:mo><mml:mrow><mml:mn>5</mml:mn></mml:mrow></mml:msub></mml:mrow></mml:math>Fe(CO)5 ,21, 22 <mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:mi>Fe</mml:mi><mml:mo>(</mml:mo><mml:msub><mml:mi>OC</mml:mi><mml:mrow><mml:mn>2</mml:mn></mml:mrow></mml:msub><mml:msub><mml:mi mathvariant="normal">H</mml:mi><mml:mrow><mml:mn>5</mml:mn></mml:mrow></mml:msub><mml:msub><mml:mo>)</mml:mo><mml:mrow><mml:mn>3</mml:mn></mml:mrow></mml:msub></mml:mrow></mml:math>Fe(OC2H5)3 ,23 and ferric acetylacetonate <mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:mi>Fe</mml:mi><mml:mo>(</mml:mo><mml:mi>acac</mml:mi><mml:msub><mml:mo>)</mml:mo><mml:mrow><mml:mn>3</mml:mn></mml:mrow></mml:msub></mml:mrow></mml:math>Fe(acac)3 ,18–20 and some groups from ferrocene <mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:mo>(</mml:mo><mml:msub><mml:mi>FeC</mml:mi><mml:mrow><mml:mn>10</mml:mn></mml:mrow></mml:msub><mml:msub><mml:mi mathvariant="normal">H</mml:mi><mml:mrow><mml:mn>10</mml:mn></mml:mrow></mml:msub><mml:mo>)</mml:mo></mml:mrow></mml:math>(FeC10H10) ,4, 14, 24 as precursors. The acetylacetonate starts to decompose in the evaporator,25, 26 the iron(III) tert-butoxide is lately synthesized,27 and the carbonyl compound is highly toxic, while ferrocene is a less toxic chemical of some industrial importance and therefore relatively inexpensive. Nevertheless, former work of iron oxide formation from ferrocene concentrated on a very limited parameter range or a certain application. Systematic studies of even the most important process parameters were not found in the literature, so that the present study wants to provide data on one of the most important parameters, namely, the influence of temperature on the deposition. For example, Sharon et al.24 varied the substrate temperature in a systematic way. However, they did not analyze the growth rate but the phase of the deposit. Liu et al.4 used plasma enhancement, so that the process is quite different. So even if one suspects that the iron oxide deposition from ferrocene oxygen mixtures should be a well established process, the main deposition parameters are not established yet. Also, ferrocene evaporates without residues at temperatures around <mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:mn>406</mml:mn><mml:mspace width="0.3em"></mml:mspace><mml:mi mathvariant="normal">K</mml:mi></mml:mrow></mml:math>406K as proved by thermogravimetric measurements, so that evaporation and decomposition do not take place simultaneously.25 This is an important advantage in comparison with the acetylacetonate.

    The present study concentrates on one of the most important parameters, the temperature influence on deposition, which is studied in a simple one-dimensional geometry, namely, a stagnation point flow. All other process parameters (pressure and gas phase composition) were kept constant. The growth rate was measured as a function of the deposition temperature between 673 and <mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:mn>1023</mml:mn><mml:mspace width="0.3em"></mml:mspace><mml:mi mathvariant="normal">K</mml:mi></mml:mrow></mml:math>1023K , and the morphology, the chemical composition, and the structure of the films were analyzed. As a consequence of the simple flow geometry, our experiment can easily be modeled. This makes our data useful for further analysis of the growth mechanism, e.g., a theoretical modeling of the process.

    Experimental

    The cold-wall CVD apparatus (Fig. 1) consisted of a stainless steel vacuum chamber, which could be evacuated to a base pressure of <mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:mn>0.5</mml:mn><mml:mspace width="0.3em"></mml:mspace><mml:mi>mbar</mml:mi></mml:mrow></mml:math>0.5mbar . For the iron oxide deposition, a mixture of ferrocene vapor, oxygen (purity 4.5), and argon (purity 5.0) as carrier gas was blown over the electrically heated substrate in a stagnation point flow geometry. The nozzle orifice had a diameter of <mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:mn>4</mml:mn><mml:mspace width="0.3em"></mml:mspace><mml:mi>mm</mml:mi></mml:mrow></mml:math>4mm and its distance above the substrate surface was <mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:mn>14</mml:mn><mml:mspace width="0.3em"></mml:mspace><mml:mi>mm</mml:mi></mml:mrow></mml:math>14mm . Ferrocene <mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:mo>(</mml:mo><mml:msub><mml:mi>FeC</mml:mi><mml:mrow><mml:mn>10</mml:mn></mml:mrow></mml:msub><mml:msub><mml:mi mathvariant="normal">H</mml:mi><mml:mrow><mml:mn>10</mml:mn></mml:mrow></mml:msub><mml:mo>)</mml:mo></mml:mrow></mml:math>(FeC10H10) with a purity of 98% was used as a precursor. It is a solid powder and was sublimated at a constant temperature of <mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:mn>343</mml:mn><mml:mspace width="0.3em"></mml:mspace><mml:mi mathvariant="normal">K</mml:mi></mml:mrow></mml:math>343K in a fluidized bed evaporator. As carrier gas, a flow of 0.05 standard liters per minute (slm) argon was applied. This flow was mixed with <mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:mn>0.9</mml:mn><mml:mspace width="0.3em"></mml:mspace><mml:mi>slm</mml:mi></mml:mrow></mml:math>0.9slm argon and <mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:mn>0.1</mml:mn><mml:mspace width="0.3em"></mml:mspace><mml:mi>slm</mml:mi></mml:mrow></mml:math>0.1slm oxygen. Finally, the gas flow at the nozzle orifice was laminar (Reynolds number <mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:mi>Re</mml:mi><mml:mo>=</mml:mo><mml:mn>217</mml:mn></mml:mrow></mml:math>Re=217 ) and consisted of <mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:mn>90.436</mml:mn><mml:mspace width="0.3em"></mml:mspace><mml:mi>vol</mml:mi><mml:mspace width="0.3em"></mml:mspace><mml:mo>%</mml:mo></mml:mrow></mml:math>90.436vol% argon, <mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:mn>9.520</mml:mn><mml:mspace width="0.3em"></mml:mspace><mml:mi>vol</mml:mi><mml:mspace width="0.3em"></mml:mspace><mml:mo>%</mml:mo></mml:mrow></mml:math>9.520vol% oxygen, and <mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:mn>0.045</mml:mn><mml:mspace width="0.3em"></mml:mspace><mml:mi>vol</mml:mi><mml:mspace width="0.3em"></mml:mspace><mml:mo>%</mml:mo></mml:mrow></mml:math>0.045vol% ferrocene in each experiment.

    The substrates used in this study were boron-doped <mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:mi>Si</mml:mi><mml:mo>(</mml:mo><mml:mn>100</mml:mn><mml:mo>)</mml:mo></mml:mrow></mml:math>Si(100) wafers (p-type). Therefore, the substrates were capped by a thermally grown silicon oxide layer, which was prepared by oxidizing the samples for <mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:mn>10</mml:mn><mml:mspace width="0.3em"></mml:mspace><mml:mi>min</mml:mi></mml:mrow></mml:math>10min at <mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:mn>1023</mml:mn><mml:mspace width="0.3em"></mml:mspace><mml:mi mathvariant="normal">K</mml:mi></mml:mrow></mml:math>1023K and <mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:mn>55</mml:mn><mml:mspace width="0.3em"></mml:mspace><mml:mi>mbar</mml:mi></mml:mrow></mml:math>55mbar in an atmosphere of 10% oxygen and 90% argon prior to each deposition experiment. These pretreatments ensure that no further oxidation occurs in the initial stages of the film growth as the temperatures during the subsequent iron oxide depositions were <mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:mn>1023</mml:mn><mml:mspace width="0.3em"></mml:mspace><mml:mi mathvariant="normal">K</mml:mi></mml:mrow></mml:math>1023K or less, and thus the initial substrate surfaces were the same for all presented measurements.

    After deposition, the films were kept under atmospheric conditions and subsequently characterized by the following methods: The phases of the films were analyzed by grazing incidence X-ray diffraction (XRD), the morphology of the surfaces by scanning electron microscopy (SEM), and the chemical composition of the films by Auger-electron spectroscopy (AES) and energy dispersive X-ray analysis (EDX).

    Results and Discussion

    Temperature dependence of the film growth

    Thin iron oxide films were deposited for <mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:mn>60</mml:mn><mml:mspace width="0.3em"></mml:mspace><mml:mi>min</mml:mi></mml:mrow></mml:math>60min at constant temperatures between 673 and <mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:mn>1023</mml:mn><mml:mspace width="0.3em"></mml:mspace><mml:mi mathvariant="normal">K</mml:mi></mml:mrow></mml:math>1023K . The results are plotted vs the inverse deposition temperature in Fig. 2. The calculation of the growth rate is based on the film density, which is not well known for deposition temperatures lower than <mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:mn>773</mml:mn><mml:mspace width="0.3em"></mml:mspace><mml:mi mathvariant="normal">K</mml:mi></mml:mrow></mml:math>773K as the phases of the films are not fully identified at these temperatures (see the section Film structure and oxide phases). Therefore, the growth rates may be somewhat ambiguous below <mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:mn>773</mml:mn><mml:mspace width="0.3em"></mml:mspace><mml:mi mathvariant="normal">K</mml:mi></mml:mrow></mml:math>773K . However, the mass deposition rates (left ordinate in Fig. 2) were directly measured for the whole temperature range. Additionally, a curve is included in Fig. 2 (dashed line), which is proportional to <mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:msup><mml:mi>T</mml:mi><mml:mrow><mml:mn>1.75</mml:mn></mml:mrow></mml:msup></mml:mrow></mml:math>T1.75 . Such a growth rate is expected for a diffusion-controlled growth mechanism: In the present experiments, a constant mass flux of the precursors and a constant distance between the inlet tube and the substrate were used. Therefore, the growth rate is either limited by the heterogeneous reaction rate or by the diffusion of the precursor through the boundary layer.28, 29 At high temperatures due to Arrhenius behavior, rate coefficients get high and thus the growth rate is in general limited by the diffusion of the precursor through the boundary layer. The diffusion coefficient increases with <mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:msup><mml:mi>T</mml:mi><mml:mrow><mml:mn>1.75</mml:mn></mml:mrow></mml:msup></mml:mrow></mml:math>T1.75 .30 Therefore, a similar qualitative behavior is expected for the growth rate (dashed line). Therefore, these deposition rates were corrected by estimating the spalled volume with SEM; the images reveal a loss of about 15% (open circles in Fig. 2).

    Morphology of the films

    The morphology of the films was analyzed by SEM and by optical microscopy (not shown here). As predicted by calculations of the Sherwood number for an impinging jet,31 a gradient could be observed in the film growth between the center of the sample and the outer parts. Usually, the film was thicker in the center and also the grain size was larger than in the outer region. Additionally, the film thickness of the samples increased with increasing deposition temperature as the deposition time was kept constant, <mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:mn>60</mml:mn><mml:mspace width="0.3em"></mml:mspace><mml:mi>min</mml:mi></mml:mrow></mml:math>60min , and the deposition rate increased (see Temperature dependence of the film growth). The measurements confirm the presence of mainly iron and oxygen, and a small amount of carbon in the film. Thereby, the composition of the films stays nearly constant with changing deposition temperatures, namely 58.5% oxygen, 38.5% iron, and 3.0% carbon. Thus, the measurements reveal an iron to oxygen ratio of around 0.66 for all analyzed deposition temperatures indicating the presence of <mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:msub><mml:mi>Fe</mml:mi><mml:mrow><mml:mn>2</mml:mn></mml:mrow></mml:msub><mml:msub><mml:mi mathvariant="normal">O</mml:mi><mml:mrow><mml:mn>3</mml:mn></mml:mrow></mml:msub></mml:mrow></mml:math>Fe2O3 . The presence of carbon impurities is not surprising as the ferrocene precursor partly consists of this element. However, the other elements that are detected by AES, namely sulfur or chlorine, could not be observed with EDX. This confirms our suggestion that these elements are only surface contaminations from the laboratory environment. One should keep in mind that AES is a surface-sensitive method whereas EDX gives information nearly about the complete film.

    Conclusion

    In conclusion, the growth of iron oxide films on <mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:mi>Si</mml:mi><mml:mo>(</mml:mo><mml:mn>100</mml:mn><mml:mo>)</mml:mo></mml:mrow></mml:math>Si(100) was studied systematically in the temperature range between 673 and <mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:mn>1023</mml:mn><mml:mspace width="0.3em"></mml:mspace><mml:mi mathvariant="normal">K</mml:mi></mml:mrow></mml:math>1023K by MOCVD using ferrocene <mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:mo>(</mml:mo><mml:msub><mml:mi>FeC</mml:mi><mml:mrow><mml:mn>10</mml:mn></mml:mrow></mml:msub><mml:msub><mml:mi mathvariant="normal">H</mml:mi><mml:mrow><mml:mn>10</mml:mn></mml:mrow></mml:msub><mml:mo>)</mml:mo></mml:mrow></mml:math>(FeC10H10) and oxygen as precursors. In the present experiments, high oxygen concentrations were used in the gas phase, and therefore the desired <mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:msub><mml:mi>Fe</mml:mi><mml:mrow><mml:mn>2</mml:mn></mml:mrow></mml:msub><mml:msub><mml:mi mathvariant="normal">O</mml:mi><mml:mrow><mml:mn>3</mml:mn></mml:mrow></mml:msub></mml:mrow></mml:math>Fe2O3 films were grown, as expected by the phase diagram of iron oxide.2, 3 Actually, it was found that in the whole temperature range the films consist at least partly of hematite, the stable phase of <mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:msub><mml:mi>Fe</mml:mi><mml:mrow><mml:mn>2</mml:mn></mml:mrow></mml:msub><mml:msub><mml:mi mathvariant="normal">O</mml:mi><mml:mrow><mml:mn>3</mml:mn></mml:mrow></mml:msub></mml:mrow></mml:math>Fe2O3 . Above <mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:mn>823</mml:mn><mml:mspace width="0.3em"></mml:mspace><mml:mi mathvariant="normal">K</mml:mi></mml:mrow></mml:math>823K , it was the sole detectable phase. Fully developed columnar <mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:mi>α</mml:mi><mml:mtext>-</mml:mtext><mml:msub><mml:mi>Fe</mml:mi><mml:mrow><mml:mn>2</mml:mn></mml:mrow></mml:msub><mml:msub><mml:mi mathvariant="normal">O</mml:mi><mml:mrow><mml:mn>3</mml:mn></mml:mrow></mml:msub></mml:mrow></mml:math>α-Fe2O3 grains were observed, which were oriented perpendicular to the surface. The films grown at these high temperatures were well adherent. Up to <mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:mn>773</mml:mn><mml:mspace width="0.3em"></mml:mspace><mml:mi mathvariant="normal">K</mml:mi></mml:mrow></mml:math>773K , further <mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:msub><mml:mi>Fe</mml:mi><mml:mrow><mml:mn>2</mml:mn></mml:mrow></mml:msub><mml:msub><mml:mi mathvariant="normal">O</mml:mi><mml:mrow><mml:mn>3</mml:mn></mml:mrow></mml:msub></mml:mrow></mml:math>Fe2O3 phases appeared in addition to hematite, which could not be resolved from XRD measurements. However, the existence of <mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:msub><mml:mi>Fe</mml:mi><mml:mrow><mml:mn>3</mml:mn></mml:mrow></mml:msub><mml:msub><mml:mi mathvariant="normal">O</mml:mi><mml:mrow><mml:mn>4</mml:mn></mml:mrow></mml:msub></mml:mrow></mml:math>Fe3O4 could be excluded from the Auger measurements at least in the upper layers of the films. Films prepared at low temperature were partly broken off the substrate.

    At temperatures around <mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:mn>823</mml:mn><mml:mspace width="0.3em"></mml:mspace><mml:mi mathvariant="normal">K</mml:mi></mml:mrow></mml:math>823K , not only the phase transition is found but also a change in the growth kinetics was observed. Below <mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:mn>723</mml:mn><mml:mspace width="0.3em"></mml:mspace><mml:mi mathvariant="normal">K</mml:mi></mml:mrow></mml:math>723K the growth seemed to be controlled by surface kinetics with simple Arrhenius behavior, while at higher temperatures the growth rate was decelerated, presumably due to transport limitations in the gas phase. The growth rate decreased further at temperatures above <mml:math display="inline"><mml:mrow><mml:mn>900</mml:mn><mml:mspace width="0.3em"></mml:mspace><mml:mi mathvariant="normal">K</mml:mi></mml:mrow></mml:math>900K , probably due to precursor depletion. Simultaneously, SEM images showed the existence of large agglomerated crystallites. Both observations indicate that presumably gas phase nucleation occurred at such high temperatures.
