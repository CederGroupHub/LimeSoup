Abstract

Cerium‐doped terbium gallium garnet single crystal having a large Verdet constant was grown by floating zone (FZ) method, which was suitable for the use in optical devices. The lattice parameters and the X‐ray rocking curve measurement of the crystal was determined by X‐ray diffraction analysis. The Verdet constant of the crystal (B = 0.55 T) at the wavelength of 632.8 nm was −165.8 rad m−1 T−1 at room temperature, 23.7% larger than that of pure TGG (−134.0 rad m−1 T−1). The performance of the high optical quality and excellent magneto‐optical properties of the crystal shows the great potential of using this new method to meet the increasing demand of VI‐NIR Faraday rotators.

1 Introduction

Optical isolators (OIs) are the core parts of high‐power‐laser equipment and advanced optical communications 1, 2, it can prevent reflected light from entering laser systems. Yttrium iron garnet, Y3Fe5O12 (YIG) is the key FR material for the infrared region (>1100 nm) 3. However, YIG cannot be used at shorter wavelengths (<1100 nm) due to its poor transparency. Recently, the demand for optical isolators that operate at shorter wavelengths (VI‐NIR) has increased greatly 4. Terbium gallium garnet (TGG) material (crystal or ceramic) has a Verdet constant of 36 rad m−1 T−1 at a wavelength of 1 µm, a high thermal conductivity of 4.9 W m−1 K−1 and excellent size scalability, leading to the implementation of TGG in FR working at shorter wavelengths 5-7. Although TGG bulk crystals can be grown by the Czochralski (Cz) technique due to its congruent melting properties 8, its growth presents serious difficulties related with the decomposition and vaporization of Ga2O3 from the melt, resulting in the comparable low quality of the crystal 9, 10. Moreover, the TGG crystal grown by Cz, usually with some core area like the Nd:YAG, significantly reduces the optical homogeneity, the extinction ratio and beam quality after passing through the Faraday device of the crystal.

From our research into the crystals with a high‐vaporization nature and that can be reproducibly grown and processed to high optical quality FRs, we realized that the FZ method has great potential to grow this sort of materials and we have grown several mm‐diameter pure TGG crystals with great optical properties 11, 12. In this way, it can be easily fabricated by the FZ method and its growth cycle is short compared to the Cz method. However, the comparatively low Verdet constant in the VI‐NIR region leads us to find a magneto‐optical material that has a larger V that can be effective in size reduction of OIs and this material is based on the TGG crystal because of its thermal and optical advantages, as mentioned above 13. The current investigation centers on the growth of crystals doped with paramagnetic RE ions (Ce) by the FZ method and their optical quality, microstructure, and magneto‐optical property.

2 Experimental

2.1 Crystal growth

Single crystals of Ce doped TGG were grown by the FZ technique. The starting materials were Tb4O7, CeO2, Ga2O3 (99.999%) mixed according to the corresponding nominal cationic ratios. First, TGG powder was pressed into rods by a cold isostatic press under a pressure of 60 MPa. Then the rods were sintered in a high‐temperature furnace equipped at 1400°C for 10 h in air. The sintered rods were about 70–80 mm long and 8–10 mm in diameter.

Crystal growth was carried out by floating zone technique using four‐halogen lamps (FZ‐T−10000‐H‐VI‐VP, Japan Crystal Growth System Co. Ltd.). The previously grown single crystal was set as a seed with the 〈1 1 1〉 plane in the growth direction. The sintered rods rotated at 8 rpm and the crystals were grown with a speed of 1–3 mm/h, under dry air flow with a rate 0.2 m3/h.

2.2 X‐ray powder diffraction and transmission spectrum

The X‐ray diffraction (XRD) analyses were performed using the Ultima IV (Rigaku, Japan),diffraction data were collected under 40 kV, 40 mA. Typical scans were carried out from 3° to 20° (2θ) at each step of 0.01° and the rocking curve for the crystal was measured by the PANalytical Empyrean XRD apparatus. The transmission spectrum and absorption spectrum were measured using a Perkin‐Elmer Lambda 900 UV‐VIS‐NIR spectrophotometer (Japan).

2.3 Faraday effect measurement

The Faraday rotation of the TCGG crystal is measured by the magneto‐optic modulated double‐frequency method 14, and the experimental setup is shown schematically in figure 1. A light beam passes through a polarizer P1 becoming linearly polarized. And then it propagates through the crystal which is placed in the magnetic field generated by an electromagnet(EM). After that the linearly polarized light is modulated by a magneto‐optic modulator(M). The output beam is detected by a PIN photodiode D after it passes through a polarizer P2. After the detected photocurrent is magnified by amplifiers, it is displayed by an oscillograph. By adjusting the input current of EM and rotating the orientation azimuth of P2, the Faraday rotation angle at different magnetic field can be obtained.

2.4 Weak absorption coefficient of the crystal

The weak absorption coefficient of the TCGG using the PCI technique 15 (photothermal common‐path interferometer system manufactured by Stanford photon‐thermal solution in Stanford, California, United States).

3 Results and Discussion

3.1 XRD patterns of as‐grown crystal

The as‐grown single crystal with 4–5 mm in diameter and 20 mm in length by the FZ method was shown in the figure 2. The X‐ray powder diffraction patterns of the powder specimen is shown in figure 3. Compared with the JCPDS standard card, the XRD powder diffraction pattern of TCGG crystal is agree well with the standard patterns(JCPDS 88‐0575) without any impurities peaks. The unit‐cell parameters are a = b = c = 1.2408 nm, slightly larger than that of pure TGG (a = b = c = 1.2355 nm). It can be explained by the reason that the radius of Ce3+ is larger than Tb3+. The full width at half‐maximum of the rocking curve for the crystal is about 38 arcsec which showed the high crystallinity of the crystal.

3.2 Optical properties of the crystal

The optical transmittance spectra of TCGG is shown in the figure 4. The absorption band around 400–500 nm due to the Ce3+:4f–5d1 transition was so strong that the absorption of Tb3+: 7F6–5D4 was covered and could not be observed. TCGG shows high degree of transmittance observed in the region of 500–1500 nm. Absorption coefficient a0 is an important parameter of magneto‐optical properties.

TCGG has a weak absorption loss in the working wavelength 500–1500 nm. The a0 for TCGG crystal was measured by the PCI technique. It can be seen in the figure 5 that the peak is around 3000 ppm/cm at 632.8 nm. Therefore, the low weak absorption loss indicates that the as‐grown single crystal in our work is suitable for application of apparatus used in the VI‐NIR Faraday rotator.

3.3 magneto‐optical property of the crystal

The Verdet constant at 632.8 nm of the grown crystal measured at room temperature was −165.8 rad m−1 T−1, which is 23.7% larger than that of pure TGG (−134.0 rad m−1 T−1). The result showed that doping with Ce3+ ions significantly enhance the the magneto‐optical property of TGG. It can be explained by that Ce3+ has the largest ionic radius among the paramagnetic rare earth ions, so when it is doped into the TAG host lattice, the crystal field of TGG would be changed much. The strong crystal field effect on Ce3+ and the quantum based super‐exchange interaction between Tb3+ and Ce3+ which may contribute to the enhancement of the Verdet constant for Ce doped TGG crystal

4. Conclusion

We successfully grew TCGG single crystals by the FZ method for the first time. Flawless TCGG crystals of 4–5 mm diameter were applicable to optical devices because of their high transparency and large Verdet constants. Owing to the low cost and the short growth cycle, we can use this FZ method to grow Ce‐doped TGG crystals with different ion concentrations, giving a huge expectation of producing a superior magneto‐optical material from Ce‐doped TGG crystals.
