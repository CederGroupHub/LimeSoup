Abstract

A convenient and general approach for cyclopenta[b]naphthalene derivatives containing a structurally diverse functionality at C-4 position has been successfully developed, whereby manganese(III) acetate plays a key role to facilitate the tandem 5-exo-dig and 6-exo/endo cyclization in an efficient manner. Various functional groups, including trimethyl silyl, aryl, ester, ketone, secondary/tertiary amide, phosphonate and halogen, capped on the terminal acetylenic unit are well tolerated under optimal reaction conditions. It is highly conceivable that this novel [6.6.5] framework formation procedure may have broad synthetic utility in light of its operational simplicity and high yields.

Introduction

Cyclopenta[b]naphthalenes are known to be important chemical components for building pharmaceuticals, dyes and liquid crystals, the naphthalene units of which have great potential to serve as attachable fluorophores and are useful for bioconjugate studies in many complex biological systems.1 Synthetically, cyclopenta[b]naphthalene substructures are hard to access by any existing methods except for an intramolecular styrenyl dehydro-Diels–Alder (DDA) reaction as typified by Scheme 1.2 However, harsh reaction conditions, such as high temperatures (160–300 °C) and long reaction times, are usually required to force the styrenyl DDA process to occur.2

α-Cyano ketones 1 (Scheme 2) are found to undergo tandem cyclization with ease to afford the corresponding cyclopenta[b]naphthalene derivatives 2 under the reaction conditions (a) or (b) previously developed by us.3,4

However, when the scope of the title system was investigated by methods (a) and (b), unfortunately, it was found that the substituent R in substrate 1 was quite limited in terms of structural diversity. For example, if R was an ester group, reactant 3 merely afforded the desired cyclopenta[b]naphthalene 4 in a trace amount (<5% yield) following these two synthetic protocols. Indeed, protocol (a) resulted in monocyclic esters 5 (E:Z=1:1; 55%) as major products, and (b) produced benzoic acid and diketone 6 in 52% and 25% yields, respectively, as major products. These results apparently indicate that under conditions (a) and (b), cyclopenta[b]naphthalenes 2 could be effectively formed with the terminal R limited to an aryl or TMS group, thus lacking generality.

Herein, we wish to report that after tremendous effort, a general and convenient procedure under mild reaction conditions, making use of Mn(OAc)3 as key catalyst to induce a sequential 5-exo-dig and 6-exo/endo cyclization process, has been experimentally realized, culminating in a number of cyclopenta[b]naphthalene derivatives 2 with a different functionality at C-4 position. This newly developed method may serve as a synthetic variant that complements the styrenyl DDA approach to provide structurally more diverse naphthalene fluorophores due to the easy removal of the cyano group α to the ketone carbonyl.

Results and Discussion

Compound 3 and related analogues 3a–h were readily prepared via a modified procedure of the Knoevenagel condensation using L-proline as base to effect the aldol condensation and Hantzsch ester as a reducing agent to reduce the α,β-unsaturated enones formed in situ (Scheme 3).5 Using compound 3 as an initial model, screening of reaction conditions was then carried out. The high-valent metal catalyst was set as the highest priority for testing since our previous experience had revealed that low-valent metals (e.g., CuI) failed to catalyze the tandem cyclization, but resulted in oxidative cleavage of the cyano group (.OCN) to form diketone 6 and the ketone carbonyl to give benzoic acid.6 As compiled in Table 1, various multivalent metals were attempted, leading to the finding that Mn(III) was the reagent of choice to induce the expected oxidative tandem cyclization in either a catalytic or stoichiometric manner.7 It is noteworthy that polar solvents usually applied for Mn(OAc)3, including AcOH, MeOH and EtOH, afforded the desired tricyclic product 4 in low yields (entries 1–4; 17–38%).

In contrast, when benzene was employed,8 product 4 was obtained in excellent yield (entry 5; 92%) in 1.5 h under similar reaction conditions, suggesting that strong solvation of Mn(III) with the polar solvent might take place, thereby weakening its chelating ability with the substrate enolate and retarding the single electron transfer (SET) process necessary for the redox cycle. Due to its high efficiency in benzene, an attempt to reduce the amount of Mn(OAc)3 to 20 mol% in the presence of AcOH (2 equiv.) was then made.8 Consequently, although only a slight decrease in yield (entry 6; 83% vs. entry 5; 92%) was observed, however, it took a much longer time (entry 6; 15 h vs. entry 5; 1.5 h) to complete conversion as compared to that using the stoichiometric amount. Again, when benzene was replaced with MeOH, a significant solvent effect was observed and the yield was reduced by ca. 30% (entry 7; 54% vs. entry 6; 83%). Other approaches using a high valent metal as catalyst, including Co(II), Fe(III) and Ce(IV),9–11 were also examined, but yields (entries 8–10; 25–72%) appeared to be inferior to those with Mn(III). Therefore, the reaction system [2 equiv. Mn(OAc)3/PhH/55 °C] was tentatively considered optimal, and thus applied to a series of structurally diverse esters 3a–h. As a result, the corresponding tricyclic products 11a–h were all produced in an expected manner in 58–93% yields (Table 2), thus demonstrating the effectiveness and generality of this newly developed method in the presence of the ester functionality. More encouragingly, when substrate 1 (R=TMS or Ph) was subjected to the same reaction conditions, tricyclic products 7 and 8 were smoothly obtained in 83% and 80% yields, respectively, indicating that this protocol should have wider applicability than the previous methods (a) and (b).

The success in converting the title system capped with an ester, TMS or aryl group on the alkyne terminus into the corresponding cyclopenta[b]naphthalene derivatives under Mn(OAc)3-mediated oxidation further motivated us to broaden the scope of substrates containing more different functionalities. Along these lines, as illustrated in Figure 1, substrates capped with amide (9a–f), ketone (9g–l), phosphonate (9m–o), and halogen (9p–r) groups were then prepared via a synthetic procedure similar to Scheme 3.4,12

Distinct from other substrates, substrates 9g–l were labile during chromatographic purification using silica gel, affording monocyclic products via a Conia-ene cyclization in an extremely effective manner as exemplified in Scheme 4.13 As listed in Table 2, treating substrates 3a–h and 9a–r with this newly developed tandem cyclization process gave the corresponding cyclopenta[b]naphthalene derivatives 11a–h and 12a–r, containing a diverse range of functionality at the C-4 position, in good to excellent yields (51–96%).

Also, X-ray crystallographic analyses of the representative products 4,14 12j,15 12m,[16] and 12p17 further confirmed the structural elucidation made by regular spectroscopic methods. Also emphasized is the fact that above isolated yields are obtained via a free-radical cascade of at least four chemical transformations, involving α proton abstraction, subsequent 5-exo-dig and 6-exo/endo cyclization followed by rearomatization.18 Therefore, even the lowest yield (12n; 51%) could be considered as being synthetically practical and useful. Compared to other capped functionalities, the ketone carbonyl, possessing a stronger electron-withdrawing nature and thus rendering the alkynyl unit a better Michael receptor for the α-cyano enolate, is believed to be responsible for the Conia-ene cyclization of all substrates 9g–l during chromatographic purification on either acidic or neutral silica gel. Although the formation of Conia-ene products is unfavorable, however, it provides strong support that cyclopenta[b]naphthalene derivatives 2 thus obtained are not the result of the intramolecular styrenyl DDA cycloaddition. Substrates capped with either a secondary or tertiary amide were found to undergo cyclization with equal ease and gave the corresponding products (e.g., 12a and 12b) in high yields, suggesting that the vinyl radical intermediate occurring in the first 5-exo-dig cyclization was apparently not quenched by the proton of the secondary amide. In general, the above Mn(III)-catalyzed tandem cyclization was little influenced by various functionalities capped on the acetylenic unit. These salient features might imply that the vinyl radical generated in the 5-exo-dig cyclization is supposed to have a strong σ-bonding rather than a weak π-bonding character with manganese(III) metal. This potential covalent characteristic might explain why the terminus functionality could be tolerated in such a wide fashion. Also intriguing is the finding that a higher electron-withdrawing character of the substituent on the phenyl ring resulted in higher yields compared to the substituent with a more electron-donating character [11a (93%) vs.­ 11e (70%); 12e (90%) vs.­ 12f (78%); 12i (76%) vs.­ 12h (61%); 12q (96%) vs.­ 12r (88%)], indicating that the 6-exo/endo cyclization of the vinyl radical to the electron-deficient phenyl ring is more favorable during the ring-closure step. For comparison purposes, when representative substrates 3a, 3b, 3d, 9a, 9b, 9c, 9i, 9k, 9m and 9p, individually capped with a different functional group, are subjected to catalytic reaction conditions instead (Table 1; entry 6), yields of the corresponding products 11a, 11b, 11d, 12a, 12b, 12c, 12i, 12k, 12m and 12p are found to be comparable to those under optimal conditions, but as expected, a longer reaction time is necessary in all cases examined as enclosed in parenthesis in Table 2. It is highly conceivable that cyclopenta[b]naphthalene products thus obtained will be useful for further synthetic elaboration due to the presence of multiple functionalities. For example, the α-cyano group could be readily replaced by a proton or alkyl group via reductive removal of the cyano group using lithiun naphthalenide (LN) at low temperature (−25 to −78 °C) followed by treatment with a protic solvent or an appropriate electrophile as demonstrated in Scheme 5 (a), in which the naphthalene fluorophore was restored after protonation.19 Similarly, those products containing a vinyl halogen unit (e.g., 12p–r) could undergo a variety of coupling reactions, such as Heck reaction, Suzuki–Miyaura reaction and Sonogashira reaction for further structural modifications as typified in Scheme 5 (b).20

A proposed mechanism for the manganese(III)-mediated oxidative cyclization process is depicted in Scheme 6 using substrate 3 as a typical example. The initial step is the loss of a proton α to the cyano to form the Mn(III) enolate which could undergo a rapid electron transfer with a concomitant loss of Mn(II) to form the radical intermediate A. This is followed by intramolecular 5-exo-dig addition to the alkyne unit to give the vinyl radical B. Mn(II) is recalled to form the Mn(III)-vinyl complex,21 whereby a subsequent 6-exo/endo cyclization will take place to give the cyclic pentadienyl radical C. Finally, Mn(III) could oxidize the pentadienyl radical to the corresponding tertiary cation, which in turn loses a proton to regenerate the aromatic system to afford product 4.18b

Conclusions

A convenient and general approach to cyclopenta[b]naphthalene derivatives containing a structurally diverse range of functional groups, including trimethyl silyl, aryl, ester, ketone, secondary/tertiary amide, phosphonate and halogen, at the C-4 position has been successfully developed, wherein Mn(III) plays a critical role to effect the oxidative tandem 5-exo-dig and 6-exo/endo cyclization efficiently. This newly developed protocol provides a milder approach to complement the styrenyl DDA reaction, in which high temperatures (160–300 °C) are usually required, and is expected to have broad synthetic utility in light of its operational simplicity, high product yields and the presence of the versatile functionality allowing for further synthetic elaboration. Mechanistically, an Mn(III)-vinyl intermediate with a strong covalent character is proposed for the methodology to rationalize why so many different functionalities capped on the alkyne terminus could be tolerated.

General Remarks

All reactions were performed under air unless otherwise stated. All solvents and reagents were employed as received without further purification. Analytical thin layer chromatography was performed on SiO2 60 F-254 plates and flash column chromatography was carried out using SiO2 60 (particle size 0.040–0.055 mm, 230–400 mesh), both of which are available from E.
