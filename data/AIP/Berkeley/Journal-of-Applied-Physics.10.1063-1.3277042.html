Abstract

Temperature-dependent modulation characteristics of 1.3 μm InAs/GaAs quantum dot (QD) lasers under small signals have been carefully studied at various bias currents. Based on experimental observations, it is found that the modulation bandwidth significantly increases when excited state (ES) lasing emerges at high temperature. This is attributed to additional photons emitted by ES lasing which contribute to the modulation response. A rate equation model including two discrete electron energy levels and the level of wetting layer has been used to investigate the temperature-dependent dynamic behavior of the QD lasers. Numerical investigations confirm that the significant jump for the small signal modulation response is indeed caused by ES photons. Furthermore, we identify how the electron occupation probabilities of the two discrete energy levels can influence the photon density of different states and finally the modulation rate. Both experiments and numerical analysis show that the modulation bandwidth of QD lasers at high temperature can be increased by injecting more carriers into the ES that has larger electron state degeneracy and faster carrier’s relaxation time than the ground state.

In this paper, we present the experimental and theoretical results on small-signal modulation responses of the 1.3 μm InAs/GaAs QD lasers at temperatures of 20, 50, and 60 °C under different bias currents. We first experimentally observed that the modulation bandwidth significantly increases when ES lasing emerges at high temperature. Then, we employed a theoretical rate equation model that includes two discrete electron energy levels of the QDs, the level of wetting layer as well as various carries relaxation paths to numerically investigate the phenomena observed experimentally. Temperature-dependent carrier lifetime is added to the rate equation to demonstrate the influence of temperature on the modulation characteristics. The theoretical analysis agrees well with the experimental observations. The results presented in this paper show a viable way to improve the dynamic characteristics of QD lasers when the single wavelength operation is not highly demanded.

II. EXPERIMENTS

The QD lasers that contain a 1.4 μm n-type Al0.4Ga0.6As lower cladding layer, an active region, and another corresponding p-type upper cladding layer were grown on Si-doped (100) GaAs substrates by molecular beam epitaxy. A 200-nm-thick p-type doped GaAs layer was used at last as the contact layer. The active region consists of five-stacked InAs QD layers, each of which is a similar recipe containing 2.3 ML QDs of InAs, followed by a 4 nm In0.17Ga0.83As strain-reducing layer and then a 40 nm GaAs barrier layer. The areal density of each QD layer is approximately 3×1010 cm−2. Stripe-type lasers with 4 μm width and 600 μm length were fabricated by standard lithography and wet etching. The facets were coated with reflectivity of 60% and 95%, respectively.
Figures 1(a)–1(c) compare the small-signal responses as a function of modulation frequency under different bias currents, measured at the temperatures of 20, 50, and 60 °C, respectively. Insets (d), (e), and (f) in Figs. 1(a)–1(c) show the injected current dependence of 3 dB bandwidth corresponding to the above three different temperatures. In the inset (d), the 3 dB bandwidth increases gradually from 3.6 to 4.94 GHz when the bias current varies from 20 to 60 mA. If the bias current is kept on further increasing, the 3 dB bandwidth reaches saturation. However, it is quite different for the situation at 50 °C [see the inset (e) in Fig. 1(b)]. At the beginning (the bias current under 70 mA), the modulation response shows the same rule as that at 20 °C. But for the bias currents of 80, 90, and 100 mA, the modulation response increases dramatically and the 3 dB bandwidth is significantly enlarged. This is beneficial to high-speed modulation used in optical communication systems. At 60 °C, the phenomenon is the same and the only difference is that the bias current for this significant increase in the 3 dB bandwidth decreases to 60 mA. In addition, as shown in the inset (f), the 3 dB bandwidth saturates at the bias currents of 40 and 90 mA, respectively.

III. THEORETICAL MODEL

Aiming at the influence of ES lasing on the 3 dB bandwidth at high temperature, an effective model for the QD lasers with two discrete electron energy levels of the QDs and the level of wetting layer is assumed to simulate the modulation response curve. The relaxation time of holes is much faster than that of electrons so that the radiative recombination of electrons and holes are mainly determined by electrons.99. A. Markus, J. X. Chen, O. Gauthier-Lafaye, J. -G. Provost, C. Paranthoën, and A. Fiore, IEEE J. Sel. Top. Quantum Electron. 9, 1308 (2003). https://doi.org/10.1109/JSTQE.2003.819494 The dynamic process of elections is schematically illustrated in Fig. 3. Electrons are injected into the wetting layer by forwarding bias current and captured by ES, then they quickly relax to the GS where they reside for recombination. Other high electron energy levels and relaxation paths as well as influence caused by inhomogeneous broadening are ignored for simplicity. The degeneracy of the GS (p0) and ES (p1) are 2 and 4, respectively, while that of the wetting layer (pw) is assumed to be much larger, indicating that it is always empty for electron in the wetting layer.

In these equations, n0, n1, and nw are the carrier’s areal densities in the ground, ES of the QD, and the wetting layer, respectively; s0 and s1 denote total photon population of ground and ESs; tsp is the lifetime of spontaneous emission; Nd is total areal dot density; g0 and g1 are the maximum modal gains for ground and ESs of the QDs laser; W and L are the width and length of the lasers; and ng is the refractive index of the active region; α is the total loss and can be expressed as
α=αi+12Lln1RfRb.(6)
Here αi is the internal loss, and Rf and Rb are the reflectivity of forward and backward facets. tij is the time constant, where the subscripts i, j=0, 1, and w, which represent the GS, ES, and wetting layer, respectively. tij can be expressed as
tij=1Rij,(7)
where Rij is dependent on temperatures and described in details in Ref. 1010. C. Z. Tong, S. F. Yoon, C. Y. Ngo, C. Y. Liu, and W. K. Loke, IEEE J. Quantum Electron. 42, 1175 (2006). https://doi.org/10.1109/JQE.2006.883471. Here, we set tw1 to be 1 ps and t10 to be 2 ps in the simulation. To deal with thermal effects, thermal escapes of electrons from the ES to the wetting and from the GS to the ES are added to the rate equations in the form of t1w and t01, respectively. They are related to corresponding relaxation times and degeneracy of the initial and final states for transition. In addition, an exponential relationship between thermal escape time and relevant relaxation time is established, which can be expressed by the following two equations:
t01=p0p1t10 exp(E10kBT),(8)
t1w=p1pwtw1 exp(Ew1kBT),(9)
where E10 and Ew1 represent transition energies of electrons from the ES to the GS and from wetting layer to the ES, respectively. pw is approximately calculated considering the wetting layer as a finitely deep quantum well
pw=meffkBTNDπℏ2.(10)

The time-domain solutions of the rate equations are solved by Runge–Kutta method. Figures 4(a) and 4(b) show the calculated electron occupation probability and photon population of GS and ES as a function of injected current at three typical temperatures, respectively. It can be seen from the Fig. 4(a) that the electron occupation probability of the GS at low temperature increases faster than that at high temperature under the injected current lower than the threshold current of the GS. This is because of the slowed electron thermal escape rate from the GS to upper state levels, indicating larger electron confinement in the GS at low temperature and resulting in the lower threshold current for GS lasing. The electron occupation probability of the GS clamps at the maximum value of ∼0.72 and approximately remains constant at all temperatures when the injected current is larger than the threshold current of the GS. Excess carriers induced by the injected current larger than the threshold current of the GS is consumed by emitting GS photons and increasing the electron occupation probability of the ES. Photons of the GS saturate when the ES begins to lasing. In Fig. 4(b), ES has the largest electron occupation probability at 60 °C as the injected current is between the threshold currents of the GS and ES. This is because under the increased electron thermal escape rate at high temperature, less occupied GS needs larger injected current to satisfy population inversion, as a result, the ES is more occupied correspondingly. The increase in the ES electron occupation probability leads to the population inversion first for the ES when injecting enough carriers.

In Fig. 6(a), the condition for population conversion of ES can be hardly satisfied even at the injected current of 100 mA, as a result, only the GS photons contribute to the modulation response. As the static photon density of the GS in the laser diode cavity increases by increasing the injected current, the relaxation oscillation frequency increases, which causes the increase in modulation bandwidth. This is why the 3 dB bandwidth in Fig. 6(a) is improved at first as increasing injected current. and

Here, tp is the cavity photon lifetime and tcap is the relaxation time from the wetting layer to the lasing state. For the GS lasing, tcap is the sum of tw1 and t10. In Fig. 6(b), the ES begins to lasing at the bias current of 90 mA and photons of the ES also contribute to the modulation response. A new expression of the maximum 3 dB bandwidth is derived to deal with ES lasing considering carrier relaxation from the ES to the GS (t10),
f3 dB=12π(1−K4π2t10)+(1−K4π2t10)2+(K4π2t10)2(K4π2)2.(13)
In particular, in the case of only ES lasing (t10→∞), Eq. (13) can be also simplified into Eq. (11). Limited relaxation time from the ES to the GS reduces the 3dB bandwidth to some extent by means of consuming carriers that are otherwise used for ES lasing. However, shorter relaxation time (tcap) for the ES lasing than that for the GS lasing plays a decisive role, resulting in significant increase in the 3dB bandwidth. IV. CONCLUSIONS

Temperature-dependent modulation characteristics for 1.3 μm InAs/GaAs QD lasers have been investigated. The experimental small signal bandwidth is observed under various bias currents at different temperatures. An idealized model for the QD lasers is used to interpret the experimental modulation response. Both experimental observations and theoretical analysis demonstrated that ES lasing can increase the 3 dB bandwidth of QD lasers at high temperature. This phenomenon shows significant potential in improving the dynamic characteristics of the lasers.
