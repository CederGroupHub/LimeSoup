Abstract

Transparent organic light emitting diodes were developed by using a thermally evaporable WO3∕Ag∕WO3 (WAW) as a transparent cathode. A thin Ag layer was introduced as an interlayer between the Li doped electron transport layer and the WAW electrode. A high transparency over 80% was obtained and electron injection was greatly improved by using the thin Ag interlayer between the Li doped layer and the WAW electrode. The driving voltage at 1000cd∕m2 was only 4.5V and the sheet resistance of the WAW electrode was as low as 12Ω∕◻.
  • Original article: APL: Organic Electronics and Photonics 1 (7), 241 (2008)
    • In recent years, transparent organic light emitting diodes (TOLEDs) have gained considerable interest because they can emit light through both anode and cathode. Transparent anodes and cathodes are required to fabricate TOLEDs, and therefore there have been many studies focused on the development of transparent cathode for TOLEDs.
    The most common transparent cathode material is Mg:Ag and it has good electron injection properties. However, it should be used as a thin layer due to its low transmittance and it suffers from high resistance.1,21. H. Riel, S. Karg, T. Beierlein, B. Ruhstaller, and W. Rieß, Appl. Phys. Lett. https://doi.org/10.1063/1.1537052 82, 466 (2003).2. M.-H. Lu, M. S. Weaver, T. X. Zhou, M. Rothman, R. C. Kwong, M. Hack, and J. J. Brown, Appl. Phys. Lett. https://doi.org/10.1063/1.1523150 81, 3921 (2002). Therefore, a Mg:Ag/ITO semitransparent cathode structure was developed to improve the high resistance of the Mg:Ag, but it has a problem of sputtering damage because high energy indium tin oxide (ITO) sputtering process degrades the Mg:Ag and the organic layer underneath.1,21. H. Riel, S. Karg, T. Beierlein, B. Ruhstaller, and W. Rieß, Appl. Phys. Lett. https://doi.org/10.1063/1.1537052 82, 466 (2003).2. M.-H. Lu, M. S. Weaver, T. X. Zhou, M. Rothman, R. C. Kwong, M. In other research, pure ITO was sputtered on an organic layer and efficient electron injection was observed.3–83. L. S. Hung and C. W. Tang, Appl. Phys. Lett. https://doi.org/10.1063/1.124107 74, 3209 (1999).4. G. Parthasarathy, P. E. Burrows, V. Khalfin, V. G. Kozlov, and S. R. Forrest, Appl. Phys. Phys. https://doi.org/10.1063/1.1359161 89, 4986 (2001). However, this type of cathode structure has a negative effect on the device stability due to the sputtering damage and it is not useful as a cathode structure for TOLEDs.
    Double layer cathode structures of Ca∕Ag and Ba∕Ag have also been reported. They showed a high transmittance of around 70% and good electron injection performances.9,109. R. B. Pode, C. J. Lee, D. G. Moon, and J. I. Han, Appl. Phys. Lett. https://doi.org/10.1063/1.1756674 84, 4614 (2004).10. R. B. Pode, C. J. Lee, D. G. Moon, and J. I. Han, Appl. Phys. Lett. https://doi.org/10.1063/1.2355464 89, 123501 (2006). However, they were applied as a thin layer and high resistance was still a problem. An ITO/Ag/ITO-based cathode structure has also been developed. It showed moderate performances as a transparent cathode. However, the ITO layer had to be deposited by e-beam deposition or sputtering, which negatively affected the performances of thin film transistors and OLEDs.1111. S. Y. Ryu, S. J. Jo, C. S. Kim, S. H. Choi, J. H. Noh, H. K. Baik, and H. S. Jeong, Appl. Phys. Lett. https://doi.org/10.1063/1.2776347 91, 093515 (2007). In addition, the ITO film fabricated by the e-beam process still had a high resistance. Therefore, a thermally evaporable, low resistance, transparent cathode structure has to be developed to overcome the problems of the current transparent cathodes.
    Recently, a WO3∕Ag∕WO3 (WAW) was developed as a low resistance electrode for OLEDs. It showed a high transmittance of around 90% and a low resistance of less than 10Ω∕◻.1212. S. Y. Ryu, J. H. Noh, B. H. Hwang, C. S. Kim, S. J. Jo, J. T. Kim, H. S. Hwang, H. K. Baik, H. S. Jeong, C. H. Lee, S. Y. Song, S. H. Choi, and S. Y. Park, Appl. Phys. Lett. https://doi.org/10.1063/1.2835044 92, 023306 (2008). However, it could not be used as a cathode due to poor electron injection properties of the WO3 and electron beam deposition process, which is not compatible with thin film transistor. Therefore, an efficient electron injection structure should be combined with a thermally deposited WAW structure for use as a transparent cathode.
    In this work, a cathode structure based on WAW has been developed using a Li doped electron transport layer and a thin Ag layer as an electron injection structure. The electron injection can be improved without a large sacrifice of transmittance of the WAW structure by using a thin Ag layer because a high transmittance Ag layer was inserted as a thin layer (1nm). The device performances of the TOLEDs having the WAW -based cathodes were correlated with the electrode structure.
    A basic electrode structure of WO3(40mm)∕Ag(14nm)∕WO3(40nm) was used in the TOLEDs after optimizing the thickness of the WAW electrodes. The device configuration of the TOLEDs was (ITO,150nm)∕N,N′-diphenyl-N,N′-bis-[4-(phenyl-m-tolyl-amino)-phenyl]-biphenyl-4,4′-diamine (60nm)∕N,N′-di(1-naphthyl)-N,N′-diphenylbenzidine (NPB,30nm)∕4,4′,4″-tris(N-carbazolyl)triphenylamine:1, 3, 5-tris(N-phenylbenzimidazole-2-yl)benzene:tris (2-phenylpyridine) iridium (30nm, 50:50:5)/4,7-diphenyl-1,10-phenanthroline (Bphen) (10nm)/Bphen:Li (20nm, 30mol%)/Ag (xnm)/WAW. The Ag interlayer was incorporated to ensure efficient electron injection. The effect of varying the thickness of the Ag layer between 0 and 1nm on the device performance of the TOLED was investigated. All materials were deposited by vacuum thermal evaporation. Current density-voltage-luminance characteristics and electroluminescence (EL) spectra of the TOLEDs were measured using a Keithley 2400 source measurement unit and a CS 1000 spectrophotometer.
    A WAW electrode structure has been known to have a high transparency and low sheet resistance due to an optical effect in the WAW structure and the low resistance of the Ag layer, respectively.1212. S. Y. Ryu, J. H. Noh, B. H. Hwang, C. S. Kim, S. J. Jo, J. T. Kim, H. S. Hwang, H. K. Baik, H. S. Jeong, C. H. Lee, S. Y. Song, S. H. Choi, and S. Y. Park, Appl. Phys. Lett. https://doi.org/10.1063/1.2835044 92, 023306 (2008). A WAW electrode can be used as an anode for OLEDs and effective hole injection from the WO3 to the hole transport layer has been reported.1313. J. Meyer, S. Hamwi, T. Bülow, H.-H. Johannes, T. Riedl, and W. Kowalsky, Appl. Phys. Lett. https://doi.org/10.1063/1.2784176 91, 113506 (2007). However, electron injection from the WAW was found to be inefficient. This may be due to the formation of a depletion region between the n doped organic layer and the WO3.1414. T. Tsutsui and M. Terai, Appl. Phys. Lett. https://doi.org/10.1063/1.1640470 84, 440 (2004). Therefore, an electron injection layer with good electron injection properties and a high transmittance should be combined with the WAW for use as a transparent cathode.
    Figure 1 shows the transmittance of the WAW and Ag (1nm)/WAW electrodes. It can be seen that a high transmittance of over 80% was obtained for the WAW-based transparent electrode. The addition of the Ag electron injection layer did not greatly decrease the transmittance of the transparent electrode. For instance, the transmittance of the WAW electrode with a 1nm thick Ag layer was 80% at 500nm because of high transmittance of 1nm thick Ag in electron injection layer. In addition, the sheet resistance of the WAW electrode was 12Ω∕◻. Therefore, it can be expected that a WAW electrode combined with a Ag interlayer can be used as a low resistance transparent cathode for TOLEDs.

    The dependence of the device performances of the TOLEDs having a WAW cathode on the thickness of the Ag layer was investigated. The resulting current density-voltage curves are plotted in Fig. 2. The current density of the WAW-based device was high in the device that had a 1nm thick Ag interlayer between the electron transport layer and the WAW electrode, while a rather low current density was obtained in the WAW device that had no Ag interlayer. It is well known that electron injection can be improved by using an alkali metal doped electron injection structure due to interfacial energy barrier lowering between the n doped layer and metal electrodes.1515. J. Kido and T. Matsumoto, Appl. Phys. Lett. https://doi.org/10.1063/1.122612 73, 2866 (1998). The Li doped Bphen layer could effectively work as an electron transport layer for efficient electron injection. However, the electron injection from the electrode to the n doped layer depends on the interfacial contact between the n doped layer and the cathode. In the case of the Li doped Bphen layer and WO3 case, the formation of a depletion zone may have a negative effect on electron injection.1414. T. Tsutsui and M. Terai, Appl. Phys. Lett. https://doi.org/10.1063/1.1640470 84, 440 (2004). A stack of a n doped electron transport layer and a metal oxide such as WO3 or V2O5 could be used as a charge generation layer in tandem devices. Charge sprouting and separation at the interface between the n doped layer and the metal oxide hinders electron injection from the WO3 to the n doped electron injection layer. The energy barrier for electron injection is increased at the Bphen:Li∕WO3 interface if a negative bias is applied to the WO3. This results in a low current density in a positive bias region. The limited electron injection in a WO3-based device can be improved by inserting a thin Ag layer that can form an Ohmic contact with the n doped electron transport layer. Therefore, the current injection was considerably improved by the presence of the Ag interlayer between the n doped layer and the WAW electrode.

    The luminance of the TOLEDs having the WAW electrode is shown in Fig. 3. The luminance values of the TOLEDs through the anode and cathode were plotted against voltage. The WAW device with the Ag interlayer showed a much higher luminance than the WAW device without the Ag layer. In general, luminance is determined by the current density and the recombination efficiency. A high current density and high recombination efficiency are favorable for high luminance and the high luminance in the WAW device with the Ag interlayer is due to the high current density. The luminance through the anode side was higher than that through the cathode side. While ITO has a transmittance of 90%, the WAW electrode has a transmittance of 82%. In addition, light emission can be reflected due to the Ag layer between the WO3 layers, resulting in a high luminance though the ITO side. The relative ratio of the luminance through the ITO and WAW electrode was 2.1 in the case of the WAW device with the Ag layer, while it was 1.9 in the WAW device without the Ag layer. The rather low transmittance of the Ag/WAW electrode increased the relative ratio of the luminance through the anode and the cathode. The total quantum efficiencies of the two devices were similar to each other (data is hot shown here). Even though current injection was retarded in the WAW device, the charge balance was not greatly affected because the n doped electron transport layer was used. The quantum efficiencies of the WAW and Ag/WAW devices were 8.1% and 9.0%, respectively.

    The EL spectra of the TOLEDs through the anode and cathode are compared in Fig. 4. The peak maximum was the same in all of the EL spectra, but the shoulder peak was strengthened in the EL spectra through the anode side. The light emission through the anode is the sum of the direct light emission from the light emitting layer and the reflected emission by the cathode, while the light emission through the cathode side mostly comes directly out of the emitting layer. Therefore, different EL spectra were obtained through the anode and the cathode.

    In summary, a thermally evaporable WAW electrode could be used as a low resistance transparent cathode for TOLEDs. Electron injection was improved by using Ag as an interlayer between the Li doped electron transport layer and the WAW electrode. A low driving voltage of 4.5V at 1000cd∕m2 was obtained in a green phosphorescent device using the WAW electrode with a thin Ag interlayer. In addition, a high transmittance of 80% and a low resistance of 12Ω∕◻ were obtained. Therefore, the WAW-based cathode structure can be used as a low resistance transparent cathode for TOLEDs or top emitting OLEDs in practical applications.
