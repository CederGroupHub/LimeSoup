Abstract

A cost-coupled life cycle environmental assessment was implemented to estimate the economic and environmental impact of corn stalk-based ethanol production. Corn-based ethanol production is used as a control group in this study. For both scenarios, the effects of respiratory inorganics, global warming, and non-renewable energy categories have important contributions to the overall environmental impact related to chemical production, coal-based energy generation, or diesel consumption for road transport stages. Corn stalk-based ethanol production is environmentally friendlier than corn-based ethanol production in the dominant categories of respiratory inorganics, global warming, non-renewable energy, carcinogens, land occupation, and terrestrial acidification/nutrification. However, producing corn stalk-based ethanol production is uneconomical because of the relatively high cost of enzymes. In addition, increasing corn stalk consumption efficiencies, decreasing stalk transport distances, and electing natural gas-fired energy-generation (e.g., electricity, steam) technologies are highly recommended for reducing adverse environment or economic effects.

I. INTRODUCTION

China is rich in grain stalk (e.g., rice, wheat, corn) resources, which accounts for 20%–30% of grain stalk resources in the world (Zhao et al., 201138. Zhao, J. N., Zhang, G. L., and Yang, D. L., “ Estimation of carbon emission from burning of grain crop residues in China,” J. Agro-Environ. Sci. 30(4), 812–816 (2011) (in Chinese).). Grain residues in China have grown proportionally with the increasing demand for food because of vast population growth. For instance, about 4.40 × 108 and 5.64 × 108 t of grain straw produced in China was reported in 2003 and 2008, respectively (Zhao et al., 201138. Zhao, J. N., Zhang, G. L., and Yang, D. L., “ Estimation of carbon emission from burning of grain crop residues in China,” J. Agro-Environ. Sci. 30(4), 812–816 (2011) (in Chinese).), corresponding to an average annual increase of 4.4%. The increasing amount of grain residues is a critical environmental problem in China (Zhang, 200939. Zhang, H. F., “ A laboratory study on emission characteristics of gaseous and particulate pollutants emitted from agricultural crop residue burning in China,” Ph.D. dissertation, Fudan University, 2009 (in Chinese).) because of grain residue burning by Chinese farmers after harvest (Feng et al., 20112. Feng, W., Zhang, L. Q., Pang, Z. W., and Guo, S. Z., “ The economic and environmental analysis of crop residues burning and reutilization in China,” Chin. Agric. Sci. Bull. 27(6), 350–354 (2011) (in Chinese).). In addition, China is facing an energy crisis because of the rapid depletion of fossil energy resources (e.g., natural gas, coal, oil) owing to rapid industrial development and economic growth (Wang et al., 201135. Wang, S. J., Yuan, P., Li, D., and Jiao, Y. H., “ An overview of ocean renewable energy in China,” Renewable Sustainable Energy Rev. 15(1), 91–111 (2011). https://doi.org/10.1016/j.rser.2010.09.040). Thus, the Chinese government is increasingly turning its attention to renewable energy sources such as bioethanol. Straws for cellulosic ethanol production have been encouraged for use as an alternative to grain-based energy production, which is limited by the Energy Independence and Security Act because of problems concerning food prices and land usage (Sims et al., 201029. Sims, R. E. H., Mabee, W., Saddler, J. N., and Taylor, M., “ An overview of second generation biofuel technologies,” Bioresour. Technol. 101(6), 1570–1580 (2010). https://doi.org/10.1016/j.biortech.2009.11.046 and Gaffigan, 20097. Gaffigan, M. E., “ Biofuels potential effects and challenges of required increases in production and use,” Report No. GAO-09-446, United State Government Accountability Office, Washington, USA, 2009.). However, grain residue-based ethanol production may have additional environmental or economic impacts because of the huge energy consumption during stalk transport and the high cost of cellulase. Accordingly, reliable environmental and economic assessments must be conducted to identify the environmental and economic impact of grain residue-based ethanol production.
Cost-coupled life cycle assessment (LCA) is well known as a technology for assessing the economic and environmental performance associated with all the stages of a product, process, and activity from cradle to grave. Thus, bio-based products cannot necessarily be applied to cellulosic ethanol production in China. Moreover, China is known as one of the largest energy consumers and greenhouse gases emitters in the world. Accordingly, understanding the environmental implications of grain residue-based ethanol production in China and assessing its improvement potential are important for China's developmental strategy and for that of the global community. Furthermore, limited cost-combined LCAs for grain residue-based ethanol production have been reported in the world. Only one LCA study on grain residue-based ethanol production has been reported in China (Tian et al., 201134. Tian, W., Liao, C. P., Li, L., and Zhao, D. The environmental and economic impact of grain residue-based ethanol production in China is assessed in the current study. Corn stalk is used as the model grain residue because it is the most dominant grain residue in China (Zhang, 200939. Zhang, H. F., “ A laboratory study on emission characteristics of gaseous and particulate pollutants emitted from agricultural crop residue burning in China,” Ph.D. dissertation, Fudan University, 2009 (in Chinese).). J., Xiang, W. D., Zhou, T., and Lei, J. S., “ Analysis on current status and tendency of fuel ethanol in China,” Energy Chin. 31(1), 31–33 (2009) (in Chinese).).

B. System boundaries
System boundaries are set using a cradle-to-gate approach. Two scenarios are considered in this study: corn stalk and corn. Both scenarios involve road transport, coal-based energy generation, ethanol production, and waste disposal processes. However, the infrastructure for each scenario is excluded because of the lack of detailed information on the present ethanol production site. Additional processes included in the corn stalk scenario are corn stalk collection, stalk compression, stalk pulverization, and energy recovery from lignin residues. For the corn scenario, additional processes are corn production at farm, corn degermination, and machinery drying. In addition, the end life cycle treatment process of open stalk burning is considered in the corn scenario because stalk burning serves a vital function in grain residue treatment in China (Feng et al., 20112. Feng, W., Zhang, L. Q., Pang, Z. W., and Guo, S. Z., “ The economic and environmental analysis of crop residues burning and reutilization in China,” Chin. Agric. Sci. Bull. 27(6), 350–354 (2011) (in Chinese).). Figure 1 presents the system boundary of this study. Mass allocation regarding ethanol production is performed in this study. As shown in Fig. 1, during the corn stalk-based ethanol production process, the final products are 0.64 t of Dried grains and solubles (DDGS) and 1 t of ethanol. Mass allocation is performed to allocate inputs for ethanol between the two co-products, i.e., 39.0% to DDGS and 61.0% to ethanol. Thus, all inputs for corn stalk are multiplied by 0.61 and used as the inputs for 4.6 t corn stalk production. During the corn-based ethanol production process, the final products are 0.92 t of DDGS and 1 t of ethanol. Therefore, mass allocation is performed relative to output products, with 52% of inputs allocated to degerminated corn production. By contrast, allocations are avoided regarding farming inputs because corn stalk is considered as an agricultural waste traditionally burned by Chinese farmers after harvests (Feng et al., 20112. Feng, W., Zhang, L. Q., Pang, Z. W., and Guo, S. Z., “ The economic and environmental analysis of crop residues burning and reutilization in China,” Chin. Agric. Sci. Bull. 27(6), 350–354 (2011) (in Chinese).). Carbon dioxide emissions from ethanol production processes are omitted from the inventory because corn and corn stalk are considered a biogenic source. C. Methodology
The life cycle impact assessment (LCIA) results are calculated at midpoint and endpoint level using the IMPACT2002+ method (Jolliet et al., 200315. Jolliet, O., Margni, M., Charles, R., Humbert, S., Payet, J., Rebitze, G., and Rosenbaum, R., “ IMPACT2002+: A new life cycle impact assessment methodology,” Int. J. Life Cycle Assess. 8, 324–330 (2003). https://doi.org/10.1007/BF02978505), which is one of mostly used method in LCA. The IMPACT2002+ method defines 15 mid-point categories (i.e., carcinogens, non-carcinogens, respiratory inorganics, ionizing radiation, ozone layer depletion, respiratory organics, aquatic ecotoxicity, terrestrial ecotoxicity, terrestrial acidification/nutrification, land occupation, aquatic acidification, aquatic eutrophication, global warming, non-renewable energy, and mineral extraction) and 4 end-point categories (i.e., human health, ecosystem quality, climate change, and resources). To compare the mid-point impact to each other as well and to analyze the respective share of each mid-point impact to the overall impact, normalization which is determined by the ratio of the impact per unit of emission divided by the total impact generated from European emissions per person per year was applied in this study (Jolliet et al., 2003). In addition, ReCiPe midpoint (E) method (Goedkoop et al., 20096. Goedkoop, M., Heijungs, R., Huijbregts, M., De Schryver, A., Struijs, J., and Van Zelm, R., “ A life cycle impact assessment method which comprises harmonized category indicators at the mid-point and the end-point level,” First edition, Report No. I, 2009.), which is the most recent indicator approach available in LCA analysis, is used as a comparison to complement IMPACT2002+ and check the robustness of the obtained results from IMPACT2002+. These two methods were chosen because the IMPACT2002+ and ReCiPe methods provide a rapid and transparent tool for toxicity assessment. For both methods, characterization factors in each category are defined based on relevant environmental mechanism, which generally involves environmental fate, effect, and damage. For the toxicity categories, additional stage of exposure is considered. Multi-media fate (i.e., industry air, urban air, rural air, freshwater, sea, agricultural soil, and natural soil) and multi-pathway (i.e., air, drinking water, leaf and root crops, meat, dairy products, and fish) models are used to identify the environmental exposure of pollutants. The intrinsic toxicity observed from laboratory studies based on animal experiments is then used to quantify the effects. Notably, human toxic effects in the IMPACT2002+ model were determined based on ED10 (benchmark dose resulting in 10% human risk of an incidence above background), whereas that in the ReCiPe model were determined based on ED50 (benchmark dose resulting in 50% human risk of an incidence above background). For eco-toxicity effects, HC50 (the dose affecting 50% of the species) of pollutants were used in both methods. The detailed methodology and complete characterization factors for IMPACT2002+ and ReCiPe are available on the website of the Risk Science Center in University of Michigan27. Risk Science Center, Michigan University, Michigan, USA, see www.sph.umich.edu/riskcenter/jolliet/impact2002+.htm (last accessed May 7, 2014). and the Institute of Environmental Science in Leiden University of Nederland33. The Institute of Environmental Science (CML), Leiden University, Nederland, see www.cml.leiden.edu/research/industrialecology/researchprojects/finished/recipe.html (last accessed May 7, 2014)., respectively.
The production costs of target scenarios are assessed by life cycle costing (LCC) method, which is an economic method for evaluating the economic burden associated with all stages of a product, process, or activity. LCC is similar to LCA but considers economic impacts instead of environmental impacts (Hong et al., 200910. Hong, J. L., Hong, J. M., Otaki, M., and Olivier, J., “ Environmental and economic life cycle assessment for sewage sludge treatment processes in Japan,” Waste Manage. 29(2), 696–703 (2009). https://doi.org/10.1016/j.wasman.2008.03.026 and Hong et al., 201211. Hong, J. M., Zhou, J., Hong, J. L., and Xu, X., “ Environmental and economic life cycle assessment of aluminum-silicon alloys production: A case study in China,” J. Cleaner Prod. 24, 11–19 (2012). https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jclepro.2011.11.003). Production costs of each life cycle stage contained in the system boundaries involve raw materials costs, energy costs, transportation costs, and waste disposal costs. The costs of bio-based ethanol consumption and its final disposal are excluded because the system boundaries are set using a cradle-to-gate approach. In addition, labor and overhead costs were excluded because of the lack of detailed information on the studied ethanol production site. Moreover, labor and overhead costs provide a minimal overall contribution to bioethanol ex-refinery price (Silalertruksa et al., 201228. Silalertruksa, T., Bonnet, S., and Gheewala, S. H., “ Life cycle costing and externalities of palm oil biodiesel in Thailand,” J. Cleaner Prod. 28, 225–232 (2012). https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jclepro.2011.07.022). For each scenario, the life cycle inventory is listed, and then combined with cost database for LCC assessment and environment database for LCA analysis. Prices of raw materials, energy, and waste disposal based on current Chinese market prices and exchange rates on 14 March 2014 (USD 1.00 = 6.14 CNY) are used in the present study.
Notably, polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs) emitted from open corn stalk burning are the most dominant substances that contribute to carcinogens (92%) during corn scenario. Thus, PAH characterization factors need to be calculated based on the corn stalk burning situation in China. D. Inventory data sources
In this study, all primary inventory data is taken from China, whereas the background inventory data including chemical production and wastewater treatment from Europe (Ecoinvent database, Frischknecht et al., 20074. Frischknecht, R. S., Jungbluth, N., Althaus, H. J., Doka, G., Dones, R., Hellweg, S., Hischier, R., Humber, S., Margni, M., Nemecek, T., and Spielmann, M., “ Implementation of life cycle impact assessment methods,” Report No. 3, Final Report Ecoinvent Swiss Centre for Life Cycle Inventories, Dubendorf, Switzerland, 2007.) were used to compensate for the lack of data in China. The reason that the authors used European background data is because no regionalized process-based database has been developed in China. Most information was obtained from the national and regional statistical data in which many important factors are not included and have low levels of quantification. Thus, the key factors (e.g., substance, process, impact category) are difficult to indentify. To reduce the regionalized effect from the Europe, Chinese data on coal-based electricity generation and road transport are used to adapt European data to the Chinese situation. Based on the data from China, operation data (i.e., energy, chemicals, water, raw material, product, and byproducts) in a corn-based ethanol production site, which is one of the largest and most advanced grain-based fuel ethanol production enterprises in China, is used in the present study. The annual actual production of ethanol in a corn-based ethanol production site in 2010 was approximately 370 000 t, which accounted for approximately 20% of the total fuel bio-ethanol production in China. For the corn stalk-based ethanol scenario, a mini-plant in East China University of Science and Technology is used in this study (Zhang, 201141. Zhang, H. Q., “ The process simulation of cellulosic ethanol production from corn stover based on the Aspen plus technology,” M.S. dissertation, East China University of Science and Technology, 2011 (in Chinese). and Zhang et al., 201142. Zhang, H. Q., Qiao, Q. A., Zhang, J., Zhang, M. F., and Bao, J., “ Flowsheet simulation of cellulosic ethanol mini-plant on Aspen plus platform,” J. East China Univ. Sci. Technol. (Nat. Sci. Ed.) 37(6), 697–705 (2011) (in Chinese).), because cellulosic ethanol production is a young and immature industry. No industrial scale cellulosic ethanol plant is currently built in China. The capacity for corn stalk-based ethanol production of the mini-plant is 1 kg/d. Data from coal-based electricity generation (Cui et al., 20121. Cui, X. W., Hong, J. L., and Gao, M. M., “ Environmental impact assessment of three coal-based electricity generation scenarios in China,” Energy 45(1), 952–959 (2012). https://doi.org/10.1016/j.energy.2012.06.063), corn production at a farm (Liu, 201019. Liu, W. Q., “ Investigation and assessment on major crop's energy consumption of Changyi city in Shandong,” M.S. dissertation, Chinese Academy of Agricultural Sciences, 2010 (in Chinese).), direct emissions (i.e., particulates, PAHs, CO, NOx, and NO2) from corn stalk burning (Zhang, 200939. Zhang, H. F., “ A laboratory study on emission characteristics of gaseous and particulate pollutants emitted from agricultural crop residue burning in China,” Ph.D. dissertation, Fudan University, 2009 (in Chinese).), and road transport in China (Ma et al., 200622. Ma, L. P., Wang, Z. H., Gong, X. Z., and Nie, Z. R., “ Life cycle inventory of two types of freight transport on city roads,” in Conference of Chinese Materials Research, Beijing, China, 25–30 June 2006, pp. 3–8 (in Chinese).) are taken from the references. However, biogenic CO2 emissions from both scenarios are omitted.

III. RESULTS

A. Midpoint
Table IV presents the mid-point assessment results using the IMPACT2002+ method. The corn scenario has high potential impact in most categories except for the ionizing radiation, ozone layer depletion, respiratory organics, terrestrial ecotoxicity, and mineral extraction categories. For both scenarios, the processes of chemical production, coal-based energy generation, or diesel consumption for road transport represent an important function in the overall environmental burden. The direct emissions (e.g., PAHs, particles, CO, NOx) from the stalk burning process serve an additional dominant contribution to carcinogens, non-carcinogens, and respiratory inorganic scores, whereas the process of land use for corn production has a dominant contribution to the land occupation category. To confirm and verify the robustness of the obtained results from IMPACT2002+, the ReCiPe method is used for comparison with IMPACT2002+. In the ozone layer depletion, acidification, and eutrophication categories, similar mid-point assessment results between the ReCiPe and IMPACT2002+ analysis are observed. The LCIA result of respiratory inorganics with the ReCiPe method is higher than the result obtained using the IMPACT 2002+ method. This finding is caused by the PM10 (particulate matter with a diameter smaller than 10 μm) and PM2.5–10, which the ReCiPe method considers to both have the same effect as PM2.5. For global warming, the LCIA result of the corn with stalk burning using the IMPACT2002+ method (1.53 × 103 kg CO2 eq) is higher than the result using the ReCiPe method (1.14 × 103 kg CO2 eq). This finding can be attributed to the characterization factor of the IMPACT2002+ method for direct CO emissions, which the ReCiPe method lacks. The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC, 200114. International Panel on Climate Change (IPCC), Climate Change 2001: The Scientific Basis ( Press Syndicate of the University of Cambridge, Cambridge, United Kingdom, 2001).) reported that carbon monoxide has an indirect global warming potential because of its effect on atmospheric chemistry. For the energy category, the IMPACT 2002+ analysis results of corn and corn stalk scenarios are approximately 316.65 and 216.30 kg oil eq (change rate at 42.0 MJ/kg oil eq), respectively. The LCIA result of the corn scenario using the IMPACT2002+ method (216.30 kg oil eq) is slightly higher than that obtained using the ReCiPe method (197.15 kg oil eq). The result may be attributed to the relatively smaller characterization factor value of natural gas (36.5 MJ primary/m3) used for chemical production in the ReCiPe method. In the non-renewable energy category, the characterization value for “in-ground natural gas” generally ranged from 38.3 MJ/m3 to 40.3 MJ/m3 (Frischknecht et al., 20074. Frischknecht, R. S., Jungbluth, N., Althaus, H. J., Doka, G., Dones, R., Hellweg, S., Hischier, R., Humber, S., Margni, M., Nemecek, T., and Spielmann, M., “ Implementation of life cycle impact assessment methods,” Report No. 3, Final Report Ecoinvent Swiss Centre for Life Cycle Inventories, Dubendorf, Switzerland, 2007.). The remaining LCIA results for each scenario are difficult to compare because of the significantly different categories or label substances. Accordingly, the IMPACT2002+ method was used in following study.

C. Cost-combined environmental assessment results
To demonstrate the cost-coupled LCA analysis, a normalized overall environmental value is selected to indicate environmental impacts (Fig. 5). By contrast, stalk compression and pulverization stages present additional high environmental impacts because of their relatively higher energy consumption. E. Sensitivity to analysis
The analysis of the sensitivity of the main contributors is presented in Table V. For the corn stalk scenario, a 5% decrease in corn stalk consumption would result in a decrease of approximately 0.26 kg C2H3Cl eq/t-ethanol, 3.95 × 10−2 kg PM2.5 eq/t-ethanol, 0.19 m2org.arable/t-ethanol, 18.96 kgCO2eq/t-ethanol, 207.16 MJ primary/t-ethanol, 3.95 × 10−5 Daly/t-ethanol, 1.78 PDFm2year/t-ethanol, 207.25 MJ primary/t-ethanol, and 18.18 $/t-ethanol on non-carcinogens, respiratory inorganics, land occupation, global warming, non-renewable energy, human health, ecosystem quality, resources primary, and LCC, respectively. For the remaining contributors, a similar analogy could be made with the sensitivity results shown in Table V. Consequently, for both scenarios, the efficiency of chemical (e.g., enzyme, sodium hydroxide, sulfuric acid, and nutrient) production and corn consumption has the highest economic benefit because of their high contributions to overall economic impact. Similar tendencies could be observed with variations in chemical and corn market prices. Regarding the environmental impact of the corn stalk scenario, the efficiency of steam consumption has the highest environmental benefit in most categories, except for respiratory inorganics, land occupation, and human health. This result is mainly attributed to the decrease in coal consumption during the steam production stage. For the remaining categories, the efficiency of corn stalk consumption has the highest environmental benefit because of the decrease in energy consumption during the transport and corn stalk pulverization stages. By contrast, road transport efficiency has the lowest environmental benefit in most categories, except for land occupation and ecosystem quality. This result is mainly attributed to pollutants (e.g., heavy metals, carbon dioxide, particles matter, nitrogen oxides) emitted from diesel combustion during road transport. The electricity generation process for land occupation and ecosystem quality has the lowest environmental benefit because of its relatively low land use. Similarly, road transport efficiency in the corn scenario has the lowest environmental benefit among all categories. Corn consumption efficiency, by contrast, has the highest environmental benefit in most categories except for non-renewable energy and resources because of the significant effects of efficient steam consumption.

IV. DISCUSSION

LCIA midpoint results show that the corn stalk scenario has low overall environmental impact (Figs. 2 and 4), which is consistent with previous reported studies (Luo et al., 201021. Luo, L., Van Der Voet, E., and Huppes, G., “ Energy and environmental performance of bioethanol from different lignocelluloses,” Int. J. Chem. Eng. 2010, 740962.). Luo et al. (2010)21. Luo, L., Van Der Voet, E., and Huppes, G., “ Energy and environmental performance of bioethanol from different lignocelluloses,” Int. J. Chem. Eng. 2010, 740962. compared the energy performance of bioethanol production from corn and corn stalk based on same system boundaries and allocation procedures. Results show that the energy efficiency performance derived from corn is significantly lower than that from corn stalk. However, the economic impact of corn stalk is higher than that of the corn scenario because of overall corn stalk consumption and high enzyme market price (Fig. 6). The total operation costs presented in this study are slightly lower than in reported researches (936.9 $/t-corn-stalk -based-ethanol (Song et al., 201030. Song, A. D., Ren, T. B., and Zhang, B. L., “ Economic analysis for production of fuel ethanol with corn straw,” Trans. CSAE 26(6), 283–286 (2010) (in Chinese).), 842.9 $/t-corn-based ethanol (Yu and Tao, 200937. Yu, S. and Tao, J., “ Simulation-based life cycle assessment of energy efficiency of biomass-based ethanol fuel from different feedstocks in China,” Energy 34(4), 476–484 (2009). https://doi.org/10.1016/j.energy.2008.12.015) because of different corn consumption efficiencies and feedstock prices (Figure 6). In addition, the global warming and energy scores in the corn scenario (1.5 t CO2 eq/t-ethanol and 13.3 GJ/ t-ethanol, Table IV) are lower than that in reported studies, which shows that the global warming gas emissions and net fossil inputs of corn ethanol in the U.S. are 1.9 t CO2 eq/t-ethanol and 16.2 GJ/ t-ethanol, respectively (Nguyen, 200724. Nguyen, T. L. T., Gheewala, S. H., and Garivait, S., “ Energy balance and GHG-abatement cost of cassava utilization for fuel ethanol in Thailand,” Energ Policy 35(9), 4585–4596 (2007). https://doi.org/10.1016/j.enpol.2007.03.012). However, the global warming (732.21 kg CO2 eq/t-ethanol) score of the corn stalk scenario is higher than that in Canada (418.25 kg CO2 eq/t-ethanol (Humbird and Aden, 200912. Humbird, D. and Aden, A., “ Biochemical production of ethanol from corn stalk: 2008 State of technology Model National Renewable Energy Laboratory (NREL),” NREL Report No. TP-510-46214, USA, 2009.), which is attributed to coal-based electricity generation and overuse of raw materials and energy. A mini-plant for corn stalk-based ethanol production is used in this study (Zhang, 201141. Zhang, H. Q., “ The process simulation of cellulosic ethanol production from corn stover based on the Aspen plus technology,” M.S. dissertation, East China University of Science and Technology, 2011 (in Chinese).), because cellulosic ethanol production has not yet been industrialized in China. Thus, high environmental and economic improvement potentials can still be developed.
Fig. 1 shows that approximately 7.5 t corn stalk is used for ethanol production, which is 2.9 t/t-ethanol higher than in the U.S. (Humbird and Aden, 200912. Humbird, D. and Aden, A., “ Biochemical production of ethanol from corn stalk: 2008 State of technology Model National Renewable Energy Laboratory (NREL),” NREL Report No. TP-510-46214, USA, 2009.). If U.S. available technology is used in the present research, approximately 1.2%–23.5% of the potential impact of electricity generation will be reduced. In particular, in the non-carcinogen, respiratory inorganic, global warming, and non-renewable energy categories, more than 2.1 kg C2H3Cleq, 0.3 kg PM2.5 eq, 146 kg CO2 eq, and 1.6 × 103 MJ primary environmental benefits will be observed, respectively. The total direct operation cost will be reduced to 588.4 $/t-ethanol, which is lower than that of the corn scenario. The result is consistent with that of a previous study on bio-ethanol production cost in Europe (Festel et al., 20143. Festel, G., Wurmseher, M., Rammer, C., Boles, E., and Bellof, M., “ Modelling production cost scenarios for biofuels and fossil fuels in Europe,” J. Cleaner Prod. 66, 242–253 (2014). https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jclepro.2013.10.038), in which the cost competitiveness of cellulosic ethanol production can be improved from technologies learning and thereby outperform that generated from corn.
In addition, corn stalk transport represents an important contribution to overall environmental and economic impact (Fig. 5). Thus, understanding the relationship between economic impact and road transport distance is necessary. Notably, the road transport aspect of corn stalk transport from local storage site to company site covered approximately 100 km in the present study. Current bio-ethanol plant gate price, ethanol subsidies for non-grain crops, infrastructure, and labor cost are 1,092.3 $/t-ethanol (www.bitauto.com/youjia/), 114.6 $/t-ethanol (Ministry of Finance of the People's Republic of China, 201223. Ministry of Finance of the People's Republic of China, Notice on the Adjustment of the Financial Subsidy of Bio-fuel Ethanol, 2012 (in Chinese).), 114.2 $/t-ethanol (Song et al., 201030. Song, A. D., Ren, T. B., and Zhang, B. L., “ Economic analysis for production of fuel ethanol with corn straw,” Trans. CSAE 26(6), 283–286 (2010) (in Chinese).), and 104.6 $/t-ethanol (Song et al., 201030. Song, A. D., Ren, T. B., and Zhang, B. L., “ Economic analysis for production of fuel ethanol with corn straw,” Trans. CSAE 26(6), 283–286 (2010) (in Chinese).), respectively. Accordingly, the maximum operation cost should be lower than 988.1 $/t-ethanol. When corn stalk consumption is at 7.5 and 4.6 t, the theoretical upper-bound road transport distance should be approximately 300 and 700 km, respectively. If the distance from the local corn stalk storage to the ethanol production site is over 300 or 700 km, the total operation cost generated by the corn stalk scenario will exceed the aforementioned maximum operation cost (988.1 $/t-ethanol). Results indicate that transport distance and corn stalk consumption efficiencies are the keys to reducing overall environmental burden.
Coal-based electricity generation is considered in the present study because coal serves an important function in China's electricity generation (Cui et al., 20121. Cui, X. W., Hong, J. L., and Gao, M. M., “ Environmental impact assessment of three coal-based electricity generation scenarios in China,” Energy 45(1), 952–959 (2012). https://doi.org/10.1016/j.energy.2012.06.063). If natural gas-based electricity generation is involved, approximately 98.8%, 89.0%, 46.4%, and 44.7% of the non-carcinogens, respiratory inorganics, global warming, and non-renewable energy potential impacts of corn stalk scenario will be further reduced, respectively. The global warming score of corn stalk will be reduced to 373.2 kg CO2 eq/t-ethanol, which is close to the reported result in Canada (Spatari et al., 200531. Spatari, S., Zhang, Y., and Maclean, H. L., “ Life cycle assessment of switchgrass- and corn stover-derived ethanol-fueled automobiles,” Environ. Sci. Technol. 39(24), 9750–9758 (2005). https://doi.org/10.1021/es048293+) (418.25 kg CO2 eq/t-ethanol).
Notably, for the corn scenario, corn stalk open burning was considered in the present study because this material is traditionally burned by Chinese farmers after harvests. However, in the case of corn stalk reused for ethanol production, the overall environmental impacts will be significantly reduced, particularly for the carcinogen and non-carcinogen categories, which exhibit lower impacts than that seen from the corn stalk scenario. However, the impacts generated from respiratory inorganic, land occupation, global warming, and non-renewable energy categories, which have a dominant contribution to the overall environmental impact, remained higher than that seen from the corn stalk scenario (data not shown). In addition, Figure 7 indicates that the LCA results of cellulosic ethanol production are largely dependent on the allocation method, which is also proven by a previous study (Luo et al., 200920. Luo, L., Van Der Voet, E., Huppes, G., and Udo De Haes, H. A., “ Allocation issues in LCA methodology: A case study of corn stover-based fuel ethanol,” Int. J. Life Cycle Assess. 14(6), 529–539 (2009). https://doi.org/10.1007/s11367-009-0112-6). Accordingly, further discussions on the applications of straw resources (e.g., ethanol, electricity, biogas, open burning, composting, pulp, and returning to field) based on the same system boundaries and allocation procedures are highly needed.
In addition, a literature review (Liew et al., 201418. Liew, W. H., Hassim, M. H., and Ng, D. K. S., “ Review of evolution, technology and sustainability assessments of biofuel production,” J. Cleaner Prod. 71, 11–29 (2014). https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jclepro.2014.01.006) showed that biomass-based fuel production had low economic competitive than fossil because of its higher production cost, which is mainly attributed by feedstock cost, fossil oil price, capital cost, plant capacity and availability. However, for a mid- to long-term perspective, the production cost of ethanol from lignocellulosic raw materials may outperform fossil fuel because of the improvement of production technologies and scale size (Festel et al., 20143. Festel, G., Wurmseher, M., Rammer, C., Boles, E., and Bellof, M., “ Modelling production cost scenarios for biofuels and fossil fuels in Europe,” J. Cleaner Prod. 66, 242–253 (2014). https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jclepro.2013.10.038). Accordingly, corn stalk-based ethanol production can be a good choice for the proper use of grain residues.
The above information shows that the economic impact can be improved by increasing the efficiency of road transport and ethanol production. Road transport and ethanol production serve as a significant “win-win” factor in terms of environmental and economic impact. The life cycle inventories, potential effects of both scenarios, and transport distance sensitivity studies will help policymakers in making decisions as regards problem rectification or construction of cellulosic plants in China.

V. CONCLUSION

This study introduced corn stalk-based ethanol and corn-based ethanol productions in China. Results indicate that adopting corn stalk-based ethanol production in China reduces the environmental effects of direct stalk burning, protects human health, and decreases dependence on food grain crop-based ethanol production. Research results will help provide basic and necessary information for evaluating environmental and economic impacts of biomass based-ethanol production, facilitate the decision making of policymakers regarding the establishment, reconstruction, expansion, or technological transformation of biomass-based ethanol industries, provide useful scientific information for policymakers in making decisions as regards corn stalk disposal, and increase the life cycle inventory database of China. However, given the limitations associated with the available data, a more systematic and reliable assessment, made in connection with uncertainty analysis, economic burden from health and ecosystem loss, governmental policies, the economy, society, and energy efficiency need to be conducted in the future. Moreover, an assessment of potential environmental impact based on detailed geography of China, environmental conditions, and population conditions will allow for the presentation of more credible research results with regard to straw open burning in China.
