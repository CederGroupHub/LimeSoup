Abstract

This paper deals with the problem of synchronization and optimization of semiconductor ring lasers. When the frequency mismatch is −20 GHz and the injection coefficient is greater than 2.5, the semiconductor ring lasers in two different configurations (open and closed loops) can all achieve good synchronizations with cross correlation coefficients above 0.95. The robustness of two configurations to frequency detuning are discussed and the results show the synchronization quality of open loop strategy are superior to that of closed loop. Effects of internal parameter mismatches on synchronization quality are also studied. Through modulating internal parameters of semiconductor ring lasers the synchronization quality of two configurations can be optimized. The correlation coefficient of closed loop strategy increases from 0.97 to 0.99, (0.95 to 0.96 about open loop).

II. THEORY AND MODEL

The synchronization schematic is shown in Fig. 1. SRL1 and SRL2 are two same semiconductor ring lasers. Each consists of a circular laser cavity, and an output waveguide located next to the ring. Part of the light in the ring can be coupled out through directional couplers and re-injected into the ring. In this set up the delayed signal of the clockwise (CW) mode(represented by the black dotted line with single arrow) of the SRL1(or SRL2) is re-injected into the CW mode of SRL1(or SRL2) itself by which SRL1(SRL2) can generate chaotic signal. Chaos synchronization can be achieved by injection a part of light intensity of the chaotic SRL1 into SRL2 (The red dotted line with single arrow represents chaos injection). It is well-known there exist two modes for each SRL so it is not easy to make a quick conclusion to the synchronization properties. Here we only concentrate on the CW mode in Fig. 1, because the counter clockwise(CCW) mode will decay due to mode competition and can be ignored when the CW mode is re-injected into the cavity.

In the following sections, a rate equation model that accounts for the dynamics of the SRL is used without loss of generality. We assume that the SRL operates in a single longitudinal and transverse mode regime which is consistent with the assumptions in the literature. The theoretical analysis of the two-mode ring laser is based on the two-mode model which has successfully described SRLs as gyroscopes and explained the observed alternating oscillation regime in the L-I characteristics of the SRL,1414. Guohui Yuan, Zhuoran Wang, Bei Li, Muhammad Irfan Memon, and Siyuan Yu, “Bistability and swithing properties of semiconductor ring lasers with external optical injection,” IEEE J. Quantum Electron 44, 41-48 (2008). https://doi.org/10.1109/JQE.2007.909523 it is used to analyze the competitions between a pair of counter-propagating longitudinal modes at a single cavity resonance. The electric field inside the ring cavity can be expressed as formula (1):
E(x,t)=E1(t)exp[−i(ω01t−kx)]+E2(t)exp[−i(ω02t+kx)](1)
Where E1 and E2 are the mean electric-field associated with the two propagating direction modes, mode1 is CW and mode 2 is CCW; x is the longitudinal spatial coordinate along the ring circumference assumed positive in the CCW direction; k is the wave vector and ω01, ω02 are the optical frequency of the lasing longitudinal modes.
The SRLs’ synchronization model consists of two equations accounting for two slowly varying envelopes of the counter propagating fields , and a third equation for the carrier number, Eq. (2) contains nonlinear coupling through nonlinear gain saturation effects and linear coupling through backscattering between the two counter-propagating fields.1515. C. Born, M. Sorel, and S. Yu, “Linear and nonlinear mode interactions in a semiconductor ring laser,” IEEE J. Quantum Electron. 41(3), 261–271 (2005). https://doi.org/10.1109/JQE.2004.841614 The noise is neglected because we are interested in the laser being biased high above threshold66. K. Technol 27(6), 631-638 (2009). https://doi.org/10.1109/JLT.2008.927755 and operates unidirectional. The subscript ‘t’ stands for transmission laser and ‘r’ stands for receiver laser, ‘1’stands for CW mode and ‘2’ stands for CCW mode. Ntr is the carrier density in the active region, α is the line-width enhancement factor accounting for phase-amplitude coupling in the semiconductor medium, Γ represents the optical confinement factor which gives the spatial overlap between the active gain volume and the optical mode volume, νg is the group velocity, gn is the differential gain at transparency, N0 is the carrier density at transparency, εs and εc are the self-and cross-gain saturation coefficients respectively, τp is the photon lifetime in the ring cavity, τs is the carrier lifetime, τin is the optical round-trip time in the ring, ωtrth is the longitudinal resonant frequency at threshold. ωtr is the optical frequency of the lasing longitudinal modes of transmission laser and receiver laser, Considering the CW mode frequency is the same as that of CCW mode in a SRL’s cavity and is the same as the resonant frequency at threshold. we define (ωt − ωr) as the frequency mismatch between the two lasers. kf is the ratio of feedback power and output power of SRL , kinj is the injection coefficient (the ratio of injection optical signal electric field amplitude and the internal reflection electric field amplitude of the receiver laser at the output facet), τf is the delay time of feedback, τinj is the delay time of injection, I is the bias current and ηi is the injection efficiency, e is the electronic charge, V is the volume for the quantum well active region, Kd and Kc are dissipative coupling and conservative coupling.

III. CHAOS SYNCHRONIZATION RESULTS AND OPTIMAZATION

Fig. 2 shows the numerically time series of the laser output, the corresponding power spectra and intensity correlation diagrams (ωt − ωr = − 20 GHz, kinj = 2.5, τinj = 6ns). Fig. 2a is the synchronization results of closed loop strategy and Fig. 2b is that of open loop strategy. When the bias current is I = 176mA (≫Ith = 21.5 mA), feedback delay time is τf = 0.1ns, feedback coefficient is kf = 0.3, the SRL oscillates in chaotic regime. The chaos signal generated by SRL1 is injected into SRL2 with/without self-feedback then the synchronization generates. In Fig. 2a the transmission and the receiver lasers have good synchronization time series, the intensity profiles are consistent with each other. The power spectrums colored in red and black are consistent also. The intensity correlation diagram shows well synchronization between two lasers with correlation coefficient of 0.97. In Fig. 2b the time series are also consistent with each other and the synchronization coefficient is 0.95, the power spectrums bifurcate a little at the frequency between 40 and 50 GHz. The intensity correlation diagram shows thicker range than closed loop. Anyway, the synchronizations of the two structures are all good enough and basically meet the need of secure communication. In simulation we compensate the injection delay time between the two lasers so the time series show accordance with each other in the time synchronizations.

The frequency detuning is an important factor which will influence the chaos synchronization quality, to obtain a better understanding on the chaos synchronization properties the two-dimensional maps are used to study the impacts of both injection coefficient and frequency detuning. In this section we compare the robustness of synchronization for both open and closed-loop configurations. In Fig. 3 there is a color scale which stands for different correlation coefficients, it can be seen that the chaos synchronization of open loop (Fig. 3a) is more robust to frequency detuning than that of closed loop (Fig. 3b). Fig. 3a demonstrates the most of the good synchronization region lies in negative frequency detuning range, while in open loop (Fig. 3b) the whole area shows good synchronization characteristic. Particularly we find the best synchronization quality regions are in the area which are labeled with white arrow in Fig. 3 and the cross correlation coefficient is close to 1.

Above results can be explained as follows. For the closed loop structure, due to the only difference of two lasers function is the injection item in the receiver laser, so when the injection coefficient is small the good synchronization region will occur near the zero detuning range. With the injection coefficient increasing the good synchronization region will move to the negative detuning range which is presented in Fig. 3(a). For the open loop the case is different, the injection item will balance the influence of lacking of feedback item to some extent, so in big detuning range the synchronization is superior to closed loop. When the injection coefficient is above 2.5 and the frequency mismatch is about −20 GHz, the synchronization correlation coefficients of two strategies are close to 1. The reason can be explained like this, due to injection power is higher enough than the power in the cavity, so the SRLs show strong injection locking and the synchronization quality achieves its best, when the SRL operates in injection-locking regime the difference of feedback signal between two strategies become weak and can be ignored.
The synchronization of some chaos lasers ask for identical internal parameters, when the lasers’ parameters are not consistent with each other the synchronization quality will degrade,1717. Cao liang-ping, Xia Guang-qiong, Deng Tao, Lin Xiao-Dong, and Wu Zheng-Mao, “Bidirectional chaos communication based on semiconductor laser with incoherent optical feedback,” ACTA PHYSICS SINICA 59(08), 5541-06 (2010). but in other lasers’ synchronization the things are different,1818. Fan Li, Xia Guang-Qiong, and Wu Zheng-Mao, “Chaotic parallel synchronization of optoelectronic feedback semiconductor lasers,” ACTA PHYSICS SINICA 58(02), 0989–06 (2009). so it is important to study the influences of laser internal parameters on synchronization. Here, we define the mismatch ratio of laser internal parameters as the ratio of the difference of laser internal parameters with transmission laser internal parameters. The influences of the mismatches of differential gain gn, the line-width enhancement α, photon lifetime τp and optical round-trip time in the ring τin on the synchronization are investigated. Fig. 4 shows the cross correlation coefficient as a function of the mismatch ratio for above parameters. It can be seen the robustness to parameters’ mismatch of open loop is superior to that of closed loop. In Fig. 4(a) the cross correlation coefficients increase with the mismatch rates of α and τin increase till the mismatch rate of 12%, at which the synchronization coefficient is 0.975 and after the point the synchronization quality deteriorates. With the decreasing of τp mismatch rate the synchronization quality improves and the correlation coefficient arrives nearly 1 till the mismatch rate of −30%, from the curve of photo lifetime mismatch we can see the rate of change of correlation coefficient achieve the minimum at the mismatch of −30%, with the mismatch rate increases positively the synchronization quality of SRLs deteriorates more quickly. At last, about the differential gain mismatch rate, it has little effect on the synchronization in the negative mismatch area and in the positive area it will decrease with the increasing of mismatch rate. Fig. 4(b) displays the circumstances of open loop which is alike with the result in1818. Fan Li, Xia Guang-Qiong, and Wu Zheng-Mao, “Chaotic parallel synchronization of optoelectronic feedback semiconductor lasers,” ACTA PHYSICS SINICA 58(02), 0989–06 (2009). except for differential gain mismatch.

From above results we can see, due to the differences between open loop and closed loop structure, the dependences of two configurations on the parameters’ mismatches are very different. For open loop the dependence on the parameters’ mismatches is weaker than that of closed loop. Besides, some detuning influences are similar to that of traditional semiconductor lasers in the same structures1818. Fan Li, Xia Guang-Qiong, and Wu Zheng-Mao, “Chaotic parallel synchronization of optoelectronic feedback semiconductor lasers,” ACTA PHYSICS SINICA 58(02), 0989–06 (2009). but some are different, which is because the differences between SRLs and traditional semiconductor lasers. SRLs have special travelling wave models and there are two opposite directional optical signals exist in the SRL’s cavity simultaneously. Traditional semiconductor lasers have standing wave model and there is one mode in the resonant cavity. The materials between these two kinds of lasers are very different also. SRLs have bistable characteristic but traditional semiconductor lasers have not this characteristic. SRLs have more abundant dynamics than traditional semiconductor lasers. SRLs devices occurred in recent decades and people have not done enough research in this field, so it is difficult to elaborate the reasons of different dependences on the parameters’ mismatches between two structures. This is a problem which will be deserved to be studied further in the future.
Based on above analysis, if we choose proper parameters at which the cross correlation coefficients are the best separately and the synchronization quality maybe improved. Fig. 5 show the synchronization time series of before and after optimization. Fig. 5(a) is the synchronization optimization result of closed loop. Here we set the mismatch rate of α and τin as 10%, the τp mismatch rate as −30% and the gn mismatch rate as 5%. It is clear that the cross coefficient of closed loop increases to 0.99 after optimization, the time series of the transmission laser and receiver lasers show very perfectly consistent with each other. In Fig. 5(b) this phenomenon is not very evident because the influence of parameters mismatch on the synchronization is weaker than that of closed loop. Here τin mismatch rate is −20%,τp and α are −30% mismatch rates, gn mismatch rate is −5%. after optimization the open loop synchronization correlation coefficient seems like a little improve which can be seen from the colored arrows in open loop of Fig. 5. There are bifurcations in the time series before and the bifurcations disappear after optimization.

IV. CONCLUSION

We study the synchronizations of SRLs with self-feedback and compare the synchronizations of open and closed loops. The results show that the chaotic synchronization of two structures(open and closed loops) with correlation above 0.95 can be obtained with the appropriate parameters. The robustness of open loop to frequency mismatch is superior to closed loop. The best synchronization regimes of the two configures both appear at the injection coefficient greater than 2.5 and the frequency mismatch rate is −20 GHz. In the influences of laser’s internal parameters mismatch on the synchronization, the open loop is also superior to closed loop, but when we optimize the parameters’ mismatches the synchronizations of two structures will become better than before especially in closed loop. The synchronization coefficient increases from 0.97 to 0.99 (closed loop) and 0.95 to 0.96(open loop). We believe that our work will propose a good basis for the future applications of SRL.
