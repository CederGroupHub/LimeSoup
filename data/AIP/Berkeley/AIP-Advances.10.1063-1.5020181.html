Abstract

We study the optical properties of light scattering by a monocrystalline silicon wafer, by using transparent material to replicate its surface structure and illuminating a fabricated sample with a laser source. The experimental results show that the scattering field contains four spots of concentrated intensity with high local energy, and these spots are distributed at the four vertices of a square with lines of intensity linking adjacent spots. After discussing simulations of and theory about the formation of this light scattering, we conclude that the scattering field is formed by the effects of both geometrical optics and physical optics. Moreover, we calculate the central angle of the spots in the light field, and the result indicates that the locally-enhanced intensity spots have a definite scattering angle. These results may possibly provide a method for improving energy efficiency within mono-Si based solar cells.

In this paper, we experimentally study the optical properties of light scattering by mono-Si wafers. In our experiments, we use a transparent material to replicate the surface structure of a mono-Si wafer and illuminate a fabricated sample with a laser source. We obtained the experimental result that the scattering field consists of four spots of concentrated intensity distributed at the four vertexes of a square with lines of intensity linking adjacent spots. The surface structure is made up of pyramids and we numerically simulate the light scattering by the mono-Si wafer by going from the part to the whole and discuss its form and distribution.

II. EXPERIMENT AND RESULTS

In our experiment, a mono-Si wafer with an anisotropically random surface is used as the research object. As can be seen from the sample’s SEM pattern in Fig. 1(a), the random surface is entirely covered by fragmentary pyramids of different sizes and different forms. We use the length of the base edge L to represent the size of a pyramid, and L ranges from 3 μm to 20 μm approximately. Although the lateral faces and base edges are incomplete and irregular, the orientations of each pyramid are constant. From scattering theory, we know that the roughness of a surface quickly broadens the widths of the scattered intensity profiles. A sample of the silicon wafer used in practical solar cells is so rough that in the reflective scattering geometry, the main scattering intensity lobes do not even appear in the observation plane. To overcome this problem, we use transparent adhesive to replicate the surface structure of the sample. Then the replicated adhesive sample converts the reflective phase 4πh(r)/λ of the surface into the transmissive phase 2π(n-1)h(r)/λ, where λ is the incident wavelength, n is the refractive index of the adhesive and h(r) is the surface height. Obviously, the replacement of the sample greatly reduces the widths of the scattering profiles and makes the measurement easy and feasible.

Ultraviolet (UV) sensitive adhesive with a refractive index of n = 1.48 was selected as the transparent material, since this adhesive is easily solidified under the illumination of a UV lamp. Fig. 1(b) is a diagrammatic sketch of the fabrication of the surface sample, with different colors labeling the different materials. We fabricated the sample by replicating the silicon wafer in the following way: (1) Drop a suitable amount of UV-sensitive adhesive of type J-646-1 onto a flat glass plate that has been carefully cleaned; (2) slowly affix a silicon wafer to the flat glass and squeeze out all the air bubbles; (3) illuminate them under a UV lamp until the adhesive has solidified completely; (4) drop a suitable amount of the same UV-sensitive adhesive onto a ground glass plate that has been carefully cleaned; (5) slowly affix the previous sample to the ground glass plate with the no-adhesive side of the silicon wafer facing it and squeeze out all the air bubbles; (6) illuminate them again with a UV lamp until the new adhesive has solidified; (7) the silicon wafer and the ground glass plate can be separated jointly from the adhesive layer, which is left intact on the flat glass plate. This adhesive layer is the replicated random surface with same characteristic structures as those of the mono-Si wafer.
Figure 2 shows the experimental setup for observing the scattering intensity profile of the sample. A He–Ne laser with a wavelength of λ = 632.8 nm serves as the optical source with the light beam perpendicular to the sample. An attenuator is used to adjust the light intensity, and a pin-hole is employed to filter out stray light and to acquire a high-quality incident beam. Lens 1 with a focal length of f = 18 cm is placed in front of the sample. On its back focal plane, the scattering intensity distribution is received by a screen. This screen, as shown by the enlarged section in Fig. 2, consists of two pieces of ground glass clamping a piece of white paper, and the paper is patterned with square grids with sides of 3 cm. Lens 2 with the same focal length of f = 18 cm is used for imaging. The scattering pattern on the screen can be uniformly imaged at the CCD camera (Cascade 1K, pixel size 8×8 μm, 1004×1002 pixels). By adjusting the distance between lens 2 and the CCD, the scattering pattern can be entirely imaged within the range of the CCD aperture. In addition, the distance z between the screen and the sample can be measured directly for later analysis.

The screen is composed of two pieces of ground glass and one piece of white paper, and the enlarged section shows its form. Lenses 1 and 2 have a focal length of f = 18 cm.

III. SIMULATION MODELS AND DISCUSSION

We further numerically calculate the light scattering of the random surface of a mono-Si wafer. In the simulation, the surface is split into different unit structures to analyze the light scattering phenomenon. These unit structures include trirectangular tetrahedrons, square pyramids and a partial region of the mono-Si wafer.
Figures 4(a) and 4(b) show simulations of the light scattering by the trirectangular tetrahedrons with the lengths of the base edge L = 5 μm and 20 μm, respectively. Comparing the light fields in Figs. 4(a) and 4(b), the scattering intensity distribution by the smaller tetrahedron has a diffused intensity spot with a larger area, while the scattering intensity distribution by the bigger tetrahedron presents a bright and concentrated intensity spot. The point for both these light fields is that the positions of the intensity spots are opposite to the orientations of the tetrahedrons in the pyramids. Figure 4(c) shows the simulated light field scattering by a complete pyramid with base length L = 20 μm. This intensity distribution consists of four bright concentrated spots at the vertices of a square, and these spots are almost the same as those in Fig. 4(b). Moreover, it is obvious that the light field scattering by a single pyramid has a similar intensity distribution to the experimental result shown in Fig. 3. In order to bring the simulation closer to the real phenomenon, we consider the light scattering of a partial surface of a mono-Si wafer.

We generated three-dimensional data for mono-Si according to the structure of the random surface. The data are composed of a two-dimensional position matrix and the height values, which can approximately characterize the surface structure of the mono-Si wafer. We generate the data in the following way: (1) Select a part of the SEM of the mono-Si wafer from Fig. 1(a) as reference, as shown in Fig. 5(a); (2) calculate the actual length of the pixels in the SEM; (3) generate a two-dimensional position matrix with equal pixel points to those of Fig. 5(a); (4) select the maximal pyramid from Fig. 5(a) and find the farthest base edge to its apex; (5) set the height values of this base edge to zero, and calculate the heights of other pixels in this pyramid according to their geometrical relationships; (6) set the height data of the maximal pyramid as the reference, and successively determine the height data of the adjacent pyramids. Figure 5(b) shows the pattern of the random surface plotted with the generated three-dimensional data. Obviously, the height data of each individual pyramid are reconstructed relative to the neighboring pyramid, and this may cause deviations from the real surface height. However, the orientations and sizes of the pyramid structures are correctly reconstructed, and these are the major characteristics of this surface and the main determinants of the distribution of light scattering. Therefore, the influence of deviations in the reconstructed height on the simulated results for the scattered field can be justifiably neglected. In Fig. 5(c), the pattern shown is the simulated light field scattering by the generated surface of Fig. 5(b). The intensity distribution consists of four bright spots and four lines distributed as a rhombus, and the form of rhombus can be approximately considered to be a square. It is obvious that the simulation is highly coincident with the experimental results.

Based on the theory of light scattering, the scattering intensity distribution of the mono-Si wafer consists of two parts, one of which is the central δ peak formed by the transmittance component of the scattering, and the other is the pure diffused profile. These two parts correspond to geometrical optics and physical optics, respectively. The light scattering of the smaller pyramids predominantly conforms to physical optics, since Fig. 4(a) shows a diffused intensity spot. On the other hand, the light scattering of the larger pyramids predominantly accords with the propagation rule of geometrical optics, since Fig. 4(b) shows a bright concentrated intensity spot. The formation of light fields with four locally-enhanced intensity spots distributed as a square is an effect of both geometrical optics and physical optics, as shown in Figs. 4(c) and 5(c). These discussions can theoretically explain the formation of the light scattering by a mono-Si wafer. Moreover, based on the scattering intensity distribution, the central angle of the locally-enhanced spots can be calculated from the geometrical relationship.
Figure 6 is a schematic for calculating the central angle of the intensity spot in the light field. The center of the spot is selected as the measuring point. The distance between the measuring point and the center of the light field is defined as h. z is the distance between the sample and the screen. β is set as the central scattering angle and determined from β = arctan(h/z). Based on the measured data of h = 33.4 mm and z = 5.0 cm, we obtain the value of β = 33.7°, which roughly determines the central angle of the light scattering. This result implies that light scattering by the random surface of the mono-Si wafer has a definite central scattering angle pointing to the concentrated intensity area. According to the β and the refraction law of geometrical optics, we can obtain an incident angle of α = 56.2°. Because the incident angle is equal to the dihedral angle of the pyramid, the calculated α can also be considered to be the average slope of the surface structure. In addition, the dihedral angle of the pyramids in mono-Si is certain to be 54.7° due to the method of chemical etching for fabricating the mono-Si wafer.2222. Z. Xi, D. Yang, W. Dan, C. Jun, X. Li, and D. Que, Renew. The above discussions indicate that when light perpendicularly illuminates a mono-Si wafer, the scattered light field contains four locally-enhanced intensity spots distributed as a square with intensity lines linking the adjacent spots, and the four spots have a definite scattering angle.

IV. CONCLUSION

In summary, we have studied light scattering by a mono-Si wafer by using UV-sensitive adhesive to replicate its surface structure and illuminating the fabricated sample with a He–Ne laser. In the experiment, the light field contains four spots of concentrated intensity with high local energy, and these spots are distributed in a square form with intensity lines linking the adjacent spots. We analyze the light scattering by the surface structure from different unit structures to the whole surface in a simulation, and calculate the central scattering angle of the concentrated intensity spots. The discussions indicate that the effects of both geometrical optics and physical optics cause the intensity distribution of the four locally-enhanced intensity spots, and the spots have a definite scattering angle. These results may provide a possible method for improving the energy efficiency within mono-Si solar cells in the photovoltaic field.
