Abstract

This paper is a brief overview of the existing systematics on nuclear mean square charge radii, obtained by a combined analysis of data from different types of experiment. The various techniques yielding data on nuclear charge radii are summarized. Their specific feature complexities and the accuracy and precision of the obtained information are also discussed.

1. Introduction

The root mean square (rms) nuclear charge radii, together with other nuclear ground-state properties, represent key information on nuclear matter. They indicate pronounced nuclear structure effects, e.g., shell closures and an onset of deformation. Nuclear quantities such as charge radii and nuclear moments are accessible by experiments based on the interaction of the nucleus with the electromagnetic field produced by the electrons or muons surrounding the nucleus (or with an electromagnetic field applied externally). There are two main types of charge radii experiments: (i) radii changes determined from optical and, to a lesser extent, Kα  x-ray isotope shifts (ISs) and (ii) absolute radii measured by muonic spectra and electronic scattering experiments. The difficulties of the different experiments for measurement of charge radii and their accuracy and precision are discussed. The four electromagnetic methods are sensitive to various properties of the nuclear ground-state charge distributions and are applicable to different (Z, N) ranges. For this reason, a combination of the data from different experimental methods generally yields more detailed and precise knowledge of the nuclear radii than that obtained from any single method alone. The aim of this brief review is to present the up-to-date status of our knowledge on this nuclear observable extracted by a combined treatment of the experimental data of both types.

2. Experimental Data Sources

2.1. Mean square (ms) charge radii changes δ〈r2〉
In early nuclear physics, the main source of information on ground-state properties of the nucleus was classical optical spectroscopy, radio frequency (RF) spectroscopy, or a combination of both. These techniques were applicable only to stable or very long-lived isotopes. Since the beginning of the 1970s, the desire to extend the research into regions far off stability was rendered possible due to the development of laser spectroscopy methods, the modern variant of the classical optical spectroscopy. The measured quantities are the hyperfine splitting (HFS) of the atomic levels of an optical transition and the IS, defined as the difference in frequency of a particular atomic transition between two isotopes A and A′,
νA′,A=νA−νA′.(1)
The IS is sensitive to a change in the charge distribution inside the nucleus and gives information on the change of the ms nuclear charge radius, δr2. Due to the large cross section for absorption of optical photons (≈10−9 cm2) and the high available laser intensity (≥10 mW/cm2) with small bandwidth (∼1 MHz), high precision and high sensitivity experiments became possible. A resolving power exceeding 106 can be achieved and radionuclides with half-lives of milliseconds can be investigated. Therefore, laser spectroscopy has become the most precise and diverse source of information on nuclear charge radii.
Generally, the statistical experimental precision of the ms charge radii, determined from ISs measurements, varies from a fraction of a percent up to several percent (see Table 1). In some exceptional cases, due to a necessary compromise between resolution and sensitivity, the statistical precision exceeds 10%. The IS between two isotopes can, to a very good approximation, be separated into two contributions: the field shift δνFSA′,A and the mass shift δνMSA′,A. The latter accounts for the finite mass of the nucleus and consists of two terms: the normal Ni and specific Si mass shifts, Mi = Ni + Si. As the nuclear masses are known, this provides no new nuclear information. The field shift, however, depends on the extent of the overlap between the atomic electronic wave function and the nuclear volume. Therefore, the field shift provides a highly sensitive probe of the variations in nuclear size as neutrons are added or subtracted from the nucleus. The total IS, δνA′,A, between isotopes with masses mA and mA′ in an atomic transition i can thus be written as
δνiA′,A=δνFSA′,A+δνMSA′,A=Fiδ〈r2〉A′A+mA′−mAmAmA′Mi.(2)

The procedures for extracting changes in nuclear charge radii from optical ISs are well known and well-described in the literature. However, they suffer from some serious problems due to the determination of electronic factor Fi (describing the electron density at the site of the nucleus) and to the difficult treatment of electron correlations entering the specific mass shift (see Otten88. E. W. Otten, in Treatise on Heavy-Ion Science, edited by D. Alan Bromley (Plenum Press, New York, 1989), Vol. 8, p. 517. and a discussion considering the current status of experiment and theory in Kluge99. H.-J. Kluge, Hyperfine Interact. 196, 295 (2010). https://doi.org/10.1007/s10751-010-0172-9). The mass shift can only be treated with the required accuracy and precision for atomic systems with at most three electrons. With increasing mass number, the mass shift contributions to the total IS decrease. As described in Eq. (2), this decrease is inversely proportional to the masses of both isotopes. In heavy atoms, the mass shift (and, hence, the specific mass shift) is almost negligible, while the field shift, which accounts for changes in radii, increases.
The electromagnetic fields at the site of the nucleus produced in neutral, many-electron atom can be calculated with an accuracy of the order of 1% (in the most favorable cases such as alkaline atoms), or 5%–10% more generally. Traditionally, Fi is evaluated from atomic electron shell data using either semiempirical procedures and/or Hartree–Fock calculations. Along a given isotopic chain, the uncertainty in Fi shows up only as an uncertainty in a common scaling factor of the slope of the charge radii. Both factors—Fi and specific mass shift—determine the systematic uncertainties of the extracted charge radii changes. The influence of the statistical and systematic uncertainties on the measured charge radii changes in different mass regions is tracked in Table 1.
The Kα x-ray ISs are sensitive to the same nuclear parameters as the optical shifts and provide an important source of complementary information on ms radii changes δr2. In comparison to optical spectra, Kα x-ray experiment is easier to interpret and has the advantage of considerably fewer uncertainties arising from the evaluation of the experimental observations. The disadvantages of the method based on Kα x-ray IS are the relatively low experimental precision and the applicability only to stable isotopes.
2.2. Rms charge radii R =〈r2〉1/2
The absolute rms charge radii values of stable isotopes are obtained by spectroscopy of muonic atoms or by electron scattering experiments. The IS in muonic spectra measures a radial moment which depends on the particular transition being investigated and probes somewhat different moments of nuclear distribution—the so called “Barrett moments” rke−αr. Nevertheless, the results are also quoted in terms of r2 using the deformed Fermi charge distribution (see Refs. 1010. R. Engfer, H. Schneuwly, J. L. Vuilleumier, H. K. Walter, and A. Zehnder, At. Data Nucl. Data Tables 14, 409 (1974). https://doi.org/10.1016/S0092-640X(74)80003-3 and 1111. G. Fricke, C. Bernhardt, K. Heilig, L. A. Schaller, L. Schellenberg, and E. B. Shera, At. Data Nucl. Data Tables 60, 177 (1995). https://doi.org/10.1006/adnd.1995.1007). In spite of the undoubtedly high accuracy of the absolute muonic Barrett moments, the accuracy of rms nuclear radii extracted from muonic spectra is strongly limited by the corrections for a nuclear polarization shift and the detector’s resolution.
An additional method for determining the absolute r2 values is the analysis of data from elastic electron scattering experiments. In principle, these experiments would provide an excellent model-independent determination of 〈r2〉 if low momentum q transfer measurements could be carried out. However, the q-range of the experiments is limited which leads to additional uncertainties. The resulting uncertainties can be only estimated by an asymptotic expansion of the form factor. The charge distribution might be affected by corrections which take into account the virtual excitation of the nucleus during the scattering process. Such corrections lead to relatively large systematic uncertainties compared to the precision of optical or muonic x-ray measurements.
Muonic atom spectroscopy and electron scattering investigations require tens of milligrams of target material at least and are applicable only to stable isotopes. The R values, extracted by both types of experiments and even by different experiments of the same type, exhibit large spread. This is demonstrated in Table 2.

3. Combined Analysis

The benefits of a combined data treatment seem to be obvious: an absolute rms charge radius R(A) of isotope A can be calculated by adding the radius change δ〈r2〉A′,A between isotopes A and A′ to the known rms radius R(A′) of isotope A′,
R2(A)=R2(A′)+δr2A′A.(3)
The combined treatment is applicable to those elements for which both types of data—on radii changes δ〈r2〉A′A and absolute radii R(A)—exist. The extension toward the neutron numbers far from stability is determined only by the range of δ〈r2〉 data. Let us note that the data obtained are not a simple compilation of individual measurements but constitute a consistent set of rms R values over the nuclear chart. In this case, “consistent” means that the data did not produce unreasonable crossings of the N or Z course of nuclear radii. Moreover, they could be interpreted in quantitative agreement with other well-established experimental facts of nuclear structure.

4. Conclusion

Transforming δ〈r2〉 into absolute rms radii values produces an overview of the charge radii trend R(Z, N) in an extended region of nuclei from He to Cm. The accuracy and precision of the combined data are high compared to those of the directly measured radii values for the same element. The table of absolute values of the experimental charge radii can be considered from different aspects. The general consequences of the radii tabulation, based on different algorithms, are already discussed in detail in the previous works.15,16,18,2215. E. G. Nadjakov, K. P. Marinova, and Yu. P. Gangrsky, At. Data Nucl. Data Tables 56, 133 (1994). https://doi.org/10.1006/adnd.1994.100416. I. Angeli, At. Data Nucl. Data Tables 87, 185 (2004). https://doi.org/10.1016/j.adt.2004.04.00218. I. Angeli and K. Marinova, At. Data Nucl. Data Tables 99, 69 (2013). https://doi.org/10.1016/j.adt.2011.12.00622. I. Angeli, Yu. P. Gangrsky, K. P. Marinova, I. N. Boboshin, S. Yu. Komarov, B. S. Ishkhanov, and V. V. Varlamov, J. Phys. G: Nucl. Part. Phys. 36, 085102 (2009). https://doi.org/10.1088/0954-3899/36/8/085102 Those include the influence of the magic neutron and proton numbers, of deformation, and of the odd–even staggering not only in isotopic but also in isotonic dependences of R over the whole nuclide chart.
Sudden changes in the N or Z radii behavior may depend on shell closure, on the change of nuclear shape (i.e., deformation), or on the presence of weakly bound nucleons. Therefore, another aspect is important, namely, correlations of charge radii with other nuclear parameters, for example, the energies, E1(2+), of the first excited 2+ state, the ratios of the energies of the first 4+ and 2+ states, R4/2 = E1(4+)/E1(2+), the reduced transition probabilities B(E2)↑ = B(E2; 2+ → 0+), and the two neutron or two proton separation energies S(2n) and S(2p). Recently, remarkable correlations between R and the above-mentioned observables in the isotopic trends have been recognized by Cakirli et al.2525. R. B. Cakirli, R. F. Casten, and K. Blaum, Phys. Rev. C 82, 061306(R) (2010). https://doi.org/10.1103/PhysRevC.82.061304 predominantly in the intermediate and heavy mass regions. This provides a new perspective in the study of structural evolution and can be a guide for future measurements. A proper arrangement of such empirical information can be exploited for the finding of hidden properties. Light nuclei are of increasing importance due to the many exotic properties discovered there. For example, a recent empirical study2626. I. Angeli and K. Marinova, J. Phys. G: Nucl. Part. Phys. 42, 055108 (2015). https://doi.org/10.1088/0954-3899/42/5/055108 indicates shell structure changes and the appearance of new nontraditional single or double magiclike numbers.
