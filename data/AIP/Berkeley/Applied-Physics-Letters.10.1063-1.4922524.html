Abstract

Anisotropy and inhomogeneity of carbon fiber reinforced polymers (CFRPs) result in that many traditional non-destructive inspection techniques are inapplicable on the delamination evaluation. This letter introduces eddy current pulsed phase thermography (ECPPT) for CFRPs evaluation considering volumetric induction heating due to small electrical conductivity, abnormal thermal wave propagation, and Fourier analysis. The proposed methods were verified through experimental studies under transmission and reflection modes. Using ECPPT, the influence of the non-uniform heating effect and carbon fiber structures can be suppressed, and then delamination detectability can be improved dramatically over eddy current pulsed thermography.

Eddy current pulsed phase thermography (ECPPT) is a powerful eddy current thermography involving pulsed inductive heating, thermal wave conduction, and Fourier analysis. It was proposed for subsurface defect evaluation in steel, and the results have illustrated that the non-uniform heating effect and the variation of surface emissivity can be eliminated.7,15,167. Y. Fig. 1(a) shows the diagram of ECPPT for CFRPs. The excitation signal is a pulse of high frequency current, which is driven to coil. Then, excitation current will induce eddy currents and then generate resistive heat in CFRP. The induced eddy currents have a skin depth, which can be calculated by
δ=1πμσf, (1)
where f is frequency of excitation signal, σ is electrical conductivity (S/m), and μ is magnetic permeability (H/m). It is concluded that the skin depths vary for different materials. For ferromagnetic materials with large electrical conductivity and permeability, skin depth is very small on the order of μm. Given that the excitation frequency is 100 kHz, the skin depth for cast iron is about 45 μm.1212. M. Pan, Y. He, G. Tian, D. Chen, and F. Luo, NDT & E Int. 52, 28–36 (2012). https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ndteint.2012.08.007 Thus, the heating style for ferromagnetic materials is considered as surface heating. Under this condition, heat conducts from surface to inside. If there is a defect that blocks the heat conduction, heat will reflect from defect to the surface. For CFRPs (σ = 1000 S/m and no magnetic), the skin depth is significantly large (50 mm when excitation frequency is 100 kHz).1212. M. Pan, Y. He, G. Tian, D. Chen, and F. Luo, NDT & E Int. 52, 28–36 (2012). https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ndteint.2012.08.007 Thus, the heating style for CFRPs is volumetric heating. Due to skin effect, the eddy current density has an exponential decay with the defect depth. Thus, surface generates a little more heat than inside. And then, heat transfers from surface to inside, although the transfer speed is slower than that of surface heating. If there is a defect, thermal abnormity caused by defect will directly transfer from defect to outside.

Thermal response in CFRPs can be recognized as a sum of thermal waves. Each of them has a different frequency ω, thermal diffusion length μ, and speed v. According to thermal wave theory, the thermal diffusion length μ can be expressed by (2)
μ=2kωρC=2αω, (2)
where, k is thermal conductivity, ρ is density, C is heat capacity, and α is thermal diffusivity. The propagation speed v can be obtained as
v=2ωα. (3)
The last two equations indicate that higher frequency thermal waves propagate nearer but very fast, while low frequency thermal waves propagate farther but very slowly. If there is a defect at some depth, the parameters (like amplitude and phase) of thermal waves will be changed. However, not all variation for thermal waves can propagate to surface. If the thermal diffusion length μ is greater than depth d, the variation of thermal wave can be observed on the temperature distribution of surface.
There are two configuration modes for eddy current thermography: reflection mode where induction coil heats the surface of object and IR camera records the temperature on the same side, and transmission mode where the object is stimulated from one side whilst data are recorded on the opposite side. For ferromagnetic material, heat conducts from surface to inside and reflects from defect to surface, as shown in Fig. 1(b). Thus, the defect characterization methods under transmission mode and reflection mode are totally different. Under reflection mode, it is easy to quantify defect depth d based on heat conduction. However, it is impossible to quantify the depth under transmission mode, although it yields high contrast results than reflection mode due to block of heat conduction.1818. Y. He, M. Pan, and F. Luo, Rev. Sci. Instrum. 83(10), 104702 (2012). https://doi.org/10.1063/1.4756211 As shown in Fig. 1(a), abnormal thermal wave will propagate from defect to both surfaces of CFRP under volumetric heating. So, the characterization methods under transmission and reflection modes are similar. If thermal wave diffusion length μ is greater than depth (dr under reflection mode and dt under transmission mode), abnormal thermal wave can be observed through the surface temperature field.

An Easyheat 224 from Cheltenham Induction Heating is used for coil excitation, which has a maximum excitation power of 2.4 kW, a maximum current of 400 Arms, and an excitation frequency range of 150–400 kHz. A rectangular coil is constructed from 6.35 mm high-conductivity hollow copper tube. Water is pumped through the coil during operation to aid in cooling. The IR camera is Flir SC7500, which is a Stirling cooled camera with a 320 × 256 array of 1.5–5 μm InSb detectors. This camera has a sensitivity of <20 mK and a maximum full frame rate of 383 Hz. The radiation of the object was sampled, and the unit is digital level (DL). In order to simplify the procedure, DL is used as the unit of temperature in experimental studies.
The photo and section schematic of CFRPs sample are shown in Figs. 2(a) and 2(b), respectively. The thickness is 3.5 mm. A man-made delamination was manufactured by inserting a polytetrafluoroethylene film between fiber layers. The lateral size for delamination is 100 mm2. The distance from delamination to front and back side is 3 and 0.5 mm, respectively.

The delamination was tested first under transmission mode, where induction coil was placed on front side of sample, while IR camera is placed on back side. The heating and cooling time are 200 and 800 ms, respectively. With ECPT, thermal images at different time are used to detect defects. Fig. 3 shows thermal images at 20, 200, and 500 ms. The unit of temperature is DL, and the temperature is normalized to [0, 1]. The coil is along the y axis, so there is no non-uniform heating effect in direction of y axis. However, the temperature gradient caused by non-uniform heating effect is dominated in x axis direction in all three images, which affects delamination identification. In image at 20 ms, fiber woven structure can be observed through hot and dark patterns. In detail, carbon fiber is highlighted, because the eddy currents directly heat the carbon fiber. In the image at 200 ms, carbon fiber structures blur but still are visible. In image at 500 ms, a low temperature area appears. Fig. 4(a) shows the temperature profile of line over delamination at 20, 200, and 500 ms. It is difficult to identify the delamination due to severe influence by non-uniform heating effect and fiber structures.

The temperature responses for four points (A, B, C, and D) are observed, as shown in Fig. 4(b). Their locations are listed in Table II. The temperature responses can be divided into two phases: rising phase and falling phase. The period of rising phase equals to heating time (0.2 s). This phenomenon is totally different to that of steel, where the period of rising phase is much greater than heating time.1818. Y. He, M. Pan, and F. Luo, Rev. Sci. Instrum. 83(10), 104702 (2012). https://doi.org/10.1063/1.4756211 And, the temperature increases almost linearly in rising phase and decreases slowly in cooling phase due to volumetric heating, while temperature decreases exponentially for steel. The locations of points also have influence on temperature responses. Points B and D have similar linear increase and higher temperature than points A and C in rising phase, because they are on the carbon fiber. Points A and C also have the similar change in the rising phase due to on the location of polymer matrix. However, Points C and D show the lower temperature than points A and B in falling phase.

In this letter, eddy current pulsed phase thermography is introduced for evaluation of inside delamination in CFRPs combing volumetric induction heating, abnormal thermal wave propagation from inside to outside, and Fourier transform based phase analysis. The experimental studies under both transmission and reflection modes address that: (1) phasegram at specific frequency and constructed by characteristic features can reduce the influence of non-uniform heating effect caused by coil and periodic fiber structures of CFRPs; (2) the same results can be obtained under transmission and reflection modes due to volumetric heating; (3) ECPPT is capable to improve delamination detectability remarkable over conventional eddy current pulsed thermography.
