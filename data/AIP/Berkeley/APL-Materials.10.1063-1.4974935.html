Abstract

We studied the emissive pits in InGaN films grown on compressive and strain-free GaN underlying layers. Pit density decreased with the full width at half maximum of ω(0002) of InGaN. The films grew on compressive and strain-free GaN underlying layers with spiral and step-flow modes, respectively. Carbon impurities accumulated inside the pits. Comparison of cathodoluminescence inside the pits and steady-state photocapacitance spectra showed that the energy level of the carbon impurities appeared at ∼2.8 eV below the conduction band (Ec) for both types of pits. Deep-level defects at Ec −2.4 eV resulting in green fluorescence emission were considered to originate from pits related to screw dislocations.
Indium gallium nitride alloy (InxGa1−xN) is an important material in light-emitting diodes (LEDs) and laser diodes with blue, green, and various other emission colors. In these optical devices, an InxGa1−xN layer that is just a few nanometers thick is conventionally used as a well layer in multiple quantum well (MQW) structures. The small effective mass, high mobility, high absorption coefficient, and stability against the UV irradiation of InxGa1−xN materials also make them attractive for use in other applications such as high-frequency transistors11. R. Wang, G. Li, G. Karbasian, J. Guo, F. Faria, Z. Hu, Y. Yue, J. Verma, O. Laboutin, Yu Cao, W. Johnson, G. Snider, P. Fay, D. Jena, and H. Xing, Appl. Phys. Express 6, 016503 (2013). https://doi.org/10.7567/APEX.6.016503 and solar cells.22. A. Devos, Endoreversible Thermodynamics of Solar Energy Conversion (Oxford University Press, New York, 1992). However, to realize such applications, thicker epitaxial InGaN films are necessary.

Passive devices such as photosensors and photovoltaic devices are influenced by deep-level defects that trap photogenerated carriers. That is, device characteristics and performance are greatly affected by the presence of deep-level defects. However, knowledge of the defect levels in thick InGaN films is very limited9,109. E. In this letter, we grow thick InGaN films and verify their deep-level defect energy. The pits formed in the InGaN films are characterized from the viewpoints of emission properties and impurities using cathodoluminescence (CL) and micro-Auger electron spectroscopy (μ-AES). Comparison of the peak positions determined by CL and steady-state photocapacitance (SSPC) spectroscopy1111. Y. Nakano, N. Matsuki, Y. Irokawa, and M. Sumiya, Jpn. J. Appl. Phys. 50, 01AD02 (2011). https://doi.org/10.7567/JJAP.50.01AD02 measurements allows the defect energy levels to be discussed in terms of carbon (C) impurities and screw dislocations.
InGaN films with a thickness of 0.2–0.3 μ m were grown on c-sapphire, GaN template prepared by hydride vapor phase epitaxy (HVPE), and GaN bulk substrates by horizontal metal–organic chemical vapor deposition (MOCVD) under atmospheric pressure. Compressive and strain-free GaN templates with lattice constants a of 0.3183 and 0.3189 nm, respectively, and GaN bulk substrates fabricated by the Na flux method and HVPE were used. After growing a GaN layer (∼2 μ m thick) for 1 h at 1000 °C on each template and substrate, the atmosphere inside the MOCVD reactor was changed from H2 to N2 for the InGaN film growth. An In0.03Ga0.97N film (0.1 μ m thick) as an interlayer was grown at 830 °C for 30 min on the GaN film. Thick InxGa1−xN films (0.2–0.3 μ m ) were then grown at temperatures from 760 to 830 °C for 1 h under trimethylgallium, trimethylindium, and NH3 gas at a flow rate of 7 l/min using a constant In/(Ga+In) molar ratio of 0.95. The InN mole fraction was changed only by the growth temperature and was estimated from the X-ray diffraction (XRD) of the films taking the strain effect into account. The XRD mapping of the ( 10 1 ¯ 4 ) plane indicated that the InGaN samples except for that with an InN mole fraction of 10% were heteroepitaxially strained on the GaN layers.
The number of pits (dark spots) with a diameter of several micrometers in the InGaN samples was counted from optical microscope images of the sample surfaces. Fluorescence emission from the samples was observed through a filter that cut wavelengths shorter than 420 nm. Detailed surface morphology was observed by atomic force microscopy (AFM). CL line-scan measurement across the pits was carried out with an electron beam spot size of 0.1 μ m at room temperature. Micro-AES mapping around the pits was conducted with a beam spot size of 0.2 μ m . The SSPC analysis was performed to measure photocapacitance transients as a function of the incident photon energy from 0.78 to 4.0 eV. Schottky diodes were fabricated on unintentionally doped InGaN surfaces by including a Ni electrode (ϕ = 500 μ m ) for InGaN on c-sapphire or Hg probe (ϕ = 1.23 mm) for the sample on GaN bulk. In the former and latter devices, the Ohmic contact was the planar ring of Ti/Au and Hg, respectively. Monochromatic light was irradiated from the bottom of the polished substrate. Prior to optical excitation, the deep levels were filled with electrons in the dark by applying a voltage pulse of +1.0 V with a pulse width of 3.0 s. The measurement frequency was 100 kHz, and the capacitance phase angle was close to −90°. Details of SSPC are described elsewhere.1212. M. Lozac’h, Y. Nakano, L. Sang, K. Sakota, and M. Sumiya, Phys. Status Solidi A 210, 470 (2013). https://doi.org/10.1002/pssa.201200716
Figure 1 shows AFM, microscope, and fluorescence images of In0.07Ga0.93N samples grown on c-sapphire and GaN bulk substrates. The InGaN sample on a c-sapphire substrate shows spiral growth (Fig. 1(a)), with a root mean square roughness (Rrms) of 1.1 nm. Spiral growth was observed for an InGaN sample grown on a strained GaN template. On a GaN bulk free-standing substrate, step-flow-like growth was realized, providing InGaN with a smoother surface (Rrms: 0.56 nm), as illustrated in Fig. 1(d). Fewer pits terminating heterogeneous steps were detected, and the pit density was drastically decreased in the film formed on the GaN bulk free-standing substrate compared with that on a strained GaN template. It is supposed that the strain of the GaN layer might influence the growth mode of the InGaN films. Optical microscope images contained pits with a diameter of several micrometers as dark spots, as shown in Figs. 1(b) and 1(e). Green fluorescence emission from the same pits was confirmed for InGaN grown on a c-sapphire substrate (Fig. 1(c)). In contrast, no fluorescence was detected from the pits in InGaN grown on a GaN bulk substrate (Fig. 1(f)), while blue emission was observed from a point on the smooth surface. Considering the relationship between the pits and structural properties of the films, next we studied the deep-level defects in the InGaN films.

The pit density depends on the full width at half maximum (FWHM) of ω(0002) of InGaN samples grown on various substrates, as plotted in Fig. 2(a). The cross-sectional profile of some pits was observed by a laser microscope. Their depth exceeded 0.7 μ m , meaning that the pit was formed from the GaN underlying layer. The pits in InGaN grown on a c-sapphire substrate possessed well-defined triangular facets, and those in the films grown on GaN bulk were composed of a combination of triangular and trapezoidal shapes. According to Miyajima et al.,1313. T. Miyajima, T. Hino, S. Tomiya, K. Yanashima, H. Nakajima, T. Araki, Y. Nanishi, A. Satake, Y. Masumoto, K. Akimoto, T. Kobayashi, and M. Ikeda, Phys. Status Solidi B 228, 395 (2001). https://doi.org/10.1002/1521-3951(200111)228:2<395::AID-PSSB395>3.0.CO;2-2 these two kinds of pits were categorized as α- and α-type originating mostly from screw and mixed dislocations, respectively, which is consistent with the growth mode on each substrate. The relationship between the pit density and fluorescence emission density was also categorized into two groups according to the spiral and step-flow growth modes (Fig. 2(b)). Most pits in the spiral growth region emitted green fluorescence, while green fluorescence was hardly detected from the pits in the step-flow region. There might be screw pits smaller than the resolution of the microscope in InGaN films with a step-like growth mode, as mentioned later.

The elemental distribution around pits in InGaN films grown on sapphire and GaN bulk substrates was mapped over an area of 30 × 20 μ m using μ-AES. Figure 6 depicts the SSPC data obtained for InGaN samples with the two types of pits grown on c-sapphire and GaN bulk substrates together with the CL spectra obtained from pits corresponding to point 3 in Fig. 4. The background carrier densities in the InGaN layer evaluated by the CV measurement were 2 × 1017 cm−3 (bias voltage range from 0 to −0.5 V) and 2 × 1016 cm−3 (from −0.5 to −1.0 V) for the samples grown on c-sapphire and GaN bulk substrates, respectively. The measurement bias voltage was changed as specified in Fig. 6. The same bias voltage does not indicate that the depletion layer for each sample has the same width because of the different Schottky electrodes used. Judging from the energy position of the near band edge of GaN in the SSPC spectra in Fig. 6(a), the depletion layer seems to expand close to the interface between InGaN and GaN layers grown on sapphire from 0 to −0.5 V. For InGaN on a GaN bulk substrate (Fig. 6(b)), SSPC signals from two InGaN layers are observed while the signal from GaN is hardly detected. The width of the depletion layer seems to be within the InGaN layers that the SSPC detected at −0.5 to 2.0 V. The total defect density is one order of magnitude lower for InGaN samples with the step-flow growth mode compared with that for the samples with a spiral growth mode. Several defect levels of InGaN have been evaluated by SSPC,21,1021. A. M. Armstrong, M. H. Crawford, and D. Koleske, J. Electron. Mater. 40, 369 (2010). https://doi.org/10.1007/s11664-010-1453-410. M. Lozac’h, Y. Nakano, L. Sang, K. Sakoda, and M. Sumiya, Jpn. J. Appl. Phys. 51, 121001 (2012). https://doi.org/10.7567/jjap.51.121001 and the onsets of defects are denoted in Fig. 6. With increasing reverse bias voltage, the signal intensity of T1 and T4 defects in Fig. 6(a) gradually decreased for the InGaN sample on a c-sapphire substrate. This indicates that the defect density must change in the depth direction of InGaN on c-sapphire substrates, with a higher defect density near the surface. In contrast, defect D1 in Fig. 6(b), the origin of which is unknown, accumulated near the surface of InGaN on GaN bulk substrates. Because the signal intensities of D3, D4, and D5 defects are almost independent of the bias voltage, the distribution of these defects in the depth profile of InGaN may be homogeneous.

We mainly discuss the deep-level defects with respect to the two CL peaks at 2.8 eV (∼443 nm) and 2.4 eV (∼517 nm) from the pits. The defect levels denoted T4 (onset at 3.0 eV for the sample on c-sapphire) and D4 and D5 (2.9 and 3.0 eV, respectively, for the two InGaN layers on GaN bulk substrates) must be related to the CL peak at 2.8 eV. Armstrong et al.2222. A. M. Armstrong, M. H. Crawford, and D. Koleske, Appl. Phys. Express 7, 032101 (2014). https://doi.org/10.7567/APEX.7.032101 also detected this SSPC signal for In0.21Ga0.79N/GaN MQWs used in green LEDs and suggested C and cation vacancies as possible origins. Both the CL peak at around 2.8 eV and the large C content are detected only inside both types of pits, and C substitution at an N site (CN) has a low formation energy.2323. C. H. Seager, A. F. Wright, J. Yu, and W. Gotz, J. Appl. Phys. 92, 6553 (2002). https://doi.org/10.1063/1.1518794 We consider that a pit might form at a carbon impurity (CN), which could produce the CL peak at 2.8 eV.
A step-like feature is clearly detected at the onset of 2.0 eV (T1), and the defect levels exist continuously from 2.0 to 2.9 eV for the InGaN grown with the spiral mode in Fig. 6(a). The green fluorescence density for InGaN samples with spiral growth is correlated with the pit density in Fig. 2(b). It is reasonable to consider that α-type pits originating from the screw dislocations in the InGaN films should be related to the green emission. One possible origin of the step-like defects may be screw dislocations. Although a similar feature is also detected around D3 in Fig. 6(b), the intensity is one order of magnitude lower for InGaN samples on GaN bulk substrates compared with those on c-sapphire ones. The number of screw dislocations in the InGaN on GaN bulk substrates might be too low to detect green fluorescence from them. This defect level must be formed at 2.4 eV below the conduction band (Ec) because of the majority carrier of electron photoemission in SSPC measurements resulting from the intrinsic n-type sample.
In summary, InxGa1−xN films (x ≤ 0.1) were grown on various templates and substrates by MOCVD. We found that α- and β-type pits originating from screw and mixed dislocations formed on the surface of InGaN films grown on compressive and strain-free GaN underlying layers, respectively. The pit density decreased with smaller FWHM of ω(0002) of InGaN. Carbon impurities tended to accumulate inside the pits. Comparison of CL and SSPC results verified that the energy level of the CN defect appeared at Ec −2.8 eV regardless of the type of pit, and that green fluorescence emission was detected from all α-type pits (Ec −2.4 eV). The use of a strain-free GaN underlying layer makes it possible to suppress the formation of α-type pits on the surface of thick InGaN films.
