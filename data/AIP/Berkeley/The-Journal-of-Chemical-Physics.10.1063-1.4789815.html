Abstract

The low-lying excited electronic states of a (Ti6O12)3 nanotube are investigated using ab initio self-consistent field configuration interaction theory. The transition energies and moments are calculated and the nature of the orbitals involved is discussed. Transitions correspond to an excitation from an O2p to a nearby Ti3d orbital and singlet-singlet transitions vary in excitation energy from 2.1 eV to 4.3 eV, depending on the oxygen site and environment of the titanium site. Two different structures for the three stacked Ti6O12 rings are found. The occluded Ti structure is lower in energy than a staggered structure by 1.25 eV, only 0.02 eV/bond. Excited electronic states are found to correspond to highly localized holes on oxygen and a highly localized electron in a d orbital on a nearest neighbor titanium. The staggered structure has four absorptions that lie within the intense portion of the solar spectrum, at 585 nm, 472 nm, 471 nm, 423 nm; the occluded structure has one strong absorption, at 532 nm.

In bulk TiO2, excitations to the conduction band are normally of primary interest because such states can transport electrons and holes. In some cases, excitons can be pinned to the origin of the excitation and be useless for electron or hole transport. In applications such as the generation of solar fuels, excitonic states need to have long enough lifetimes so that energy can be transferred to surfaces and to reacting adsorbates. In the case of nanoparticles, their properties could be used to advantage by generating excitons interior to the particle followed by evolution of the excited state toward lower coordinated sites on the surface. Some loss of energy will occur, but it is possible that the excited state energy could lead to charge transfer excitations of adsorbates much like occurs in electron attachment reactions. The molecule surface complex in its excited state could then initiate a reaction on the excited state surface such as the dissociation of water.
In contrast to bulk TiO2, where rutile and anatase phases have been extensively studied, the behavior of TiO2 nanoparticles is much less well characterized. Several studies suggest that small particles or molecule-like structures could have localized excited states or excitons with transition energies well below the bandgap without recourse to doping. An accurate description of highly correlated, localized excited electronic states requires attention to many details. For these purposes, ab initio configuration interaction theory provides an attractive way to proceed. In the present work, core electrons of Ti are described by atomic orbitals that define Coulomb and exchange potentials that act on electrons explicitly included in the wavefunction. Only 1s, 2s, 2p electrons are included in the Ti core to allow a response of the n = 3 shell to changes in the electronic structure that occur on bonding and photoexcitation. Although the 1s electrons of oxygen could be assigned to an oxygen core, in the present work all of the oxygen electrons are included in the wavefunction.
In order to treat the large number of valence electrons in (Ti6O12)3 we make use of a method developed in our earlier work for treating adsorbates on metal surfaces where high accuracy is needed in the vicinity of the adsorption site. Following an initial SCF treatment of the entire nanoparticle, the resulting delocalized orbitals are transformed by a unitary transformation that maximizes the exchange interaction of the transformed orbitals with orbitals near the site of interest, i.e., the Ti and O orbitals involved in the electron-hole pair. The resulting localized orbitals extend over several atoms and can be thought of as a subspace of N electrons embedded in the total number of electrons of the system. Configurations are generated by excitation from this set of localized orbitals to virtual orbitals obtained by the same type of localization. The occupied and virtual orbitals are transformed separately. The single determinant SCF wavefunction is invariant under a unitary transformation of the orbitals. Numerous applications of the embedding method to ground and excited state reactions on transition metal surfaces have been reported and details of the theory are given in Refs. 18–2018. J. L. A. Electronic structure
The tubes are created by stacking three D3h Ti6O12 rings. Valence molecular orbitals are expanded by a flexible set of Gaussian basis functions [7s7p5d]/(2s2p2d) for titanium and [10s5p]/(4s2p) for oxygen. Wavefunctions are initially constructed by SCF procedures (504 electrons treated) and are followed by multi-reference CI expansions. Details of the CI procedure can be found in Refs. 2121. B. N. Papas and J. L. Whitten, J. Chem. Phys. 135, 204701 (2011). https://doi.org/10.1063/1.3659702 and 2222. Y. Zhang and J. L. Whitten, Int. J. Quantum Chem. 109, 3541 (2009). https://doi.org/10.1002/qua.22345 . As described above, prior to the CI expansion, occupied and virtual orbitals are transformed to maximize their exchange interaction with orbitals defining a region of the tube where excitations will occur. The resulting localized orbitals, which have large interactions with the titanium and oxygen atoms corresponding to the origin of the excitation, are used to construct the CI wavefunction. This choice allows inclusion of a well-defined portion of the electron correlation of each system (treated the same way for all excitations) associated with the change in occupancy. The procedure derives from the fact that the electron density change on the excitation is almost entirely in the region near the electron-hole pair. The localized orbitals are ordered by the magnitude of the exchange interaction and in this case eight localized orbitals are found to have large interactions with the O 2p orbitals at the site of interest. Sixteen electrons, which occupy the localized orbitals, are correlated by carrying out multiple excitations while the occupancy of the remaining orbitals is constrained to remain unchanged.
As we consider localized excited states that appear to break the symmetry of the system, we should note that although the total wavefunction must correspond to the symmetry of the Hamiltonian or nuclear framework, there is no fundamental symmetry requirement on individual molecular orbitals in a many-electron system. In small molecules, however, imposing symmetry on individual orbitals offers conceptual and computational advantages, and, in the limit of infinite configuration interaction, will converge to the exact description of the electronic state. Whether excitonic states are created early in the calculation or later using symmetric orbitals is a question of rate of convergence of the CI wavefunction. As noted in the study of periodic rings, allowing symmetry breaking excitonic states at the SCF stage leads to much more rapid convergence.
Since the electron-hole pair turns out to be highly localized over at most four atoms in both the ring and nanotube systems, the resulting wavefunction, even after CI, will not have the correct symmetry. In order to satisfy symmetry conditions rigorously, a linear combination of equivalent electron-hole pair CI wavefunctions could be constructed. Orbitals in these wavefunctions are non-orthogonal and evaluation of their interaction is difficult. However, since the equivalent wavefunctions have oxygen hole orbitals that are separated by several bond lengths (similarly the Ti 3d orbitals are widely separated), the interaction is not expected to cause more than a minor lowering of the excited state energy.
B. Simplex geometry optimization method
Geometry optimizations are carried out by the Nelder-Mead simplex procedure.2323. J. A. Nelder and R. Mead, Comput. J. 7, 308 (1965). https://doi.org/10.1093/comjnl/7.4.308 The optimization allows for custom variation of the geometry and orientation of the three (TiO2)6 rings, subject to symmetry-maintaining constraints. A single ring has D3h symmetry, and in a series of calculations, the middle ring was rotated to two different orientations relative to the other two rings (for simplicity the top ring is a translational copy of the bottom ring). In both cases, the symmetry of the nanotube is D3h. In the simplex procedure, the parameters that define the geometry are taken as components of a so-called vertex or n-tuple. The energy is calculated for that vertex and a systematic variation of the parameters is carried out in a way that eliminates the worst vertex after each iteration. The procedure is widely used in applied mathematics and is surprisingly robust.1717. T. Tsuchiya and J. L. Whitten, J. Chem. Phys. 134, 114701 (2011). https://doi.org/10.1063/1.3564880 It has an advantage in that energy gradients are not required.

III. RESULTS

The geometry optimization scheme used in this work found two stable arrangements for a stack of three (TiO2)6 rings. The more stable structure, which corresponds to a geometry where the titanium atoms are aligned (occluded), is lower in energy than the next most stable structure by 1.25 eV (only 0.02 eV per bond). This arrangement maximizes the distances between the oxygen atoms on adjacent rings, and places the titanium rings 3.80 Å apart with a radius from the axis through the center of the nanotube to the Ti nuclei of 2.80 Å. In this system, every titanium atom in a ring is in an equivalent environment, with four oxygen atoms bonded to each. These oxygen atoms can be classified as exterior, interior, and surface (see Figure 2). Additionally, two surface oxygen atoms associated with adjacent rings are located close (2.97 Å) to each titanium atom. The less stable structure maximizes the distance between the titanium atoms on adjacent rings by staggering them. This tube has a larger radius and smaller inter-ring distance compared to the previous nanotube geometry, with rings of radius of 2.91 Å stacked 3.27 Å apart. This causes neighboring titanium atoms within a ring to no longer be equivalent. Three of the titanium atoms will be comparable to the titanium atoms in the occluded structure, only there are no longer proximal oxygen atoms on adjacent rings. The remaining three titanium atoms are in an oxygen saturated environment, with not only the four bonded oxygen atoms, but two oxygen atoms, only 2.16 Å away from the titanium atom, which are associated with adjacent rings. The staggered and occluded tube structures are depicted in Figure 2.
All excitations examined correspond primarily to an excitation from an oxygen lone-pair orbital into a vacant titanium d orbital. For each unique titanium atom on the central ring of a tube, excitations from all proximal oxygen atoms were considered. Excitations involving more distant pairs were not considered, as these should have higher excitation energies and lower transition dipole moments. In the case of the occluded (o) structure, there are one type of titanium atom (o1) and four types of oxygen atoms considered. These are the interior (in), exterior (ex), surface (su), and inter-ring (ir) oxygen atoms depicted in Figure 3. For the staggered (s) structure, there are two types of titanium atoms. The first (s1) is the titanium atom with the lower oxygen coordination. This titanium has only nearest neighbor in-, ex-, and su- type oxygen atoms. The second titanium atom (s2) has the same oxygen atoms as (s1) plus ir-oxygen atoms. Oxygen atoms considered as excitation origins are depicted in Figure 3. Each excitation is described in shorthand by (O atom designation)-(Ti atom designation); e.g., su-s2 is the excitation from a surface (su) oxygen to the second type (2) titanium atom in the staggered (s) tube.

In all of the electron-hole pairs, one singly occupied orbital is on oxygen and the other on titanium, and it follows that the singlet-triplet splitting will be small because of the small exchange interaction. However, if the singlet excited state is of the same symmetry as the ground state, the energy splitting can increase. This is seen in only two of the excited states reported in Table I.
Analyzing the character of the lowest-lying excitations suggests three traits to keep in mind when attempting to design a titania system with sub-bandgap absorbances. Two of the four lowest excitations involve holes where the electron loss is shared between an oxygen atom adjacent to the particle center, and an oxygen atom on a neighboring ring. None of the higher excitations involve this shared-hole character. The remaining two low excitations involve a hole created on an ex oxygen atom. The lone pair on such an oxygen atom, stabilized only by the presence of two nearby titanium atoms, lies higher in energy than oxygen atoms with a larger number of titanium neighbors. Such a bridging, exposed oxygen atom is of particular interest as it has a prime location for carrying out photocatalytic processes. The third ex state (ex-s2) differs from the other two in that the electron accepting titanium atom is over-saturated with neighboring oxygen atoms: that is, in addition to the normal four bonded oxygen atoms, there are two additional oxygen atoms (of ir type) at only 0.2 Å farther away than the bound oxygen atoms. This makes electron acceptance less favorable and increases the excitation energy (the ex-s2 excitation is 0.6 and 0.9 eV above the other two ex excitations). Note that this effect is masked in the other s2 excitations, as they all include significant hole delocalization, suggesting that there is greater energy lowering from delocalizing the hole than from ensuring that the electron accepting titanium atom is favorable. These results suggest two approaches to creating a titania system which would absorb solar electrons: include oxygen atoms which are external to the particle and which only bridge two titanium atoms; or create a structure in which oxygen atoms that are not bonded to a common titanium atom are close enough to delocalize the hole. In both cases, the electron-accepting titanium atom should not be near electron-rich atoms with which it is not formally bound.

IV. SUMMARY

Two nanotubes derived from a Ti6O12 ring have been examined using SCF-CI theory. Transition energies for low-energy singlet-singlet and singlet-triplet excitations have been determined, and several are found to be within the visible range of the solar spectrum. Key findings are summarized as follows:
(1)
Two different structures for the three stacked (TiO2)6 rings are found corresponding to occluded and staggered Ti atoms in adjacent rings. The occluded structure is lower in energy than the staggered structure by 1.25 eV, only 0.02 eV/bond.
(2)
The excited electronic states correspond to highly localized holes on oxygen and a highly localized electron in a d orbital on a nearest neighbor titanium. Comparisons with excitations in single rings show that shifts in excitation energy are not uniform for the different orientations of the O2p hole.
(3)
The staggered Ti structure has four absorptions that lie within the intense portion of the solar spectrum, at 585 nm, 472 nm, 471 nm, 423 nm; the occluded structure has one strong absorption, at 532 nm.
(4)
Low-lying excitations involve external oxygen atoms or oxygen atoms able to share the hole with nearby oxygen atoms and do not include excitations into tetra-coordinated titanium atoms with additional electron-rich neighbors.
The present results encourage the investigation of excited state lifetimes and the reaction of excited states with adsorbates at the surface. An application of particular interest would be the splitting of physisorbed water to form oxygen and hydrogen.
