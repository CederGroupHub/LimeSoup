Abstract

The reaction of carbon dioxide (CO2) with aqueous 2-aminoethanol (MEA) has been investigated using both blue moon ensemble and metadynamics approaches combined with ab initio molecular dynamics (AIMD) simulations. The AIMD simulations predicted the spontaneous deprotonation of the intermediate compound, MEA zwitterion, and they were used to study two possible routes for subsequent proton transfer reactions: the formation of the protonated MEA and the formation of MEA carbamic acid. The free-energy curve depicted by blue moon ensemble technique supported the favorable deprotonation of MEA zwitterion. The overall free-energy profile showed the favorable formation of the ionic products of MEA carbamate ion and protonated MEA.

I. INTRODUCTION

Although it is more and more important to reduce global environmental load, recent years have seen hardly any sign that the emission of greenhouse gases is decreasing. The key technology for reduction of greenhouse gas is the capture of carbon dioxide (CO2) from the flue gas emitted from the thermal power plant and the steel production.11. G. T. Rochelle, Science 325, 1652–1654 (2009). https://doi.org/10.1126/science.1176731 The cyclic system of amine-based absorption and stripping is an established and proven technology for the CO2 capture. In this process, the CO2 in the flue gas is absorbed into aqueous amine solution in an absorption column at low temperature, and nearly pure CO2 is then extracted in a stripping column by heating the aqueous amine solution. The solubility of CO2 is one of the most important quantities in the CO2 capture technology and is determined by the vapor-liquid equilibrium (VLE) between gaseous CO2 and dissolved CO2. The concentrations of chemical species in aqueous amine solution are governed by the chemical equilibrium relationships through the reaction equilibrium constants. Hence, the Henry’s constant for the CO2 adsorption and the equilibrium constants for all the reactions are required to calculate the solubility of CO2. The equilibrium constant for the reaction of CO2 with aqueous amine compound, however, cannot be directly measured at present. III. RESULTS AND DISCUSSION

A. BOMD of MEAZW in aqueous solution
We first examine interaction of the MEAZW molecule with solvent using the standard BOMD simulation. We used two supercells of different dimensions to explore the model size on the results. Our larger model contains the same number of molecules as used in the work of Guido et al., involving one MEAZW and 122 water molecules, with an edge length of 1.565 nm (hereafter designated as Run L).2727. C. A. Guido, F. Pietrucci, G. A. Gallet, and W. Andreoni, J. Chem. Theory Comput. 9, 28–32 (2013). https://doi.org/10.1021/ct301071b The smaller model contains one MEAZW and 106 water molecules in a cubic box with an edge length of 1.497 nm (hereafter designated as Run S). Figure 1 shows the geometric structures of all compounds considered in this study as reactants, products, or reaction intermediates. We distinguish different skeletal conformations of MEAZW using a pair of symbols XY, where the symbol X designates the C(CO2)–N–C–C dihedral angle, and Y is the N–C–C–O dihedral angle. As a starting configuration for each equilibration run, we arbitrarily choose the Gauche Gauche’ (GG’) form with the intramolecular hydrogen bond of O(COO)–H(OH) (see Fig. 1(c)). For the Run L, the cleavage of the intramolecular hydrogen bond was observed in the middle of the equilibration run of 10 ps. We thus used the GG’ conformer that did not form an intramolecular hydrogen bond as the starting conformation of the BOMD simulation. For the Run S, on the other hand, we did not observe any changes of the skeletal conformation and the intramolecular hydrogen bond during the equilibration of 8 ps. As shown in Table II, both of our standard BOMD simulations (i.e., Run L and S) indicated the deprotonation reaction of MEAZW in contrast to the claim of Ma et al. Our result suggests that the cell size used by Sumon et al. was not too small for the observation of the deprotonation of MEAZW.2828. C. Ma, F. Pietrucci, and W. Andreoni, J. Chem. Theory Comput. 11, 3189–3198 (2015). https://doi.org/10.1021/acs.jctc.5b00379 For the Run L, we observed three temporary proton transfers from MEAZW and the crucial deprotonation of MEAZW. We found the N–C axis rotation of MEAZW between the second and third temporary proton transfers, which resulted in the skeletal conformation of Trans Gauche’ (TG’) form. In order to qualitatively appreciate the role of the solvation effect in stabilization of MEAZW, we performed exploratory BOMD simulation of a non-interacting gas phase molecule of MEAZW (i.e., in absence of solvent molecules). Interestingly, we observed almost immediate collapse of MEAZW to CO2 and MEA in this case. Our results therefore suggest that the solvent water molecules play a double role in the process studied here: they prevent a spontaneous CO2 release, and they also assist the deprotonation reaction through the intermolecular hydrogen bonding.

IV. SUMMARY

In this paper, we have investigated the reaction of CO2 with aqueous MEA using BOMD simulations empowered by BME and mMD approaches. The standard BOMD simulations indicated a short life time of MEAZW and predicted subsequently two reaction routes to formation of MEAC and MEAA. In both final compounds, the water solvent plays the important role of both the proton acceptor and donor. The results obtained using the BME technique suggest that MEAC is slightly more stable than MEAZW in terms of free-energy. It is shown by both BME and mMD methods that the final product is the ionic products of MEAC and MEAH. We presented a complete free-energy profile of the reaction between CO2 and MEA in aqueous solution by constructing the free-energy barriers for all elementary reactions involved in the dissolution process. The highest free-energy barriers were found for the CO2 binding. Similarly, deprotonation of MEAH was identified as a bottleneck for the reverse reaction mode. The predicted overall free-energy difference is in a good agreement with the experimental values.
