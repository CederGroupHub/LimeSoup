Abstract

Three dimensional (3D) printing is a revolutionizing technology, which endows engineers, designers, and manufacturers with the ability to rapidly translate digital sketches into physical objects. The advantages that lie in the high resolution and accuracy of this technique were not concealed from the eyes of tissue engineers that soon harnessed this power for fabrication of complex biological structures. Nevertheless, while the conventional 3D printing scheme is oriented to yield durable and sturdy structures, the delicate nature of the substances used in 3D bioprinting results in fragile and mechanically unstable constructs. This poses a significant restriction that needs to be overcome in order to successfully complete the printing of intact, accurate, and biologically relevant constructs with desirable properties. To address these complications, advanced means of stabilization which are applied during and/or following the printing procedure are constantly being developed. In this review, the rational and principles behind widely used stabilization strategies in extrusion-based bioprinting will be covered. Examples of implementation of these strategies in recently published research in the field of tissue engineering will also be presented and discussed.

I. INTRODUCTION

Three dimensional (3D) printing is an additive manufacturing process, in which digital data are used to guide a computer-controlled instrument that successively joins building materials to construct spatial objects. This is in contrast to the more conventional method of subtractive manufacturing, in which material is reduced by milling from a solid block. From its infancy in the early 1980s, a variety of strategies and methods for 3D printing, such as stereolithography (SLA), digital light processing (DLP), fused deposition modeling (FDM), inkjet 3D printing, and selective laser sintering (SLS), have been developed. All of which are based on the consecutive addition of layers of material, which fuse to create continuous 3D structures. The third bioprinting technique is the commonly used extrusion-based method. In this technique, a 3D printer, equipped with screw or pneumatic-driven pistons, is used to force out continuous cylindrical strands of bioink from dispensing tips attached to cartridge reservoirs. The deposited layers of filaments then fuse to create the 3D structure. This contact-based printing technique uses cellular or acellular bioinks with a wide range of viscosities (30 mPa/s to > 6 × 107 mPa/s) and is generally considered to be inferior to the laser and inkjet bioprinting approaches in terms of resolution. Cell viability may also decrease as a result of the shear stress to which the cells are exposed during their passage through the nozzle. In fact, printing resolution and cell viability are related: the higher the nozzle gauge, the higher the printing resolution and also the pressure required for extrusion and the damaging shear stress that is imposed on the cells. Printout stabilization strategies can be roughly divided into two main categories. The first is the “bioink crosslinking” method, in which the printed material itself is reinforced at the molecular level upon deposition [Fig. 1(A)]. The second is the “bioink support” method which is based on incorporation of elements that mechanically support the extruded bioink and the resulted printed structure [Fig. 1(B)]. The basics of these strategies and examples of their implementation as standalone or in combination in recently published works are described in Secs. II and III. A summary of the relevant data from the reviewed researches can be found in Table I that follows the text.
FIG. 1. Schematic representation of stabilization methods. (A) Bioink crosslinking—The bioink is reinforced during or after the fabrication process using ionic, enzymatic, light irradiation, or thermal energy-induced crosslinking. (B) Bioink support—The incorporation of elements that mechanically support the extruded bioink and the resulted printed structure. This method can be divided into two main strategies: (I) Printed support—The support ink is co-printed with the bioink, stabilizing it externally or internally. Later, the support can either be left as an integrated part of the printed structure or be sacrificed to leave voids within. (II) Pre-casted support—The bioink is extruded into a bath pre-filled with support medium that entraps and holds the position of the deposited strands, enabling free-form printing in all dimensions. Later on, after reinforcement of the structure, this support can be removed.

II. BIOINK CROSSLINKING

In addition to the pre-requirement for free flow through narrow dispensing tips, bioinks used in extrusion-based printing should also support viability, motility, and healthy metabolism of the incorporated cells. Because of the weak and delicate nature of the substances that fulfill these criteria, printing of stable, thick, multi-layer constructs at high resolution generally requires further processing. In order to preserve the shape of the extruded bioink, prevent collapsing, and generate robust structures which can tolerate the conditions of in vitro culturing and/or implantation, the molecular structure of the printed material should be reinforced. This can be done by introducing crosslinks between the polymer molecules, resulting in a self-supported and considerably stiffer substance. Obviously, this modification should be gentle enough so as not to adversely affect the cytocompatibility of the bioink. The timing of the crosslinking depends on the nature of the crosslinker, the mechanical properties of the bioink, and its ability to maintain its shape after leaving the nozzle. Weak hydrogels tend to spread on the substrate and deform when pressure is applied from the top layers. Consequently, these hydrogels generally require that the crosslinking will be performed concurrently with the extrusion or before a new layer of bioink is deposited. This is also applied when using crosslinking agents that lack the ability to infiltrate deeply into multiple-layered structures. In these cases, the crosslinker could be introduced, for example, by simultaneous extrusion with the bioink through a coaxial nozzle; by spraying, deposition, or projection on every newly printed layer or by printing directly into a crosslinking-inducing environment.
In contrast, stiffer materials with superior mechanical endurance that can be printed with higher shape fidelity may crosslink at wider time intervals, given that the crosslinker can efficiently penetrate through the printed material. This can be done, for instance, by soaking the printout in a solution bath containing the crosslinking agent or by exposing the fabricated structure to physical crosslinking-inducing conditions.
In either case, a stabilizing crosslinking is a necessary step that is applied to most bioinks during the printing process or after its completion. The most commonly used procedures to reinforce printed bioinks are based on ionic, enzymatic, light irradiation, and thermal energy-induced crosslinking. These are described in Subsections II A–II D.
A. Ionic crosslinking
Ionic crosslinking is implemented via formation of electrostatic bonds between ions and polymer chains with opposite charge, forming a branched network [Fig. 2(A-I)]. The capacity of the ions to instantly crosslink the polymer solution and penetrate by diffusion deeply into the emerging hydrogel enable the generation of highly stabilized, thick structures that can be used to encapsulate pre-loaded cells and biofactors.

C. Light irradiation-induced crosslinking

III. BIOINK SUPPORT

The effectiveness of the bioink crosslinking strategy, when used as the only means of stabilization, depends on two key factors: first, for mechanically weak bioinks, a fast crosslinking reaction must be executed concurrently or shortly after deposition, in order to avoid deformation, spreading and collapsing of the printed material. Obviously, the extruded material must maintain its shape from the moment of deposition to the time of stabilization. Second, the geometry of the printed structure should not include elements with overhang angles greater than 45–60°, as the extruded material need sufficient supporting space to be printed on and cannot be deposited over thin air. In practice, the bioink and the printing scheme cannot always be tailored to support in-process, rapid crosslinking. Moreover, biologically relevant structures are often characterized by complex spatial geometries that make fabrication impossible without providing adequate support to overhangs. To address these complications, printing strategies have been developed, in which the fabricated structure is supported by external elements that hold and preserve its shape until final crosslinking of the bioink is attained.9191. R. Suntornnond, J. An, and C. K. Chua, “ Roles of support materials in 3D bioprinting—Present and future,” Int. J. Bioprinting 3(1), 83–86 (2017). https://doi.org/10.18063/IJB.2017.01.006 The supporting elements, which are composed of natural or synthetic biocompatible substances with preferable mechanical properties, can be printed alongside the bioink as part of the fabrication procedure. Alternatively, they can be pre-casted to form a “support bath” into which the bioink is extruded. At the end of the printing process and following bioink stabilization, the support materials may be extracted (these are known as “fugitive” or “sacrificial” materials) or preserved as an integral part of the printout.
The principles of the “printed support” and the “pre-casted support” strategies, together with relevant examples from recently published researches, are reviewed in Subsections III A and III B.
A. Printed support
One of the widely used strategies for stabilization of printed structures is based on the incorporation of supportive elements into the digital design of the object. The stabilized model is then printed by a concurrent deposition of both the bioink and the support materials. The latter can be patterned around (“extra-structural”) and/or within (“intra-structural”) the construct and are generally composed of acellular, biocompatible substances that possess good printability and robust structural properties.

The ability to print soft materials freely in all dimensions is a perquisite for engineering tissues and organs with predefined and complicated geometry. IV. FINAL REMARKS

Three-dimensional printing technology revolutionized the concept of manufacturing, enabling rapid prototyping and small scale, cost-effective production of a wide range of products. The past decade has been marked by a continuous development of cutting-edge printing machines, advanced fabrication protocols and assortment of production materials with diverse chemical and physical properties. As a result, the variety of 3D printing applications has been markedly extended, also to include fabrication of complex structures made of soft materials. These include tissue patches and artificial organs, principally constructed by extrusion-based 3D bioprinting techniques. However, the mild fabrication conditions and the delicate materials used in extrusion-based bioprinting pose substantial constraints to the process. As a result, a huge gap exists between the outcomes of extrusion-based bioprinting and conventional 3D printing, mainly in terms of resolution and robustness of the printouts. Fortunately, the extensive efforts that have been invested toward addressing this challenge bore fruits. The studies covered in this review demonstrate the feasibility to accurately print stable, tissue-like structures with complex architecture. Fabrication of these constructs can be accomplished by using customized extrusion-based bioprinters, innovative stabilization methods, and designated bioinks. While further substantial research and development are still required in order to obtain optimal results, exciting new frontiers has been opened in the art of tissue engineering due to these recent progresses. We foresee that in the near future, extrusion-based bioprinting will rapidly evolve to become a prominent fabrication scheme in engineering of thick, vascularized tissue patches. The complex, native-like structure, and organization of these constructs would endow them with improved cell viability, host integration capacity, physiological functionality, and, as a result, overall superior therapeutic potential.
