Electronic Level Alignment in Inverted Organometal Perovskite Solar Cells

Abstract

The measured energy diagram for inverted cell architectures for perovskite photovoltaic devices is presented. Band offsets are determined in direct and inverse photoemission spectroscopy. The perovskite films assume a slightly p-type characteristic on top of NiO and show good energetic alignment to adjacent organic electron transport layers. The finding explains the function of inverted devices and gives guidelines for optimization.

The rapid rise of organometal halide absorber materials for photovoltaic applications has triggered considerable interest in understanding the basic physical, chemical, and (opto)electronic properties of these materials, as well as the impact of various processing techniques on these very properties.[1-3] A hallmark of the lead halide perovskites has been the ease with which films leading to high performing solar cells could be produced. In that regard, recent computational studies suggest that the formation energies of mid-gap trap states, in this case lead (Pb) and iodide (I) interstitials, are relatively high.[4, 5] Energetically more favorable intrinsic defects in methy­lammonium lead triiodide (MAPbI3) films are found to induce shallow states close to the valence band maximum (VBM) or conduction band minimum (CBM). A consequence of such a situation would be that perovskites could readily adopt p-type or n-type conductivity depending on growth conditions and hence defect composition.[4]

Direct measurements of interface band offsets on perovskite layers grown on TiO2 substrates have shown the Fermi level (EF) to be close to the CBM all the way to the free surface of the film.[6] However, an initial study indicates that perovskite film formation on different oxide surface may change the position of EF.[7] Thus, the proposed unusual defect energies presumably open a route to engineer p-type perovskite films for inverted solar cells.

The inverted cell architecture, where the perovskite layer is deposited on a contact for hole extraction and then covered by an electron transport layer (ETL), has recently attracted attention because of less pronounced hysteresis in the current–voltage response in corresponding devices.[8, 9] The realization of efficient inverted devices would lead to additional flexibility in the integration of perovskite subcells into tandem solar cells, allowing more choices for a second subcell and recombination layer. Initial efforts have been pursued with incremental success.[10-13] To this date, power conversion efficiencies (PCE) over 15% have been reported,[8] leading to expectations that the PCE of conventional perovskite solar cells (>20%) could be matched soon. To this point, rationalizations of the function and performance of inverted cells have been based mostly on device energy diagrams derived from energy values of the isolated components.[11-13] Yet, demonstration has been made that a judicious choice of charge extraction layers and design of interfaces involved could further enhance the high performance of perovskite solar cells.[14] We recently reported quantitative bulk and interface energetics of the functional materials stacked in conventional perovskite solar cells measured via ultraviolet and inverse photoemission spectroscopy (UPS, IPES) and presented a route to improve device characteristics with judicious choices of transport layer materials.[6, 15] We offer here the analogous quantitative information for the construction of inverted solar cells. Devices reported in the literature include nickel oxide bottom contacts and mesoporous nickel oxide scaffolds as the hole-collection layer.[8, 13] Nickel oxide is a natural choice, as it is one of the few stable transparent p-type oxides and has proven to perform well as hole-collection and electron-blocking interlayers in organic photovoltaic cells, in particular when derived from a sol–gel process.[16, 17] On the other side of the perovskite layer, phenyl-C61-butyric acid methyl ester (PCBM) or other fullerene derivatives have been used as organic ETL, a material choice that is based on the widespread use of fullerene-derivatives as acceptors in organic electronics, with reasonable charge transfer to transparent conductive oxide cathodes, such as n-type zinc oxide.[18] In the present study, we use UPS and IPES to determine the energetics of the NiOx/MAPbI3/C60 structure, where the bottom NiOx layer serves as hole-collector/electron-blocker and the C60 top layer stands in place of PCBM. We show that MAPbI3 films spin-coated on sol–gel nickel oxide surfaces (sNiOx) exhibit p-type character, i.e., EF in the bottom half of the perovskite energy gap, with good alignment of the perovskite and sNiOx VBMs, and a good electron-blocking configuration due to the large gap (low electron affinity) of the sNiOx. Furthermore, we show that the CBM of the MAPbI3 is in close energy alignment with the lowest unoccupied molecular orbital (LUMO) of the fullerene C60 layer evaporated on top, allowing a nearly loss-less electron transfer from the perovskite layer to the ETL.

The first investigation focuses on the energetics of a 400-nm thick layer of MAPbI3 on top of sNiOx via UPS and IPES. The spectra are shown in black in Figure 1. For reference, the spectra for MAPbI3 deposited on TiO2­ are shown in orange in the same figure. When MAPbI3 is deposited on sNiOx, the secondary electron cutoff (left panel) points to an increase in work function of 0.7 eV compared with MAPbI3 deposited on TiO2. At the same time, the UPS He II spectrum (center panel) shows that the valence band features of MAPbI3 on sNiOx rigidly shift by ≈0.6 eV to lower binding energy compared with MAPbI3 on TiO2. This shift is accompanied by a comparable shift of 0.7 eV in the VBM (see inset). On the other side of the gap, the conduction band measured via IPES (right panel) of MAPbI3 on sNiOx shifts by 0.7 eV away from EF compared with MAPbI3 on TiO2. The combined UPS and IPES measurements yield for both cases a perovskite bandgap of 1.7 eV, in accord with our previous measurements.[6] In the case of MAPbI3 on sNiOx, the Fermi level is in the lower half of the perovskite gap, i.e., slightly p-type, and approximately 0.7 eV lower than that of MAPbI3 on TiO2. The sNiOx itself is found to be highly p-doped, with EF at 0.45 eV above the VBM (see Figure S1, Supporting Information), and apparently imposes its doping characteristics onto the MAPbI3 film. Similarly, the heavily n-doped TiO2 film had been found to cause the MAPbI3 layer to be n-type.

The ability to swing the Fermi level position in the gap and change the majority carrier type with the underlying substrate has deep implications on the density of free charge carriers and defects in the perovskite layer. It suggests a low density of intrinsic carriers, and thus of unintentional dopants. It further suggests a low density of electronically active mid-gap bulk and interface defects, which would otherwise pin EF and screen charge exchanges to and from the high and low work function substrates. Under these conditions, the contact not only dictates the position of the Fermi level in the perovskite gap at the interface, but deep into the material as well. In the present case, the Fermi level position is largely preserved throughout the film, at least for a MAPbI3 thickness of ≈400 nm. Major variations in perovskite formation, i.e., structure and orientation, with substrate can be excluded here based on X-ray diffraction measurements, which show both films to possess the same perovskite crystal structure and the same unit cell orientation, giving rise to identical diffraction patterns (see Figure S3, Supporting Information). Furthermore, AFM measurements reveal that the surface morphology of the MAPbI3 films grown on TiO2 and sNiOx is very similar with low surface roughness on the order of 7–10 nm (see Figure S4, Supporting Information). This observation argues against fundamental differences in bulk defect formation between perovskites formed on sNiOx and on TiO2, and strengthens the interpretation that changes in the Fermi level position originate at the interface and depend on the substrate work function. Note that the perovskite layers investigated in this study were also used for device fabrication in order to confirm the quality of the material. Devices with the conventional layer stack (Glass/ITO/TiO2/MAPbI3/Spiro-MeOTAD/Au) were found to exhibit average power conversion efficiencies of 10%–11%. Although the quality of perovskite films is known to depend sensitively on the processing conditions, these device results added to the well resolved valence band features of the UPS spectra of MAPbI3 on both TiO2 and sNiOx (Figure 1) are a strong indication of the homogeneity and high quality of the films investigated here. This finding is further corroborated by the narrow shape of the emission in the photoluminescence spectra and long lifetime of the peak emission for both perovskite/oxide combinations (see Figure S5, Supporting Information).

In the second part of this study, we incrementally evaporated C60 on top of the glass/ITO/sNiOx/MAPbI3 sample stack, varying the fullerene film thickness from 5 to 80 Å. Note that a nominal coverage of 5 Å corresponds to roughly half a monolayer of C60. In terms of interface energy level offset, C60 can be regarded as a close analog to the solution processed fullerenes PCBM or 1′,1′′,4′,4′′-Tetrahydro-di[1,4]methanonaphthaleno[1,2:2′,3′,56,60:2′′,3′′][5,6]fullerene-C60(ICBA),[19] which are usually employed as ETLs in inverted perovskite solar cells.[8, 11-13] The UPS spectra of the MAPbI3/C60 layers are given in Figure 2. The onset of the C60 HOMO level (middle panel) is steady at 1.3–1.4 eV below EF for every layer thickness. On the other side of the bandgap, the onset of the LUMO level is measured via IPES (right panel) at 0.9 eV above EF. These combined measurements yield an edge-to-edge HOMO–LUMO gap of 2.2–2.3 eV, in good agreement with literature values.[20, 21] Pristine UPS and IPES spectra of the molecular orbitals of C60 without contribution from the underlying MAPbI3 layer are obtained for overlayer thicknesses of 40 Å and above, as the substrate signal is fully attenuated. The vacuum level position is determined from the secondary electron cut-off in the left panel of Figure 2. With layer thickness at and above 40 Å, the vacuum level shifts up to 5.0 eV above EF. At this point, the C60 electron affinity (EA) of 4.0–4.1 eV and ionization energy (IE) of 6.4 eV are reached.[20]

The UPS/IPES study of the sNiOx/MAPbI3 and MAPbI3/C60 systems leads to the following considerations on the interface energetics (summarized in the energy diagram of Figure 3). Using the positions of the MAPbI3 and sNiOx VBMs with respect to the common Fermi level, i.e., 0.7 and 0.5 eV below, respectively, and assuming no significant band bending in the MAPbI3 layer nor in the oxide layer due to the deposition of the perovskite layer (the sNiOx VBM cannot be measured directly through the 400-nm thick MAPbI3 layer), the MAPbI3 VBM is found to be ≈0.2 eV below the sNiOx VBM at the interface. This result suggests that no barrier exists for hole extraction from the perovskite absorber into the nickel oxide layer, and that the holes undergo only a small 0.2 eV potential energy loss upon transfer between the absorber and the oxide. Given the VBM offset and the large difference between the bandgaps of the two materials, the offset between the conduction bands amounts to 1.3 eV (Figure 3), making the sNiOx layer a good electron-blocker for the perovskite, and eliminating in principle the detrimental electron transfer and recombination at this interface. We stress again that these numbers are based on measurements taken at the free perovskite surface with the assumption of negligible band bending in the oxide as well as in the perovskite, the buried sNiOx/MAPbI3 interface being difficult to access directly. In addition, time resolved photo­luminescence measurements do not yield evidence for differences in the extraction of charge carriers from the perovskite layer to either the TiO2 or sNiOx (see Figure S5, Supporting Information).

The electronic structure of the MAPbI3/C60 interface is more easily determined, as the fullerene layer is deposited incrementally. That interface is defined by a ≈0.1 eV vacuum level offset between the two materials, indicative of a small interface dipole. The onset of the LUMO level of C60 is closely aligned with the MAPbI3 CBM. This alignment is conserved even for thicker overlayers, while the work function gradually increases until the equilibrium EA (4.1 eV) and IE (6.4 eV) of C60 are reached. The latter effect can be attributed to a small charge transfer at the C60/MAPbI3 interface and a subsequent redistribution of charges in the C60 film as the layer thickness is increased, leading to a change in surface potential. The measured configuration of unoccupied energy levels at the MAPbI3/C60 interface appears therefore to be ideal for electron transfer from the absorber into the transport layer, given the absence of a barrier and a loss in potential energy of only 0.1 eV. On the contrary, the situation for hole-blocking at the absorber/ETL interface is not as clear as for electrons at the sNiOx/MAPbI3 interface. The energy offset between the MAPbI3, VBM, and the C60 HOMO level is 0.7 eV, which in principle is sufficient for hole-blocking. However, the UPS spectra of the thin C60 layers (Figure 2, middle panel) point to a significant density of states tailing into the C60 gap, effectively reducing this barrier. Even though the hole mobility in C60 is relatively low,[21] holes could be extracted from the top perovskite layer onto adjacent C60 molecules and recombine with electrons at this interface. This effect can be expected to be even more severe when PCBM is used as ETL, since its bandgap is narrower than that of C60 by approximately 0.3 eV, placing the PCBM HOMO level closer to the MAPbI3 VBM and further reducing the hole-blocking barrier. Recombination events on the organic transport layer could therefore explain why generally lower values for Voc are reported in inverted perovskite solar cells as compared with conventional perovskite solar cell architectures. The slightly lower electron affinity of PCBM (3.8 eV) compared with that of C60 (4.0–4.1 eV) could also contribute in creating a small electron extraction barrier, another unfavorable condition.

Given the electronic level alignment described above, we propose two ways to improve the energetics for the common inverted perovskite solar cell structure. First, an increase in the sNiOx work function and a higher doping ratio in the surface layer could pull the MAPbI3 VBM further up with respect to the oxide VBM, thus minimizing the potential drop for holes traveling across the HTL/absorber interface. Enriching the nickel oxide surface with oxygen and increasing the relative concentration of nickel vacancies, for example, by an O2-plasma treatment, would increase its work function.[22] On the other side of the device, replacing C60 or PCBM with a larger bandgap and higher ionization energy ETL should enhance hole-blocking and decrease recombination at the perovskite/ETL interface.

In conclusion, we measured and described the energy level alignment across key carrier extraction interfaces in a prototypical inverted perovskite solar cell structure. The alignment between the sol–gel processed nickel oxide HTL and the MAPbI3 absorber VBMs provides barrier-free hole extraction, a high electron-blocking barrier and only a small loss in hole potential energy of 0.2 eV. The perovskite layer itself becomes slightly p-doped on top of the sNiOx film, as opposed to the usually n-type doping characteristic on top of TiO2, indicating a unique composition of free carriers and gap states in MAPbI3. On the other side of the cell, the MAPbI3 CBM is closely aligned to the LUMO level of the C60 ETL, such that neither a barrier for electron extraction nor a loss in accessible open-circuit voltage is expected. However, we propose to investigate the use of large bandgap ETL materials instead of fullerenes to decrease the probability for interface recombination at this contact.

Experimental Section

Sample Preparation: ITO-covered glass substrates (≈10 Ω cm−2, Thin Film Devices, Inc.) were cleaned by sonication for 30 min in acetone and isopropanol with subsequent drying in a stream of N2(g). The substrates were subsequently treated by UV-ozone for 15 min prior to the deposition of nickel oxide. sNiOx films were spin-coated at 4000 rpm for 60 s on the ITO substrates from a nickel formate precursor, annealed at 300 °C for 1 h and stored under nitrogen. Further processing parameters for sNiOx films can be found in ref. [22]. The TiO2 films were spin-coated at 3000 rpm for 30 s from a methanolic titanium isopropoxide solution. The TiO2 thin films were further annealed at 90 °C for 10 min. To process the MAPbI3 films, a precursor solution consisting of a methylammonium iodide (MAI):lead iodide (PbI­2) molar ratio of 1:1 and a total material content of 37 wt% in dimethyl sulfoxide was prepared. The MAI:PbI­2 solution was spin-coated at 2000 rpm for 30 s on top of an ITO/TiOx and ITO/sNiOx substrate, respectively. Following spin-coating, the substrates were annealed at 100 °C for 45 min. Both precursor and film preparations were performed inside a N2-filled glovebox to avoid sample degradation due to changes in humidity conditions. In the glovebox, the samples were introduced in a sealable ultrahigh vacuum (UHV)-compatible container, which could be mounted on our analysis system and subsequently pumped, allowing transfer to UHV without ambient exposure. Samples were therefore never exposed to oxygen and water levels above 5 ppm.

C60 layers were evaporated from a resistively heated source containing once-sublimed material in a UHV chamber connected to the UPS/IPES system, at a rate of 0.2 Å s–1 and at a pressure below 10–8 Torr during evaporation. The films were then transferred back to the analysis chamber for UPS, IPES, and X-ray photoemission spectroscopy (XPS) measurements.

Sample Measurements: UPS, XPS, and IPES spectra were acquired in a custom-built UHV chamber equipped with a cylindrical mirror electron analyzer and operated at a base pressure of 10–10 Torr. UPS was performed by using He I (21.22 eV) and He II (40.8 eV) excitation lines from a He plasma discharge lamp. Spectra were taken at a pass energy of 5 eV for a nominal experimental resolution smaller than 150 meV. IPES was performed in isochromat mode using a low energy electron gun and an electron multiplier behind an optical band pass filter as detector giving a total experimental resolution of 400 meV. Further details on this setup are described elsewhere.[23] XPS was performed on an additional sample series, using the Al Kα line (1487.6 eV) with a resolution of 0.8 eV. MAPbI3 films showed some instability under X-ray irradiation, losing their p-doping characteristics within few seconds of exposure (see Figure S2, Supporting Information).

Finally, the crystallinity of the perovskite films was investigated via X-ray diffraction (XRD). Figure S3 (Supporting Information) displays the XRD pattern for a 400-nm thick MAPbI3 film deposited on top of TiO2 and NiOx, respectively. As observed in Figure S3 (Supporting Information), two reflections at 14.1° and 28.3° were identified. These diffraction peaks correspond to reflections associated with the (110) and (220) crystallographic planes of the room temperature I4cm tetragonal system. [24] No further reflections associated with the crystal structure of MAI, PbI2, or any other admixture of MAPbI3 phases were observed, hence indicating the formation of a phase-pure MAPbI3 film.
