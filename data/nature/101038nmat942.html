Two- versus three-dimensional quantum confinement in indium phosphide wires and dots

Heng Yu1, Jingbo Li2, Richard A. Loomis1, Lin-Wang Wang2 & William E. Buhro1

Abstract

The size dependence of the bandgap is the most identifiable aspect of quantum confinement in semiconductors; the bandgap increases as the nanostructure size decreases1, 2, 3. The bandgaps in one-dimensional (1D)-confined wells, 2D-confined wires, and 3D-confined dots should evolve differently with size as a result of the differing dimensionality of confinement1. However, no systematic experimental comparisons of analogous 1D, 2D or 3D confinement systems have been made. Here we report growth of indium phosphide (InP) quantum wires having diameters in the strong-confinement regime, and a comparison of their bandgaps with those previously reported for InP quantum dots4, 5, 6, 7. We provide theoretical evidence to establish that the quantum confinement observed in the InP wires is weakened to the expected extent, relative to that in InP dots, by the loss of one confinement dimension. Quantum wires sometimes behave as strings of quantum dots8, and we propose an analysis to generally distinguish quantum-wire from quantum-dot behaviour.

Introduction

The theoretical relationships in Fig. 1 derive from simple particle-in-a-box models for the kinetic energy of electron and hole confinement in planar wells9, 10, cylindrical wires11, 12 and spherical dots13. At this level of approximation, Eg (the increase in the effective bandgap above the bulk value) depends linearly on 1/d2 (the inverse square of diameter or thickness) for each case1, 2, with the well, wire and dot slopes varying in the ratios Awell/Awire/Adot = 1.00/1.17/2.00, respectively. Thus, the slope of a quantum-wire line should be 0.585 times that of a corresponding quantum-dot line. This particle-in-a-box approach provides only a first approximation of quantum confinement in semiconductors, but its general predictions as given in Fig. 1 are consistent with the results of higher-level theoretical studies12, 14, 15, 16.

Figure 1: Predictions of simple particle-in-a-box models for the size dependences of the kinetic confinement energies of electrons and holes in corresponding quantum wells, wires and dots.

The size parameter d refers to the thickness of a well, or the diameter of a wire or dot. The slopes of the lines are (h2/8)[1/me + 1/mh] for the wells9, 10, (1.17h2/8)[1/me + 1/mh] for the wires11, 12, and (2h2/8)[1/me + 1/mh] for the dots13, where h is Planck's constant, and me and mh are the effective masses of an electron and hole, respectively.

As noted above, to our knowledge, experimental comparisons of corresponding sets of quantum wells, wires or dots to test the Fig. 1 predictions have not been made. Excellent studies of the diameter and length dependences of the bandgaps in quantum rods have begun to probe the Fig. 1 comparisons, but these systems remain anisotropically 3D confined17, 18. True quantum-wire specimens having diameters within the strong-confinement regime of about 2–12 nm, spanning the available size range for high-quality quantum dots4, 5, 6, 7, are not generally available11, 19, 20. We previously reported solution-phase synthesis of InP (Ref. 21) and other III-V nanowires by the solution–liquid–solid (SLS) mechanism22, in which nanowire growth is catalysed by metallic nanoparticles composed of low-melting metals or alloys23 such as In or AlxGa1–x. However, the nanowire diameters were too large and the diameter distributions too broad to enable study of quantum-confinement effects, because we lacked control over catalyst–nanoparticle sizes, which determine the nanowire diameters20, 24, 25. We have now prepared monodisperse metallic nanoparticles with controlled diameters by heterogeneous seeded growth26. This method and its variations afforded a set of monodisperse In-nanoparticle catalysts that we have used to grow InP quantum wires having diameters in the range of 3–11 nm and narrow diameter distributions, as detailed below (see also Supplementary Information, Section 2). Nanowires of GaAs were grown similarly27.

The SLS growth of InP quantum wires21, 22 was conducted in solution (at 203 °C) by thermal decomposition of [Me2InP(SiMe3)2]2 (Ref. 28) in the presence of In-nanoparticle catalysts. The solutions contained the polymer poly(1-hexadecene0.67-co-vinylpyrrolidinone0.33) to stabilize both the catalyst nanoparticles and the product nanowires. The resulting dark-red quantum-wire solutions (dispersions) were stable for several months when protected from the atmosphere (Supplementary Information, Section 3).

Analysis by transmission electron microscopy (TEM; JEOL 2000 FX operating at 200 kV) revealed that the quantum wires were several micrometres in length, and possessed diameters that depended on the size of the catalyst nanoparticles used in their growth (Fig. 2). Electron diffraction established that the wires were crystalline with the [111] direction of the zinc-blende structure oriented parallel to their long axes, as previously established22. Mean diameters and diameter distributions were determined by statistical analysis of TEM images (Table 1 and Supplementary Information, Section 4). Standard deviations in the diameters ranged from 13–21% of the mean diameter, demonstrating that the quantum-wire specimens approached monodispersity in that dimension. The relationship between the diameters of the quantum wires (dwire) and the initial diameter of the catalyst nanoparticles (dcat) was empirically fit by the linear expression in Equation 1.

The quantum-wire bandgaps were estimated from the lowest-energy exciton features in the absorption spectra (Fig. 3a; obtained using a Varian Cary 1E ultraviolet–visible spectrophotometer) of each size distribution, collected from toluene dispersions of quantum wires. Each spectrum (except that of dwire = 11.01 nm) showed one or more excitonic peaks, which were broader than the corresponding peaks in the InP quantum-dot absorption spectra reported previously4, 5, 6, 7. The peaks in the quantum-dot spectra are sharper and better resolved probably because the diameter distributions of the dot specimens are narrower (standard deviations about 5–7%)4, 5 than are those of the wire specimens reported here. To determine the wavelength maxima in the quantum-wire exciton features, the absorption spectra in Fig. 3a were empirically fit and the broad backgrounds subtracted using commercial nonlinear least-squares-fitting software (see Supplementary Information). The lowest-energy features in the resulting background-adjusted spectra (Fig. 3b) were fit with partial gaussian functions, yielding their wavelength maxima and estimated errors, from which the quantum-wire bandgaps and Eg values were calculated (Table 1, and Supplementary Information, Section 5).

Plots of Eg versus 1/d2 (where d = diameter) for InP quantum dots and wires are compared in Fig. 4. The quantum-dot data are taken from Refs 4–6, and the quantum-wire data from Table 1. Linear fits to these data yielded slopes of Adot = 6.1 0.1 eV nm2 and Awire = 3.8 0.2 eV nm2, and the slope ratio Awire/Adot = 0.62 0.03. As noted above, rudimentary particle-in-a-box models predict the slope of the quantum-wire line in Fig. 1 to be 0.585 times that of the quantum-dot line; this prediction is within the experimental error of our observed value of 0.62. Therefore, the size dependence of the bandgap in InP quantum wires scales as expected in comparison to that observed in InP quantum dots, consistent with 2D versus 3D confinement.

The lines are least-squares fits to the experimental data, yielding the slopes Adot = 6.1 0.1 eV nm2 and Awire = 3.8 0.2 eV nm2, and the ratio of the slopes Awire/Adot = 0.62 0.03. Theoretical data obtained by semiempirical pseudopotential calculations for InP quantum dots (black circles) and quantum wires (purple circles) are also shown.

The overly simple particle-in-a-box models used above, based on an effective-mass approximation, do not correctly predict the absolute (as opposed to the relative) slope values and non-zero intercepts we observed (Fig. 4). Therefore, we conducted plane-wave semiempirical pseudopotential calculations29, 30 for InP quantum dots and wires to test our conclusion that the bandgaps scale properly for 3D versus 2D confinement (Supplementary Information, Section 6). The results of these high-level calculations are also plotted in Fig. 4. Excellent agreement between the experimental and theoretical data for InP quantum wires further indicates that quantum confinement is weakened to the expected extent in the wires.

The agreement between the experimental and theoretical data for InP quantum dots (Fig. 4) is not as close, and the theoretical Eg values are obviously not proportional to 1/d2. We find that these theoretical Eg values are instead proportional to 1/d1.35 by empirical fitting, consistent with previous semiempirical pseudopotential results30. Correspondingly, we find that the theoretical Eg values for InP quantum wires are proportional to 1/d1.45. Figure 5 shows the fits of our semiempirical pseudopotential results for dots and wires to equations (2) and (3) (below), respectively, giving the slope values of Bdot = 3.30 0.02 eV nm1.35 and Cwire = 2.49 0.02 eV nm1.45. Thus, the theoretical slope ratio predicted by the semiempirical pseudopotential calculations is Cwire/Bdot = 0.75 0.01 nm0.10.

Figure 5: Theoretical and experimental InP quantum-dot and quantum-wire data plotted as Eg versus 1/dn, for n = 1.35 (dot) and 1.45 (wire).

The dotted lines are least-squares fits to the theoretical data (equations 2 and 3), yielding the slopes Bdot = 3.30 0.02 eV nm1.35 and Cwire = 2.49 0.02 eV nm1.45, and the theoretical slope ratio Cwire/Bdot = 0.75 0.01 nm0.10. The experimental quantum-dot data are from Refs 4–6. The solid lines are least-squares fits to the experimental data, yielding the slopes Bdot = 3.6 0.1 eV nm1.35 and Cwire = 2.4 0.1 eV nm1.45, and the experimental slope ratio Cwire/Bdot = 0.66 0.02 nm0.10.

To provide an experimental comparison to this theoretical prediction, we have fitted the experimental results from Fig. 4 to equations (2) and (3), as shown in Fig. 5, obtaining the experimental slope values of Bdot = 3.6 0.1 eV nm1.35 and Cwire = 2.4 0.1 eV nm1.45. These values give an experimental slope ratio of Cwire/Bdot = 0.66 0.02 nm0.10, which compares favourably to the theoretical value of 0.75 nm0.10 predicted above. Furthermore, the small difference between the theoretical and experimental slope ratios is quite evidently due to the slight disagreement between the theoretical and experimental Eg values for the dots (Fig. 5). Therefore, we conclude on the basis of this higher-level theoretical comparison that quantum confinement in the wires is weakened to the expected extent by the loss of one confinement dimension.

Because such high-level theoretical studies on quantum wires of various composition are not yet widely available, we propose that the prediction of Fig. 1, Awire/Adot = 0.585, may provide a generally useful although very approximate 'rule of thumb' for assessing 2D confinement in quantum wires. For example, small-diameter nanowires having significant diameter fluctuations or closely spaced internal grain boundaries should behave as strings of 3D-confined dots rather than as true 2D-confined wires, which should be revealed by Awire/Adot values tending towards 1 (Ref. 8). This expectation and the general applicability of our proposed rule of thumb require further testing. To that end, we have compared the bandgaps in CdSe quantum wires and quantum dots, and have determined an experimental slope ratio of Awire/Adot = 0.53 0.05; this work will be described elsewhere. In summary, the size dependence of the bandgap in InP quantum wires is weaker than that in quantum dots, as expected, and scales according to a simple quantitative prediction; the slope of an experimental Eg versus 1/d2 line for a set of quantum wires should be approximately 0.6 times that of a corresponding set of quantum dots.
