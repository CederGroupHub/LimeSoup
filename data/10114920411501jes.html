    Microstructure and Ionic Conductivity in Li7La3Zr2O12 Film Prepared by Aerosol Deposition Method

    1. Cheol-Woo Ahnz,
    2. Jong-Jin Choi,
    3. Jungho Ryu,

    6. Woon-Ha Yoon,
    7. Joon-Hwan Choi and
    8. Dong-Soo Park
    1. Functional Ceramics Department, Powder & Ceramics Division, Korea Institute of Materials Science (KIMS), Changwon, Gyeongnam 641–831, South Korea

    Abstract

    In all-solid-state Li ion batteries, solid electrolyte layers must be fabricated without an annealing process because of the reaction between electrolytes and active materials. Hence, a lot of researchers have an interest in the aerosol deposition (AD) films which can be formed at room temperature and show dense microstructures without an annealing process. In this study, the microstructure and ionic conductivity of an Al2O3-added Li7La3Zr2O12 (LLZ) AD film were investigated. The LLZ AD film showed the dense microstructure which had nano-size grains. Because of the small grain size, low electric conduction was observed at a LLZ AD film. In addition, the moisture in air might be also responsible for the low electric conduction. In order to demonstrate the Li ion conduction in a LLZ AD film, a solid state cell was fabricated using a C-coated LiFePO4 (LFP) AD film as a working electrode [structure: LFP AD film/LLZ AD film (solid electrolyte)/Li foil]. The electrochemical properties of the solid state cell were compared with them of a liquid electrolyte cell [structure: LFP AD film/Liquid electrolyte/Li foil].

    All-solid-state Li ion batteries have been widely studied because they can show high safety and energy density, compared with liquid-electrolyte-based Li ion batteries.1–18 Key materials, in all-solid-state Li ion batteries, are solid electrodes and electrolytes. Recently, we have reported the electrochemical properties of a C-coated LiFePO4 (LFP) film, which was fabricated by an aerosol deposition (AD) method, as a candidate for a cathode in an all-solid-state Li ion battery.1 However, in that study, the cell was assembled using not a solid electrolyte but a liquid electrolyte to confirm the electrochemical behavior of a LFP AD film as a cathode in Li ion batteries. Hence, in this study, the electrochemical properties of a solid state cell were investigated. The solid state cell was fabricated using a solid electrolyte film.

    Among the various oxide-based solid electrolytes such as (Li,La)TiO3 [LLT], Li7La3Zr2O12, Li1+xAlxTi2−x(PO4)3 [LATP] etc., 1 wt% Al2O3–added Li7La3Zr2O12 (LLZ) has been considered as a promising candidate in all-solid-state Li ion batteries. It is because of high ionic conductivity, high chemical stability, and wide potential window.3,6,8–18 In particular, the Li metal can be used for a counter electrode in LLZ-based solid state cells.6 Thus, LLZ was chosen for a solid electrolyte in this study.

    Recently, a sol-gel spin coating method has been tried to develop a LLZ film for a electrolyte layer in a solid state battery.19 However, the film was annealed at 600–800°C, although it has been well-known that LLZ reacts with active materials at the temperature range.3,19 In addition, the cell fabrication was not achieved in that study. Therefore, in this study, a LLZ AD film was considered as an electrolyte layer in a solid state battery. It is because an AD method is a room temperature process for the formation of thin films and an annealing process is not required to obtain the dense microstructure.1,20 Hence, a lot of researchers have an interest in AD films for all-solid-state batteries. Furthermore, in this study, a solid state cell was fabricated by the AD method in order to confirm the Li ion conduction of a LLZ AD film.

    AD is the deposition method of thin/thick films using fine particles as shown in Fig. 1. The fine particles are accelerated by air compressors and ejected through a nozzle. The particles collide onto a substrate with high speed. A dense film is deposited at room temperature because of the fracture and plastic deformation of primary particles and the grain size of an AD film is quite small (approximately a few nanometers) as indicated in Fig. 1. In particular, since this AD process does not require an annealing process to obtain a dense film, it is expected that an AD method can be a good candidate technique to assemble an all-solid-state Li ion battery.

    The microstructure and ionic conductivity of a LLZ AD film were investigated in this study. In addition, the electrochemical properties of a solid state cell (LFP AD film/LLZ AD film/Li foil) were also investigated, in order to clarify the Li ion conduction of a LLZ AD film. Furthermore, the electrochemical properties of the solid state cell were compared with them of a liquid electrolyte cell (LFP AD film/Liquid electrolyte/Li foil).

    Experimental

    LLZ was prepared by conventional solid state synthesis. The powders of LiOH, La2O3, ZrO2, and Al2O3 (all obtained from Sigma Aldrich) were mixed for 24 h in a polypropylene jar with zirconia balls. 1 wt% Al2O3 was added because it was required to obtain the cubic garnet structure which had high ionic conductivity in LLZ.3,15 The mixed powders were dried and then calcined at 1100°C for 2 h. The calcined powders were planetary-milled for 30 min and then dried. Using this powder and LFP powder (Daejung EM, Korea), LLZ and LFP films were fabricated by an AD method.1,20 Their crystal structures were examined by a Rigaku D/max-RC X-ray diffractometer. The microstructures of specimens were observed using a scanning electron microscope (SEM, JSM-5800; JEOL CO., Tokyo, Japan) and a transmission electron microscope (TEM, JEM 2100F; JEOL CO., Tokyo, Japan). The conductivities of LLZ AD films were measured by an AC impedance method and obtained using impedance analyzers (4294A; Agilent Technologies, Santa Clara, CA and VMP3; Bio Logic SAS, France) at the range of 1 mHz – 110 MHz.

    In order to demonstrate the Li ion conduction of a LLZ AD film, a LFP AD film (thickness: 1 μm) was deposited at the bottom of a stainless steel plate for a working electrode and a LLZ AD film (thickness: 20 μm) was deposited at the bottom of the LFP AD film for a solid electrolyte layer. A Li metal foil was attached at the bottom of the LLZ AD film as a counter electrode. The specimens were dried for 24 h in a vacuum chamber in order to remove water. The cells were assembled in an argon-filled glove box, using coin-type cells (CR 2016). They were cycled using VMP3 and the current density was 5 μA cm−2.

    Results and Discussion

    Figure 2 shows the X-ray diffraction (XRD) patterns of LLZ and LFP powders and AD films. As seen in Fig. 2a, LLZ powders showed cubic garnet-like structure and any secondary phase was not found. Thus, it could be expected that LLZ powders, which had cubic garnet-like structures, were well-formed. In LLZ AD films, XRD peaks were broader than them of LLZ powders and it might be due to the small grain sizes (a few nanometers) of LLZ AD films as indicated in Figs. 2a-2b and 3. In addition, XRD peaks for LFP AD films were also broader than them of LFP powders and it could be also due to the small grain sizes of LFP AD films as seen in Figs. 2c-2d and 5.

    The microstructures of LLZ powders and films were exhibited in Fig. 3. As seen in this figure, LLZ powders had the grain size of a few micrometers and the grain size was changed to be a few nanometers in LLZ AD films. In addition, LLZ AD films showed dense microstructures as seen in the LLZ film surface of Fig. 3. The small grain size and dense microstructure of LLZ AD films are due to the fracture and plastic deformation of LLZ particles during an AD process as shown in Fig. 1. They are the typical microstructures of AD films.1,20 The thickness of LLZ AD films was approximately 20 μm as shown in the top figure of Fig. 3.

    Figure 4a shows the impedance plots of a LLZ AD film as a function of frequency and temperature. When the temperature of a LLZ AD film was lower than 130°C, the impedance was not clearly distinguished. On the contrary, the electric conductivities could be obtained at 140–200°C in LLZ AD films as seen in Fig. 4a-4b. However, the conductivity of a LLZ AD film was quite low approximately 1.0 × 10−8 S cm−1 at 140°C as indicated in Fig. 4b, although the LLZ AD film had a cubic garnet-like phase as seen in Figs. 2a-2b. Therefore, the low conductivity of LLZ AD films might be due to the small grain size of a few nanometers as shown in Fig. 3, since the grain sizes of specimens were larger than 10 μm in the sintered LLZ specimens which showed the high conductivity around 10−4 S cm−1.8 The detail description about the effect of small grain sizes on the conductivities of LLZ AD films is made below.

    The Arrhenius plot of a LLZ AD film is shown in Fig. 4c. The activation energy of a LLZ AD film was quite high (approximately 1.3 eV), compared with the reported values of LLZ bulk specimens (approximately 0.3 eV).9 This high activation energy was similar to 1.25 eV which was found at a sintered LLZ specimen when it was exposed to air.21 Thus, the effect of moisture must be considered in LLZ AD films, because an AD method is not a moisture-free process. The relative humidity of air was 54%. When LLZ is exposed to air, La(OH)3 is formed at the grain boundary and the decrease of grain boundary conductivity occurs.18,21 In addition, this decrease of conductivity can be more significant at a LLZ AD film which has nano-size grains and the wide area of grain boundary. However, XRD peaks for La(OH)3 are not observed at a LLZ AD film because of the thin grain boundary. Therefore, it could be suggested that the small grain sizes of a few nanometers and the effect of moisture (the formation of the secondary phase at grain boundary) might be responsible for the low conductivity of LLZ AD films. However, it was not identified, in this study, how it showed the high activation energy of 1.25-1.3 eV when LLZ was exposed to air.

    In order to recognize whether the conductivities of LLZ AD films were due to Li ion conduction or not, a solid state cell was prepared using a LFP-LLZ multi-layer film and a Li foil, as shown in the inset of Fig. 6a. For the solid state cell, a LFP-LLZ multi-layer was fabricated by the AD method, as indicated in Fig. 5. The grain sizes of LFP powders were located at the wide range (from submicron-scale to micron-scale), but the small grain sizes (a few nanometers) were observed in LFP AD films. Moreover, the dense microstructures were also formed at LFP AD films as seen in the LFP film surface of Fig. 5. The small grain sizes and dense microstructures of LFP AD films are the typical features of AD films.1,20 The thickness of LFP AD films was approximately 1 μm as shown in the top figure of Fig. 5.

    The electrochemical properties of a solid state cell (LFP AD film/LLZ AD film/Li foil) were investigated at 140°C as seen in Fig. 6. As shown in Fig. 6a, the capacity of a solid state cell was relatively high at the 1st cycle (approximately 19 μAh cm−2, 53 mAh g−1, 0.3C), but it was suddenly decreased to be 5 μAh cm−2 at the 2nd cycle. From the 5th cycle, it was not decreased any more. We have reported that a liquid electrolyte cell (the thickness of LFP AD film: 745 nm) showed 49 mAh g−1 at 0.8C and exhibited the excellent cycle performance.1 Hence, it might be expected that the solid state cell did not show the significant difference at the capacity of the 1st cycle, compared with a liquid electrolyte cell. However, their discharge voltages were quite different (2.5 V at the solid state cell and 3.4 V at the liquid electrolyte cell), although they showed similar charge voltages.1 These trends might be explained by some factors such as the low Li ion conduction of a LLZ AD film, the cell structures etc. Fig. 6b shows the impedance plots of a solid state cell (at 140°C, before and after the cell test) and a liquid electrolyte cell (at 20°C). As seen in this figure, the solid state cell had the higher resistance than a liquid electrolyte cell because of the low conductivity in a LLZ AD film. In addition, the resistance was significantly increased after the cell test. This variation of the cell resistance might be responsible for the charge/discharge profiles of a solid state cell. It was not identified in this study how the cell resistance was increased during the cell test. Nevertheless, it could be suggested that the measured conductivity of a LLZ AD film might be due to Li ion conduction, because the solid state cell showed the charge/discharge behavior of Li ion as seen in Fig. 6.

    Conclusions

    The microstructures and ionic conductivities of LLZ AD films were investigated in this study. Cubic garnet-like phases were observed at the XRD patterns of LLZ AD films, which showed the dense microstructures and the small grain sizes of a few nanometers. The conductivities of LLZ AD films were quit low (approximately 1.0 × 10−8 S cm−1 at 140°C). It was demonstrated that the conductivity was due to Li ion conduction, by measuring the electrochemical properties of a solid state cell. The solid state cell was assembled by a LFP AD film, a LLZ AD film, and a Li metal foil (as a working electrode, an electrolyte, and a counter electrode). Therefore, it was found that a solid state battery could be fabricated by an AD method without an annealing process. However, the microstructure of a LLZ AD film must be improved. The low conductivities of LLZ AD films can be due to the small grain sizes of a few nanometers. Moreover, during the fabrication process of LLZ AD films, the removal of moisture might be also required to obtain the high Li ion conductivity.
